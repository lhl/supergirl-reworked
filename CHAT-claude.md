Here's the concrete version of what you're asking: why does the 2026 DC Studios *Supergirl* — directed by Craig Gillespie, written by Ana Nogueira, starring Milly Alcock, produced (not written) by James Gunn, and adapted from Tom King and Bilquis Evely's miniseries *Supergirl: Woman of Tomorrow* — feel dour and boring when its source is warm and emotional? The core failure is that the movie kept the comic's *plot* but inverted its *thesis*, then had to rig everything else to prop up the inversion. The comic's entire engine is that revenge is hollow and that Kara's refusal to kill is what saves Ruthye's soul — the point is that she doesn't. The film spends its runtime making that same argument (killing is a line you can't return from, vengeance won't heal Ruthye) and then has its hero cross the line anyway. The "justification" — Krem is irredeemable, sparing him earlier got more people killed, killing him spares Ruthye the burden — is special pleading the script manufactures so the execution reads as protective sacrifice. That's the sourness you're feeling: the movie is arguing against its own stated theme and stacking the deck so hard that "revenge is hollow" quietly becomes "some people just need killing."

To make that kill land, they had to remake the villain. Comic Krem is a sniveling, cowardly opportunist — a Tom Chaney/True Grit analogue; the film turns him into a superhuman marauder and sex trafficker who murders children with equal disregard. A cartoon monster removes all moral tension from the "should she?" question the movie keeps posing — the answer is obvious, so the climax it's been building toward is deflated before it arrives. The comic's pathetic Krem is precisely what makes sparing him *mean* something. Swap in pure evil and you've gutted the dilemma while keeping its runtime.

This is also exactly why it reads Snyder, not Gunn. Snyder's DC was defined by heroes who kill and films that treat the kill as mature, consequence-laden gravitas (Superman snapping Zod's neck). Gunn's DCU — and his 2025 *Superman* specifically — was built to repudiate that, with heroism as kindness and restraint. A *Supergirl* that climaxes on a justified execution, recasts the villain as maximally monstrous to earn it, and laces in a grimdark sexual-violence texture is tonally a regression to the mode the new universe was supposed to bury. Critics caught it: reviewers note the kill permanently separates her worldview from Superman's and that the film can't find a tone — visually silly and playing Kara's drunkenness for laughs while also wanting you to feel the trauma — so it just lands dour.

The boredom in the middle is a separate but related problem. The comic's emotional engine is interiority and mood — Evely's cosmic loneliness, the elegiac quiet, the slow build of the Kara/Ruthye bond. The film converts that into a string of interchangeable set-pieces (space-bus attack, pub brawl, ship infiltration) that reviewers describe as one superfluous sequence after another and jerky and disjointed. The action never escalates in kind — Kara is fast, everyone else is slow — so it becomes white noise, and the relationship that should carry the emotion gets built at surface level between fights instead of in the stillness the comic lived in. The personal quest also keeps getting interrupted by franchise admin (the Lobo cameo, *Man of Tomorrow* setup), which dilutes the intimacy further.

Then the "whatever" feeling at the end is about cost. The comic's ending *pays* for itself — centuries, genuine ambiguity about whether redemption is real, Ruthye's own choice. The film's resolution is frictionless: dog lives, villain dies, Ruthye is "saved" but the moral stain just gets transferred to Kara off-screen, and she goes home — with no mid- or post-credits scene, which reads almost like the film knew you wouldn't be jazzed. The execution is treated as a clean fix, and a clean fix is the *least* Gunn-like move available.

Here's what a version that respects all that might look like.

**Act two** runs the power as a gradient tied to her emotional re-engagement, not as a yo-yo for fights. **Act three** is where you finally let the power return — fully, under the yellow sun — and the trick is that its return reframes the entire climax. Full power is the temptation, because power is the capacity to do harm. She has Krem dead to rights with every justification stacked in her favor, Ruthye begging for it, and the movie's whole design has been withholding her strength precisely so that it lands at the moment of moral test. She spares him — and the crucial fix is that sparing has to *cost* something concrete, not the frictionless dust-your-hands version. Maybe restraint costs her the easy path to the antidote, or means Krem might return, or means Ruthye turns on her for a while. Mercy that costs nothing isn't a choice; it's a formality.

And the payoff that the film completely whiffs: choosing *not* to kill is what finally lets her grieve. The whole movie she's confused destruction with control and numbness with peace. Ruthye is saved not by Kara taking the kill onto herself — which is just transferring the wound — but by Kara demonstrating you can survive the thing that broke you without becoming it.
