# SUPERGIRL — Story Bible
### *A "Woman of Tomorrow" adaptation that belongs in the DCU, not the DCEU*

---

## North Star

A coming-of-age story about **grief**, in which Kara (Supergirl) must win an argument about **what it means to be good**. She must win this argument *for herself* as much as for the child she's trying to save.

The wonder and hope of *All-Star Superman*; the seasonal, small-scale melancholy of *For All Seasons*; the cosmic loneliness and lyricism of *Woman of Tomorrow*. Set in a genuinely violent universe the story refuses to flinch from or launder.

The thing being adapted is **not the plot**. It's the **thesis**: *in a world that can be cruel and unfair, hope, love, and caring (goodness, basically) is a choice you must actively make.*

---

## Why Supergirl (2026) Misses

The movie is based on Tom King/Bilquis Evely's 2021 graphic novel, Supergirl: Woman of Tomorrow. It kept much of the comic's plot, characters, and narrative framing, but it twists the thesis and fails to land in many areas.

Kara spends the film (halfheartedly) arguing that revenge is hollow and that killing is a line you can't return from, and then coldly executes a disarmed Krem after talking Ruthye out of it.

We see she doesn't believe what she's saying (or that she views herself as so unfixably broken that it doesn't matter if she takes on more of the soul-killing as a burden, but in the next scene she seems fine afterwards - it's ok, we the audience don't believe it either).

To make this land, the film rigs the board: it's not enough for Krem to be a murderer — he has to be a sociopathic monster — the leader of an unstoppable multi-planetary genocidal society of rapists and child-slavers. Someone so heinous that the kill feels deserved, the only fitting solution and punishment for some otherwise unstoppable ongoing atrocity. The deck is stacked so that execution is a protective mercy, the only solution, the only question is who is going to deliver it.

The comic series, with more breathing room, actually builds some of that same structure (Krem is hapless, but, joining the Brigands, is shown to be involved in more and more heinous, perhaps unforgivable sins), but its answer could not be more different from *Supergirl* (2026). ("It's too big. We're too small.")

The comic had emotional resonance, gravitas. It is a poignant argument for the importance of goodness in the face of the overwhelming universe. It didn't just pay lip service or moralize on this while hypocritically (or worse, lazily) betraying/contradicting its own stated themes.

Thematically, this doesn't sit right, but structurally, the movie suffers as well:

- **Power is a dial, not a rule** — strong enough for spectacle, conveniently weak when innocents die.
- **The power math eats the drama** — Kara snaps between too weak and too strong, while Krem and the Brigands are either irrelevant next to her or implausibly strong enough to matter. The audience stops caring, does any of the punching even matter?
- **The set pieces are detachable** — nothing escalates, so it feels like it should have ended three fights earlier. 
- **Ruthye's change of heart is asserted, not dramatized** — she never makes the choice the story should be about.
- **Krem is a symbol, not a person** — there's no moral tension in killing a cartoon.
- **The camera loves the death the script condemns** — the core DCEU incoherence.
- **What does Supergirl actually learn** — she has a change of heart about Earth at the end, but it's unclear why. A bit of murder as a reset?

Let's see if we can fix this.

## The Rules

1. **No power math** Vulnerability obeys a law the world consistently honors. Red sunlight is not an instant off-switch; it drains her over time. The longer she stays, the weaker she gets — and the drunker she gets, the less well she can use what's left. Depowering is character, not a tension valve. However, the story should not ask the audience to solve whether Kara is 18%, 47%, or 100% Supergirl in a given scene. The rule is experiential and legible: under red light she has residue, not command; under yellow light she has capacity, and therefore moral danger. Krem and the Brigands are not "strong enough to fight Supergirl"; they are dangerous through timing, numbers, leverage, ships, terrain, logistics, and systems.
2. **Callbacks, not repetition** If the story needs yo-yoing power levels (poison, green and yellow suns, convenient kryptonite), maybe it's just bad? Should we really have duplicate stowaway beats or recycled boarding tricks? The repetitions in the movie should be thematic, not mechanical. That's just lazy. And boring.
3. **Theme and Character arcs** What is the story actually about? I'd say recovering from trauma. What is the actual conflict to be resolved? Supergirl wants to prevent Ruthye from making a mistake she knows she'll regret, while needing to convince herself of the same thing: that there is life after tragedy. We need to see the characters grow.
4. **The conflict is not fisticuffs** Supergirl is weakened because she *chose* to hide on a red-sun world to stop feeling Argo. We know that when she gets her powers back, any non-cosmic entity is done for. If she's not fighting gods, who can punch the hardest is not the point.
5. **Every scene is load-bearing.** Each beat changes someone. Rising action toward one destination. No interchangeable brawls.
6. **The climax is a choice, not a fight.** Full power returns precisely at the moral test. The question is never "can she win" — it's *"now that she can do anything, what will she choose?"* Power is the temptation, because power is the capacity to do harm.
7. **Mercy must cost something concrete.** Restraint that costs nothing is a formality, not a choice. Make it expensive.
8. **The real line is combat vs. execution — not kill vs. no-kill.** In a violent world, *never kill* is a lie no one could live. Killing to save (the heat) is not killing for vengeance (the cold). Kara or others can fight, can even kill in the chaos — she cannot coldly finish the helpless. The cold kill of a beaten enemy as the answer to grief is the one forbidden act.
9. **It's not that kind of story** That being said, how much offhand violence you want is a choice. Yes, wanton violence exists in the world (universe), but is it core to a Supergirl story? Spend the minutes on emotional depth, not on gratuitous fight scenes. 
10. **Persuasion is not argument.** Nobody is reasoned out of grief. Change happens by mirroring — Ruthye is Kara at month one; Kara is Ruthye at year ten.

## Power Logic Map — Make the Rules Felt, Not Calculated

The movie should never become a math problem about whether Kara is "strong enough" this minute. The audience needs a small number of visible states, each tied to character and scene function.

| State | Where / when | What Kara can do | What she cannot do | Dramatic purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Red-sun saturation** | Bar-world, after choosing to stay too long and drink too much | Residual flashes: a burst of speed, one too-hard hit, durability enough to survive what would kill a human | Sustain flight, control heat vision, dominate a room, protect everyone at once | Her avoidance has a physical cost. She is still Kara, but not commandingly Supergirl. |
| **Red-sun recovery / away from the dead light** | Early road, moving out from the refuge but not yet restored | Endurance, trained fighting, brief saves that hurt her, moments of instinctive heroism | Clean spectacle, easy victory, solving the plot by force | Action becomes improvisation and consequence, not power display. |
| **Yellow-sun flicker → rebirth** | Lobo scene — first solar recharge, rising through the dead atmosphere | Kara heals from the slash. Power floods back. She is *reborn* — not just physically but spiritually. Full Supergirl. | — | The sun does what the sun does: life insists on itself. This is the only solar transition and the one true power spectacle — a promise of what Kara is, not the new action template. After this, she is full-strength Supergirl for the rest of the film. The temptation shifts from weakness to power. |
| **Full yellow-sun return** | Climax | She can win. That is no longer interesting. | She cannot make vengeance mean healing | Power becomes temptation: now that nothing external can stop her, only character can. |

**Krem and the Brigands are not power peers.** Against full Supergirl in an empty field, they do not matter. So the story should never stage them as if they are simply "strong enough" to trade blows with her. They matter because they control conditions: red light, numbers, ships, poisoned time, damaged infrastructure, route access, terrain, medical access, and Ruthye's grief.

**No kryptonite, no green-sun escalation.** If Krem knows Kara is Kryptonian and knows exactly how to counter her, the movie becomes gadget chess: why not just lure her under a green-only sun, or use kryptonite, or keep stacking weaknesses? That is too much lore math for too little payoff. The cleaner rule is that Krem does **not** need special Kryptonian knowledge. He is exploiting the situation in front of him: a drunk, depleted woman on a red-sun world, a loyal dog, a ship, and a route out.

**Use the sun like a central conceit, not a gimmick.** Red sun at the beginning is Kara's avoidance made physical. Yellow sun at the end is capacity and responsibility returning. The yellow-sun rebirth is the audience's one cathartic burst of Supergirl spectacle — then the story immediately changes the question from "can she do it?" to "what will she do now that she can?" Anything more complicated has to earn being the premise of the whole movie; otherwise it overstays its welcome.

**Rule of thumb:** every fight should be decided by a situation, not a stat sheet. If the question is "could Kara beat this guy?" the scene is wrong. The question should be: *what does saving one thing cost while another thing is being lost?*

## Characters & Arcs

**Ruthye — the protagonist.**
Thirteen, fierce, the elder of two. Cold, and not helpless — serious in a way that unsettles adults. She cites her people's code: the law demands she hunt down her father's killer. The code is real, but it's also armor — a way to process unbearable loss as obligation instead of grief. Underneath the formality: she may blame herself (did she freeze? could she have stopped it?), or she may simply want blood. Probably both, and she can't tell the difference yet, which is why the code is so useful — it doesn't ask her to.

Arc: Krem's creed ("this is what the world is") against the life she left behind. The code gives her a path toward death; the brother and mother are the pull toward life. Her arc is not being *argued* out of revenge — it's being forced, in an irreversible moment, to *choose*. She raises the sword and cuts the chain, not the neck — taking the antidote, choosing life. A choice, not a speech. The road of life, not death — and the audience has to feel what it costs her to turn away.

**Kara / Supergirl — the deuteragonist with the harder internal arc.**
The more dangerous of the two, because she could actually do it and be right to. She *agrees* with Ruthye; she wants Krem dead too. Her restraint is barely-held, not serene — she talks herself down and lets Ruthye overhear. She has killed before, justified, and it hollowed her: the drinking and the red-sun drifting are aftermath, not quirk. She isn't modeling a virtue she's mastered; she's modeling a recovery she's barely holding. Arc: avoidance/numbness → engagement/integration. She convinces herself as much as anyone.

**Krypto — the last of Argo.**
An adopted stray from a dead world, and the most important living being in Kara's life. Everyone else she loved is gone; Krypto is the last link to her past that still breathes. He is what she has instead of a family — she can leave Clark's voicemails unanswered, but not the dog. When he takes the poison bolt meant for her, her avoidance stops being a mood and becomes a countdown: the one bond she never numbed is the one her hiding almost kills. The clock the whole movie runs on isn't a MacGuffin; it's a dying dog who trusted her.

**Krem — the blight and the mirror.**
Motivated, never sociopathic: a survivor of his own dead world who chose predation and kept choosing it. He arrives at Ruthye's homestead needing weapons and supplies; he leaves having killed her father — not out of necessity but out of *spite*, a deliberate act aimed at the defiance in a thirteen-year-old's eyes. *"This is what the world is."* He's not just a predator; he's an evangelist of cruelty, a recruiter — he wants to unmake the belief that the world is fair, because his was unmade. Origin as explanation, never mitigation. A *finished* tragedy — a memento mori, Ruthye's possible future and Kara's nightmare answer to grief: *this is what's down there.* The cold open is the off-ramp made concrete: things cool, the transaction is survivable, and he kills anyway. No redemption arc; he is the proof of what un-redemption looks like. Ruthye's entire quest is, at bottom, a fight over whether she accepts his worldview.

He does not need to be physically scaled to Supergirl. That is a trap. Krem's strength is that he represents a view of the world that might be right, one that Ruthye might choose. 

Diegetically, he is embedded in machinery larger than his body: men, ships, weapons, stolen routes, damaged infrastructure, escape corridors, etc.

**Lobo — the Universe, personified.**
The answer to "what if killing is nothing." A single Act II encounter, not a plot crutch. 

Lobo, the main man, is one of the most OP characters in the DCU (can fight Superman to a standstill). He exudes coolness and a devil-may-care attitude, and has an anti-hero charm (his flaunting/bending of rules, but also his own demands on honesty and his own particular code of honor). Lobo is dangerous, yes, but capricious, not malevolent. Introduced in the 90s to poke fun at the grimdark trends of late 80s/90s superheroes, Lobo's MO is absurdly over-the-top ultra-violence and he gives us a look at what a Supergirl *without* a moral code looks like.

His appearance is also an example of the capricious and strange universe, and serves as a counterpoint to Kara (regret), Ruthye (revenge), *or* Krem (cruelty). Most of the time, death and cruelty in the universe... just doesn't care how you feel about it. It just happens. As part of the plot, it's important that Lobo's appearance doesn't act as a crutch or convenience. It's a slight complication, a chaos monkey wrinkle, yes, but it serves as an agency *concentrator*. He clears away the table for the actual conflict.

His scene gives us the traditional superhero/action movie carnage that gets the blood pumping — it might be what many expect and want from their summer blockbuster. It should be fun and absurd in the creativeness of its ultraviolence, but should be gruesome enough to make the audience question that's what they really want, especially as corpses pile up.

His appearance is fun and refreshing, his power is effortless, it's a breath of fresh air, seductive versus the heavy emotional struggles Kara and Ruthye have endured, but in the end we should be snapped back to reality for Ruthye's decisive moment. At the end, as he toys with the final Brigands on his way to Krem we should be getting queasy, and feeling ambivalent about it with uncomfortable laughter.

Lobo was created out of satire and farce - his liminal space is the half-wink (the default mode for the Superhero movie genre?) - our Supergirl story is his Kryptonite, actually. Earnestness, sincerity, *unirony*. Lobo is going to drop in, realize he's actually in the wrong movie, and then leaves.


**The Brigands — a survivor-culture's answer to extinction.**
*Take, so you don't end.* Motivation explains without excusing. Lobo kills the ones we see on screen; the system they represent is larger, but the film doesn't pretend to solve it. What matters for the climax is that Krem — stripped of his men, his system, his philosophy — is alone with the consequences of everything he chose.

## The Relationship Engine

Not mentor and student. **Two people at different mile-markers on the same road, keeping each other from relapsing.**

Three survivors of dead worlds run three responses to catastrophe — Kara's numbness, Ruthye's vengeance, the Brigands' predation — and the film is about which responses leave you a person. The rescue is **mutual**: the student saves the teacher. Each convinces herself by watching the other do the brave thing first.

## Story Spine

**Act I — Avoidance causes the wound.**
Kara is hiding on Evelie, a red-sun world, because powerlessness is numbness. Evelie is Ruthye's world too: the cold-open homestead is local, and the Brigands are still dirtside because she stole the drive crystal / fuel core their ship needs. Separately, her tracker is keyed to one of her father's taken weapons — intergalactic inventory management, not magic — and it points her to the port bar. Kara tells her to go home; Ruthye turns on the tracker; Krem walks through the door. In the brawl, Krypto takes a poison bolt meant for Kara — Brigand-tipped, not kryptonite. The medbay identifies poison and gives a countdown; only a ranking Brigand's antidote will save him, and Krem has it on his body. Krem recovers the fuel core and escapes in his own ship. Kara's ship is not the target; it is just the nearest bad hospital and her first desperate way to reach a yellow sun. When Kara redlines the drive for the closest yellow star, they still cannot make it. The burned-out ship limps into a deep-space rest stop to refuel/repair, where Krypto stays in a dock-medbay stabilizing tube that buys hours, not the days or weeks needed for drive regrowth. Kara still has flashes of power, but she is too depleted and impaired to stop Krem — *by her own choice to hide.* The intergalactic bus becomes the only way Kara and Ruthye can keep moving. Ruthye leaves behind a living mother and younger brother to pursue Krem — off her own world for the first time, choosing the road over the life she has. Quest = penance + a ticking poison clock.

**Act II — Endurance, not spectacle.**
Mostly depowered, but never in a way that requires arithmetic. The relationship carries the film. The mechanics are deliberately mundane: medbay countdown, failed yellow-sun sprint, burned-out drive component, rest-stop information desk, public transit drop-shell to the edge of Bilki. Three load-bearing beats escalate Ruthye from passenger to agent:

1. **Kara protects Ruthye.** A fight goes wrong and Kara physically shields Ruthye — takes a hit, absorbs damage, doesn't let her near the fire. This becomes Kara's default and her cross to bear: protect the kid, carry the weight. Ruthye resents it. She didn't come here to be protected. She came to fight.

2. **Kara stops Ruthye from a cold kill — the Lobo scene.** Lobo rips through Brigands, hands Krem to Ruthye on a platter, and she draws her sword. Kara — at her weakest — steps between them and takes the slash. Bleeds. "That's my code." Two codes, one sword. The fracture: Ruthye didn't mean to hurt Kara, but the thing she wanted just cut the one person who cares about her. Krem scrambles away. Lobo, repulsed by the emotion, flies them to a yellow sun and vanishes. Kara's first recharge isn't a choice — it's survival. (See: "The Lobo Scene.")

3. **Ruthye saves Kara — the Lobo scene climax.** The flip happens inside the same encounter: after Kara takes the slash, Ruthye has to choose between pursuing Krem and saving the person who just bled for her. She turns to Lobo — the most dangerous being on the planet, who respects nothing but codes — and invokes hers: *"Take us to the sun or kill me now. The code demands it."* Not begging. An ultimatum from a thirteen-year-old girl to the Main Man, delivered with the same iron she used to cite her father's right to vengeance. Lobo, against every instinct, complies — not out of kindness (beneath him), but because a kid who'll die before she backs down from her code is the one thing in the universe he can't dismiss. The yellow-sun ride is Ruthye saving Kara's life, and the rehearsal for the climax: she's practiced choosing life over death once, with someone she loves. Now she knows she can do it.

Between these beats: the ship scene under the medbay countdown, the deep-space rest stop/bus sequence where the faint directional tracker is lined up against a public spacemap and the destination resolves as Bilki, the Brigand dead world, the bubble-shell drop through nuclear winter, a moment where Kara's instinct is to numb out and Ruthye names it, and the dead-world trail that turns Krem's nihilism into landscape. Krypto is already the consequence of Kara's avoidance; the story doesn't need an extra civilian death to prove the price. The obstacles should be tactical and moral, not "is this henchman somehow stronger than Supergirl this week?"

**Act III — The choice.**
Power returns under the yellow sun, reframing the climax: not "can she win" but "what will she do with everything." The forced, irreversible choice arrives through the Krypto clock waiting back at the rest-stop medbay — revenge and the antidote made physically incompatible, the same instant, her own hands. Ruthye slashes the chain, not the neck. Kara holds her own fire. We never see if Krem lives or dies; the camera stays with Ruthye as she walks away. They race the antidote back to the rest stop. Krypto breathes. The close is warmth: Ruthye home with her family, the birthday finally celebrated, a girl giggling for the first time in the film. Kara's resolution is quieter — a mid-credits beat, alone at a window in Metropolis, choosing to stay instead of hide.

*Avoidance → engagement → integration. Paid in full.*

## Cold Open — "This Is What the World Is"

Evelie, a red-sun farm world. Ruthye's homestead. A kitchen table. The family mid-meal — father, mother, Ruthye (13), her younger brother (5 or 6). Ordinary domestic warmth: the kind of life that exists because someone chose to build it.

Krem enters with a few of his men. He doesn't break down the door. He sits. Helps himself to pie. He needs weapons, supplies — practical demands delivered as though he's entitled to the hospitality. His men fan out through the house.

Then he starts talking. Not threats — a story. His world. How it ended. The people who stood by, who traded with the destroyers, who looked the other way. *"I was like you once. So haughty. Before my world was destroyed. Before I saw how things really are."* He's calm, almost reflective. The violence is in the past tense; the man at the table seems tired, not cruel.

The father tries to de-escalate. Apologizes. Says no, it's not true, his people weren't part of that. But it is true, and the denial sits in the room like a held breath. Krem registers it. Lets it pass. Keeps eating.

Then he strikes the mother. Not to kill — to test. The father reacts, protective, instinctive. Krem's men hold him. The room goes loud, then louder, then —

It cools. Krem could leave now. He has what he came for. The father is restrained but alive. The transaction is ugly but survivable. The off-ramp is right there.

Krem locks eyes with Ruthye.

He sees it — the defiance, the pride, the conviction that the world is fair and this is an aberration. He recognizes what he used to be. And something in him, the thing that chose predation and kept choosing it, refuses to let it stand.

He kills the father. Not in the scuffle. After it. A deliberate act, eye-contact with Ruthye the whole time. A lesson.

*"This is what the world is."*

He says it like he's doing her a favor. Like he's the first honest man she's ever met.

He leaves. Takes the weapons, the supplies. Doesn't touch the children. Doesn't need to — he's already done the worst thing he came to do, and it wasn't the killing. It was the teaching.

**Final image:** Ruthye, shielding her brother against her body. The boy is huddled into her, shaking — he knows, but hasn't seen. Her arms are around him and her eyes are fixed on the door Krem walked through. Not grief. Not shock. *Rage* — the kind that has already decided something, before the mind catches up.

The quest begins here: not when she meets Kara, not when she picks up a sword, but in this image — a thirteen-year-old girl holding the life she should choose, staring at the road she will.

## Kara's Morning — The Nightmare and the Voicemail

Cut from Ruthye's rage to silence. Kara, asleep — on her ship, or in a rented room in Evelie's port town. Same world, same red sun: Ruthye's rage and Kara's hangover are neighbors, two lives not yet touching. Not peaceful. A nightmare: Argo dying. Her parents' faces. The last moments she can't stop replaying. Fragmentary, visceral — not a full flashback, just the images that won't let go.

She wakes gasping. Reaches for the bottle before her eyes are open. That tells you everything about how long this has been going on.

On a device, a voicemail from Clark. His voice is warm, concerned, reaching out — the cousin she has, the family she's choosing not to answer. She listens. Doesn't call back. Gets another drink.

We see the ship. We see the red sun through a porthole. We understand: she has a way home, she has people who love her, and she is choosing the bottle and the dead light of a star that makes her feel nothing. Avoidance isn't something that happened to her. It's a practice she maintains every morning.

This sequence does one job: by the time Kara meets Ruthye and hears "I have a mother and a brother," the audience already knows what that sentence does to her, because they've just watched her refuse her own family. The grief is front-loaded, not hidden until a mid-film reveal — so the "go home" scene lands on first contact. The nightmare is fragmentary on purpose: the full memory — the fight, the promise, "be good" — is held back for the near-death flashback in the Lobo scene, where it becomes the emotional core of the film.

## First Meeting — "Go Home"

A bar in Evelie's port town. Kara is drunk — not charmingly, not as a bit. Avoidance wearing a glass. She wears the battered oversized leather jacket she'll carry through most of the movie: too big, beat to hell, half armor and half blanket. The physics and the addiction rhyme: the longer she stays under the red sun, the weaker she gets; the more she drinks, the less able she is to marshal whatever strength remains.

Ruthye walks in already on the hunt. A ruffian gets in her way and she disarms him — clean, one-on-one, no hesitation. Not a trained fighter, but cold and serious in a way that makes grown men recalculate. She's thirteen and she just won a barfight. The room notices.

Kara notices too. She's intrigued despite herself — or maybe she recognizes something. They sit. Kara asks where she's from, what a kid is doing alone in a port bar. Ruthye cites the code, explains the hunt, matter-of-fact: she has tracked Krem and the Brigands from her family's homestead to this port. They are still on this world. Nearby. She isn't asking for a mentor. She's looking for muscle.

Kara asks about family.

Ruthye says she has a mother and a younger brother. Still alive. Waiting for her.

Kara goes quiet. Then emotional — not performatively, not a speech. The kind of crack that alcohol lets through. Her family is dead. Her world is dead. She's sitting across from a girl who has everything Kara lost, and the girl is *leaving it behind* to go chase a death.

She tells Ruthye to go home.

Ruthye refuses. The code demands it. Her father demands it. She won't hear otherwise. To prove this isn't fantasy, she pulls a baseball-sized, grungy metal-and-glass case with a glowing drive crystal inside — the Brigand ship's fuel core. *"They aren't leaving without this."* Then she puts a separate tracker device on the table: keyed to one of her father's weapons Krem took in the raid, not to the crystal. The audience doesn't need the explanation on-screen; the bible does. It is faint and directional until she aligns it.

She turns it on.

It glows intensely bright.

Not *somewhere out there.* Here.

The bar door slams open. Krem and the Brigands have come for their drive crystal. Ruthye isn't the only one with tracking capabilities.

The brawl is immediate and ugly. Kara is not instantly powerless, but she has been here too long and drunk too much. She has residue, not command: a burst of speed here, a too-hard punch there, then a stumble that would never happen under a yellow sun. She is not invincible — just stubborn, trained, and still Kara. Krypto fights like the loyal idiot-saint he is. A Brigand poison bolt meant for Kara hits Krypto instead. Kara flips and throws a heavy table through the wall and rushes to Krypto. And the Brigands beat a hasty retreat. (They have what they came for.)

That is the inciting wound. No kryptonite, no special anti-Kryptonian trick — the Brigands tip their weapons with a raider's poison, the kind built to drop tough targets. It's what they use. The antidote exists because Brigand leaders carry doses for their own people (friendly fire, accidents), not because every corpse on the field is a cure cabinet. The clock is set: hours of function, then stasis or death. The dock medbay can slow the collapse; it cannot make the deadline go away.

They get Krypto back to Kara's ship because it is the closest thing Kara has to a hospital and a way off-world. The ship is nearby because Kara has been living out of it; the Brigands do not need it and do not need to steal it. They have their own ship — faster, crewed, loaded, already pointed at escape. Krem recovers the drive crystal Ruthye stole from him — the thing he came for, the thing that had him pinned on Evelie — and leaves in his own vessel with the leader's antidote dose on his body. Ruthye still has the tracker.

Kara's medbay is not a solution, but it is useful enough to be cruel. It identifies poison in Krypto's system, estimates the irreversible-collapse window, and turns the emergency into numbers on a screen. It cannot synthesize the cure. Under a red sun, with Kara impaired and the ship half-neglected, the medbay gives them a countdown, not hope.

So they launch because staying means watching Krypto die. Kara's first instinct is not revenge or pursuit; it is to get Krypto to the closest yellow sun as fast as the ship can go. 

She explains this to Ruthye and the audience ("under the red sun, we get weak; a yellow sun can heal us"). She redlines the drive trying to outrun the poison and fails. The ship burns through its fuel, overheats the jump system, and drops them at the nearest place that can refuel or repair anything: a deep-space intergalactic rest stop / transit hub, not a power-transition location. There, a dock-healer/diagnostic rig confirms the specific type: Brigand poison. Antidote-specific. Not something Kara can punch or sunbathe away. The healer also gives the useful, awful detail: Brigand leaders carry the antidote, because their own weapons are as dangerous to them as to anyone else. Krem is the leader in play. Kara's ship has gotten them off the red-sun world, but it cannot keep going until it is refueled and repaired. The chase becomes public transit by necessity, not whim. Kara still has not agreed to Ruthye's revenge quest. She goes because Krypto is dying, because Krem has the only cure on him, and because her chosen powerlessness put everyone in this room within reach of a monster.

(We could inject humor and a callback if we have the ship warning the it's low on fuel/near at the beginning movie; or we can have energy drained as she tries first to fire yellow light created by the drive that draws down its power. It should not be well thought out as a inebriated Kara tries her best to hold it together and to save Krypto...)

The scene establishes: Kara is the broken one, not the wise one. Ruthye is the composed one, not the helpless one. The power dynamic is inverted from the start — the adult is a mess and the child has a mission. And "go home" is planted as the line the whole movie bends toward: Ruthye can't hear it yet, but the audience will remember it when she finally chooses to. No stealth boarding or repeated stowaway mechanics: Ruthye belongs in the plot because her choices created the collision and because her tracker is still the only line to Krem.

---

## The Road, Part One — The Rest Stop and the Bus

*Early Act II. They've left the red-sun world. Kara is functional but diminished — residual strength, nothing commanding. They're in Kara's ship only long enough to try the obvious solution: reach the closest yellow sun before the poison wins. She pushes the drive past safe limits. The medbay has identified poison and given them a countdown; the yellow sun is too far; now the jump system is burning out underneath them.*

The bonding isn't a speech. It's proximity — and logistics.

On Kara's ship, before the rest stop, there is time for the first forced intimacy: the medbay counting down in the background, Krypto breathing in little mechanical assists, Kara pretending she knows which warning light matters, Ruthye watching an adult fail without falling apart. Kara is all motion — rerouting power, swearing at the drive, trying to make the ship do one impossible thing. Ruthye is stillness. She reads the tracker, reads Kara, and starts to understand that this woman is not refusing revenge because she feels less. She may feel too much.

They limp into a deep-space intergalactic rest stop: alien truckstop energy, somewhere between a bus terminal, a refueling dock, a clinic, and a market bazaar. No sun, no power transition — just fluorescent commerce in the middle of nowhere. A mechanic tells Kara the ship can be refueled, but the real problem is the burned-out drive component — we recognize it as Kara's version of the Brigand drive crystal, coil, seed-core, whatever the local tech calls it. It doesn't grow on trees. Or maybe, annoyingly, it *does* grow: in a vat, over days or weeks. The dock-healer tells her the same thing the medbay and failed yellow-sun sprint implied, now with a name: Brigand poison. Krypto needs the antidote a Brigand leader carries, not better monitoring and not just sunlight. The clinic can put him in a liquid stabilizing tube and buy them a little time, but it is not the days or weeks the drive regrowth needs. The ship stays docked. Krypto stays in the dock medbay now — a slight reprieve, but condition worsening. Kara promises to return with the antidote, or to die trying.

So the cosmic road trip becomes humiliatingly mundane: Supergirl and a vengeance-bound thirteen-year-old, boarding an intergalactic bus. (That being said, Ruthye has never been off-world and this is the first time to take it all in. It should be a juxtaposition of weird and mundane — we're seeing it all for the first time and it should be instantly recognizable, but also totally weird.)

At the information desk, Ruthye lines the faint directional tracker up with the public spacemap. The route resolves: not a bar, not a second-family betrayal detour, not another disposable side-quest — a dead-zone world under travel advisory. Nuclear winter. Collapsed biosphere. The attendant gives them the look every bus-terminal employee gives doomed travelers.

*"You don't want to go there."*

Kara asks what it is.

The attendant: *"Bilki. Was. Brigand homeworld, depending who you ask. No one lives there anymore."*

Ruthye points at the route line.

*"Is it still on the drop-off route?"*

The attendant hates that the answer is yes.

*"Technically."*

(Reaction beat: Ruthye expectant, wide-eyed. Kara with a triumphantly goofy grin.)

That feels right. Krem isn't headed to a lair because the plot needs one; he's headed back to the graveyard that made him. The Brigands use their dead world as a depot, a hideout, a dare, a wound they keep picking. A people who survived the end by becoming the kind of people who could live in the ruins.

Kara and Ruthye need supplies: food, bus tickets bought with whatever Kara has left after paying for the dock and the diagnosis. The mundane logistics of a road trip through space. Kara knows her way around these places; Ruthye doesn't. The dynamic is parent-dragging-teenager-through-errands, except the teenager has a sword.

In the washroom, Kara and Ruthye stand side by side at the basin mirrors, cleaning up. Kara looks wrecked; Ruthye looks smaller than she has all movie. For the first time, she looks less like a soldier and more like a thirteen-year-old girl who misses her mother. Kara sees the armor drop in the reflection and puts a hand on her shoulder. A tear rolls out; Kara wipes it away. No words, no platitudes. Then Ruthye's mask is back on. She walks out composed, Kara following, decidedly less composed.

Small things: Kara buys something Ruthye didn't ask for (food she noticed her eyeing, a self-deploying bubble space-suit ring, a glib "now you won't die in space"). Ruthye reads star maps better than Kara does and doesn't gloat about it. The ice cracks not through drama but through competence — two people becoming useful to each other before they're ready to be kind.

---

The bus takes off, out of frame, and we refocus on a bulletin board: Krem's face, among others, on a faded posting — BRIGANDS, WANTED: DEAD OR ALIVE. We see a flash of a giant white hand (and a chain) ripping the paper.

(Alternate: this is an unspecified amount of time later — funny attendant interaction with Lobo. He puts the paper down (These guys." and gets the location. "You missed the last bus; next one is tomorrow." "No need." How much we want to show — blue flame of the bike, etc. — is something we can debate, depending on how we want to do the reveal.)

## The Road, Part Two — "I've Done What You're Planning"

*Mid Act II. Flashbacks from Argo; Kara startles awake. They're on the bus, seated between some funky aliens.*

Ruthye eyes Kara as she pulls out a flask and takes a swig. Not a dramatic bender — just the quiet, practiced reach for the bottle, the thing she does to stop feeling. The same gesture from the morning sequence, the one the audience already knows.

Ruthye says "You should stop that."

The child, wiser than the adult again. It's an interesting line delivered as a matter-of-fact statement, a controlled demand? But behind it, an understanding of what Kara is doing.

Kara does. Not quite mid-swig, a bit embarrassed. A thirteen-year-old girl being the adult. And in the silence that follows, she talks.

Not all of it. Not a monologue. Pieces. Argo's destruction. Not the noble sacrifice the legends will tell — the *slow* version. The sky going wrong. The ground shaking for days. People she knew dying in ones and twos and then all at once. The smell. The sounds. Her parents making her leave while they stayed. The pod, and the silence after.

Ruthye listens. She doesn't interrupt. She doesn't compare grief. She just listens — the same way Kara listened to the code speech in the bar, recognizing something without trying to fix it.

Then Kara, quiet:

*"I've been down your road. Revenge won't make you feel better."*

She doesn't explain further. Doesn't say who, or when, or whether it was justified. The audience doesn't need to know. What matters is the confession: Kara isn't speaking from moral theory. She's speaking from the other side.

Unconsciously, Kara has taken her flask out again, and looks at it long and hard, and puts it away.

Ruthye doesn't respond. The scene doesn't resolve. The confession just sits between them, and the road continues. But something has shifted — Ruthye can't pretend Kara is just a drunk with opinions anymore. She's been there. She came back. She's still coming back.

Ruthye looks outside the bus window, we haven't noticed but it's actually beautiful, nebulas and stars, a myriad of colors, but also cold emptiness.

---

## The Brigand Trail — Bilki, the Dead World

*The bus doesn't take them to Krem. It takes them as close as public transit is willing to admit it goes.*

The last stop is not a landing pad. It's a floating drop point with a big angry red holographic warning sign above a world that looks already dead from orbit: completely gray atmosphere, lightning under cloud cover, patches of old fire still burning in the dark. The driver will not land there. Nobody lands there. The "drop-off route" is a clear bubble-shell bolted to the underside of the bus — half escape pod, half luggage chute — rated for toxic atmosphere and plausible deniability.

Kara looks at it.

*"Wait. How do we get picked up?"*

The driver shrugs, already pressing the eject control.

For one clean second as the shell falls away from the bus, the yellow sun catches on its curved surface — a tiny, impossible glint of life above the planet. Kara presses her hand against the clear orbital shell, not because it will work, but because she needs it to: a reflexive, almost childish attempt to drink in even a thread of solar energy through the glass. 

Ruthye gives a meaningful look, she notices.

Ruthye's tracker points down.

They descend.

The tracker dot isn't far, which is worse. Close means they don't have the luxury of waiting. In the sky, a blue flame arc streaks toward the same direction they're headed. Kara and Ruthye look at each other: another mystery, moving faster than either of them can afford to think about.

The world looks like Argo at the end, but a whole planet. Kara is grim — lack of sunlight, flashbacks, alcohol withdrawal, the medbay countdown still in her head. All of it. She's in bad shape.

The trail leads into old Brigand infrastructure: depot, bunker, transit spine, whatever remains of a civilization that learned to move supplies after it stopped knowing how to make a home. Krem's crew hasn't set a mastermind trap; they have simply reactivated dead-world defenses because that is what this place was built to do. Automated turrets, pressure mines, old perimeter guns coughing back to life, machines with no ideology and no mercy.

This is where **Kara protects Ruthye** gets its home. Ruthye wants to move toward the tracker. Kara keeps putting herself between the kid and the fire. She wraps Ruthye in her arms through a turret burst, takes shrapnel across the back, drags her behind broken concrete, then hauls her onto a piggyback run when the ground starts detonating under them. It is not clean superspeed; it's a ruined, depleted approximation of it — Kara stumbling through gunfire, shielding Ruthye with her own body, getting beaten to hell by machines she could have ignored at full power. The leather jacket gets shredded here: less cool costume now, more ragged hide, something she is still clinging to.

Ruthye hates it. Not because she is ungrateful, but because every save feels like being made small. She came to kill Krem. Kara keeps making her a child.

Then: smoke, a giant explosion, and the sound of gunfire and screams from ahead. Something alive is tearing through the Brigands.

Kara adjusts Ruthye on her back and runs the last stretch, barely standing, heaving against the world as she reaches the ruckus, explosions and flames licking around them, running on fumes.

---

## The Lobo Scene — "That's My Code"

*Act II. Kara is depleted. Ruthye is hardening. The Brigands apparently have their own problems.*

Lobo is there, literally tearing a Brigand in half, laughing. He's here for the bounty, but he loves it when they fight back. He rips through the Brigands effortlessly, cheerfully, the way a man swats flies. Bodies everywhere. One-liners zing. The audience watches the most likable guy in the movie do the thing the movie has been agonizing over, and it takes him about ninety seconds. The ease is the horror. The ease is also the temptation.

We get an Action Comics shot of the Main Man, with a truck over his head, silhouetted by the glow of flames and a slow-mo explosion. It's glorious, iconic. Then he throws it down and we get a grisly slick crunch as it crashes down, blood from the front cabin oozing out, splattered against the windshield. There were of course *people* in there.

We should start getting the idea that the carnage isn't just spectacle. It's murder. As he works his way through the final remnants (crushing one of the remaining suffering/begging dismembered Brigands like an ant. "Please..." he begs. "You're welcome," Lobo might say genially as he snuffs him out like you might crush an ant).

He ends up with Krem, alone. (Maybe kicking him, lifting him up and ready to squeeze.) Krem is sniveling, begging for his life — the philosopher of cruelty reduced to pleading the instant his own creed is applied to him. This is a turning point for Krem: the man who told a thirteen-year-old girl "this is what the world is" just proved his worldview is a lie, because when the world came for *him*, he showed his strength was really just a facade. It turns out, he's a coward. By the climax, he won't be the same man. Lobo is about to finish it — casual, meaningless to him, another corpse on a planet full of them.

Ruthye seeing this, is enraged. She calls out "You can't kill him." Cites the code. By her people's law, *she* has the right to vengeance.

Lobo stops. Looks at this thirteen-year-old girl who just told the Main Man to wait. He gets serious — genuinely, not mockingly. Something in him recognizes what she's carrying.

*"Kid, if anyone looks at me funny, I kill them. That's my rule. But a code — having a code is worth more than all the corpses on this planet."*

Beat. (we see the landscape) Then the grin comes back. Main Man energy.

*"You got moxie. I like that. I can tell — this guy's been eating you up. Today's your lucky day. I'm basically done here anyway, so I'm going to give this to you. All yours. You'll feel great. Trust me."*

He roughly throws Krem down, on his knees. This will be execution. Krem looks at Ruthye, pleading, pathetic. She's having none of it. She steels her face. "This is what the world is," she says, drawing her sword back, snarling.

This is the moment. Lobo — the most powerful being in the scene — has made killing *effortless*. No fight, no moral anguish, no cost. Just take the swing. The movie's entire thesis is on the line.

Kara — depleted, barely standing, the weakest version of Supergirl — steps between Ruthye and Krem. Ruthye's sword catches her across the body. Kara collapses, bleeding.

Lobo is dumbfounded with "ooh that's gotta hurt"/"didn't see that one coming" reaction. Genuinely speechless. He's killed gods, fought Superman, crossed the universe — and he has never seen someone voluntarily step into a blade to save a person from *themselves*. A beat of stunned silence before anything else happens. Even Main Man has no frame of reference for this.

Silence. Ruthye stares at what she just did. She didn't mean to hit Kara. She wanted Krem. But the thing she wanted just cut the one person who gives a damn about her.

Lobo's focused on the scene. Whatever is happening between these two is *feelings*, and feelings are disgusting. Krem starts crawling away in the chaos (pathetic) — Kara is bleeding, Lobo looking down at the Ruthye, dropped down cradling Kara's bleeding body. Nobody *let* Krem go; they had bigger things to deal with. And crucially, Krem escapes *because Ruthye tried to kill him* — her revenge attempt caused Kara's wound, which caused the emergency, which opened the gap. Her own vengeance is why the quest continues.

Ruthye is on the ground with Kara. Heart to heart.

*"Why?"*

A whisper that hides a wail.

Kara, fading: *"I had to save you. That's my code."*

Two codes. Ruthye's demands she kill. Kara's demands she prevent it. One sword, and Kara's code cost her blood. The mirror the whole movie has been building — Ruthye is Kara at month one, Kara is Ruthye at year ten — collapses into a single image: the mentor bleeding from the student's blade, not because the student is evil but because grief almost made her something she isn't.

Kara's eyes flutter, looks up at the faint light through the darkened nuclear winter sky. She's fading.

Ruthye looks up too, and then glances at Krem, she has to choose — right now, on her knees in the blood — between Krem disappearing with her revenge, and the woman bleeding out in front of her. It's not that hard. She looks up at Lobo.

*"Take us up to see the sun."*

Lobo snorts. He doesn't do favors. He doesn't do *feelings.*

*"Listen kid, the nobody tells the Main Man-"*

Ruthye stands. The same iron she used to cite her father's code. The same girl who told the Main Man to wait.

*"Take us or just kill me now. The code demands it."*

She means it. She will die here before she watches Kara die. Lobo sees it — a thirteen-year-old girl who just accidentally cut a person she loves, issuing a death-or-life ultimatum to the most dangerous man alive. Not begging. *Demanding.* Using the one currency he respects.

A beat. Lobo's face is unreadable.

Then: *"Fine, get on"*

Ruthye does not waste the half-second wondering if he means it. She hits the button on the self-deploying bubble space-suit ring Kara bought her at the rest stop. The bubble snaps open with an offensively cheerful little chime (*Deploying!*) around Ruthye — practical, ridiculous, lifesaving. A tiny callback to Kara's throwaway *"now you won't die in space"* becoming the thing that lets Ruthye save her.

Lobo rolls his eyes, lifts and throws Kara over his shoulder in one go.

Kara has a calm look on her face; she's made her peace with her decision — if this has cost her her life, it was worth it.

She closes her eyes.

**The flashback.** Not the fragments from the morning nightmare — the *full memory*. Argo's final moments. Kara is fighting with her parents. She doesn't want to go. She wants to stay and die with them. The argument is ugly, desperate — a teenage girl screaming that she won't leave.

Her parents break. *"If not, it'll all be for nothing."* They collapse, crying. Hugging her. The fight goes out of everyone and what's left is just a family holding each other at the end of a world.

Her mother, holding her face: *"You must live."*

Beat.

Father: *"Promise me you'll be good."*

*"I promise."*

That's the thesis. Not a philosophical position Kara arrived at. A promise she made to a dying woman. Every time she steps between someone and a blade, every time she holds her own fire, every time she chooses restraint over the cold kill — she's keeping a promise. And every day she spent on the red-sun world, drinking, hiding, numbing — she was breaking it.

(Is "good" a state of mind, a philosophy, a measure of actions, a measure of self-love? The answer is probably "yes.")

Cut back to the present. Lobo's space bike. They're rising through the dead atmosphere — nuclear winter, the sky choked, no sunlight reaching the surface. Another dead planet. This does a number on Kara's trauma: everywhere she goes, worlds are ending. But the yellow sun is *right there*, just above the atmosphere. No interstellar travel needed. Kara is slumped against the bike, the battered leather jacket torn open and wrapped around her almost like a death shawl. A tear floating weightless in space. Silence. The nuclear winter thinning around them.

They break through into brilliant, blinding yellow light.

(Alternatively, we could skip the flight up — the flashback covers that time, and we could cut to her opening her eyes and taking that reflexive breath.)

Kara's limp form floating (fetal like, certainly not cruciform), bright yellow light bathing her form. And she takes a reflexive breath — rebirth. Her body insisting on living the way her parents insisted she live. The wounds close. The sun does what the sun does. Life insists on itself. The jacket, finally too damaged to hold together, slips loose and drifts away behind her — the old hide, the death shawl, the thing she wrapped around herself to disappear. The metaphor doesn't need to be stated: from death to life, from avoidance to engagement, from the surface of a dead world into the sun. Kara feels it hit her. Power returning. And with power, everything she's been hiding from.

Under the yellow sun, Kara heals. Ruthye is crying. Kara reaches out and holds her. The lesson that Supergirl needed to show Ruthye. And herself. Lobo watches this, *mortified*. Two people hugboxing in the sunlight and he cannot get away fast enough.

He kicks the bike into gear. Still processing what he saw — a woman stepping into a blade, on purpose, for *feelings.*

*"Well... that was fucked up."*

Over his shoulder, already accelerating away from the crying and the hugging and everything he can't stand:

*"Fucking codes, man."*

Gone. The strongest man in the scene exits because vulnerability is the one thing he can't do. The universe, personified, can do anything except feel — and that's exactly why he can't help them.

**What the scene does:** It fuses Act II beats #2 and #3 into a single encounter. Beat 2: Kara stops Ruthye's cold kill by stepping into the blade. Beat 3: Ruthye saves Kara by choosing her life over revenge and forcing Lobo to take them to the sun. The yellow-sun moment is the rebirth — Kara goes from her weakest to full Supergirl in one transition, never to power down again. It makes mercy cost blood (Rule 7). It gives Ruthye the rehearsal she needs: she's practiced choosing life over death once, so the audience believes she can do it again at the climax. And it removes Lobo from the story permanently, motivated by character, not plot.

## Climax — "Let's Go Home"

**The approach.** Kara is reborn. Full yellow-sun Supergirl — not just physically restored but unburdened. The promise recommitted. The weight off. The audience has waited two hours for this, and the movie gives it to them: she carries Ruthye back down through the dead atmosphere under her own power, a sonic boom clearing the clouds. This is Supergirl.

We see Lobo's carnage below, and Kara scans the area. It's easy. There is a row of Brigand shuttles half-buried in the depot wreckage; one is warmed up, engines hot, loading bay open — the ride Krem is trying to use to run again.

We see Krem: he's gotten it back together a bit. He has a huge duffel bag of gold and jewels in one hand; he's picking a giant energy weapon from the corpse of one of his men. He grimaces as it attaches onto his left shoulder/arm with a grisly thunk.

"Stay back" Kara tells Ruthye as she lands. Ruthye starts to argue. Kara gives her a look — not words, just a look. They're past words now.

Krem stands up, looking at Supergirl, a quick glance at Ruthye, but he can't hold the eye contact.

His eyes are still red from the previous encounter. The dead world lets Krem speak without giving him a villain speech. 

*"Look around you."* he says quietly. *"There was never anyone to save us."*

He could have been talking about Lobo, or about his dead world. Not a mustache-twirling villain. Just a bitter, tired man, almost bored with his own pain. This is the place that taught him his creed, and he has mistaken surviving it for understanding the universe.

He raises his arm — Kara recognizes it, it's his poison bow. But she's focused on the antidote he's wearing around his neck.

**The non-fight.** Krem shoots and we see the absurdity of the power disparity. Kara is already floating off the ground and flying towards him. The arrow moves so slowly it might as well not be moving. We see a shockwave as she moves towards Krem, he's moving too slow to even register, but his eyes widen.

Kara plucks the poison arrow out of the air, and as she closes to him, she slams the crossbow shooter out of his hand, maybe rougher than she needed to.

We spring back to real time and hear Krem howl. The shockwave has blasted him meters back on the ground and his right arm is definitely not pointing the right way. Kara is taken aback; she either didn't expect him to be so weak or she didn't expect herself to be so angry.

Kara takes a beat, looks down, holding her fingers to her head. "Listen, I just want the antidote to save-"

We see a huge red glow filling the screen and Kara looks up to see Krem's arm gun sparking/exploding — fully overloaded for a kamikaze kill shot. The screen goes red and instantly white.

We see a red arc across the sky, the concussive energy blast shooting past the horizon, with Supergirl nowhere to be seen.

Panning back down, through the smoke, we see Ruthye walking over to Krem now. Menacing, badass. He's bleeding and burnt, and what's left of his arm and arm-gun is smoldering and charred, but the antidote still hangs around his neck unharmed.

She's looking down with that same determination from the cold open. Sword drawn. The same look she wore when she disarmed a ruffian in a bar. Nothing in her body language telegraphs mercy. Nothing suggests hesitation. The audience should be in genuine suspense about what will happen — because this girl has been building toward this moment for two hours, and everything about her says she's going to finish it.

Krem looks at her. Not the evangelist from the cold open, not the philosopher who killed her father with deliberate spite. The man who begged for his life and watched his own creed collapse. He's tired. Hollowed out.

*"Just do it."*

Not a lesson this time. Not "this is what the world is." Just a man who doesn't care anymore whether he lives or dies — because the version of himself that believed in something, even something monstrous, died on his knees in front of Lobo. He's not offering her graduation. He's not recruiting. He's just... done.

Ruthye looks at him. The thing she sees isn't the monster from her kitchen. It's just a tired man. The regal cruelty that murdered her father is gone. There's nothing left to rage against — no creed to defeat, no philosophy to reject. Just a used-up person in the rubble. Killing him wouldn't prove anything. It wouldn't disprove anything. It would just be killing.

She raises the sword.

The audience holds its breath.

She slashes — not his neck. The chain. Antidote lifted on her blade, she turns around.

Kara has been standing behind her (looking none the worse for wear; this is Supergirl). How long has she been there? She didn't intervene. Certainly she *could* have... but *would* she have?

The look on her face as Ruthye turns around with the antidote instead of blood on the blade: relief, mostly, but also pride. She's watching a thirteen-year-old stepping off of Krem's path. Maybe avoiding her own as well.

The full arc of Kara's relationship to Ruthye, completed in one beat of *inaction*: she protected her (Act II beat 1), she restrained her (the Lobo scene), she was saved by her (beat 3) — and now she *trusted* her. The last and hardest thing.

Ruthye hands over the antidote, their hands once again connected, and Kara hugs her, both tearful.

*"Let's go home."* Ruthye says.

They board the warmed-up Brigand shuttle and launch back toward the rest stop. We never see if Krem lives or dies. The movie doesn't show it because Ruthye doesn't look back, and the camera stays with her. His fate is irrelevant. The film's refusal to show it is part of the point: the story was never about him.

---

## Ending — The Birthday

Cut. Back at the rest-stop clinic, the antidote is injected into Krypto. No fanfare, no speech — just the needle, and the breath that follows. Krypto lifts his head up, and Kara puts hers to his. Life, chosen and saved. Kara's ship is there too, repaired enough to leave, a salvaged Brigand drive crystal installed in her ship.

Cut. Later, at Ruthye's homestead: extreme close-up of Krypto leaping into Kara, alive, warm, the reunion the whole film has been running toward. As we pull out of the close-up, we see it's not just Kara — Ruthye is beside her, and behind Ruthye stand her brother and her mother. Kara's repaired ship waits in the background. Home. The place Ruthye almost threw away.

Ruthye smiles — wistful, not triumphant. *"I wish I could go with you, but I have..."* She nods back at her family. She doesn't finish the sentence. She doesn't need to.

Kara, solemn: *"I would trade the world for what you have. This is where you belong."*

The line that started as a drunk's plea in a bar — *go home* — is now the truest thing either of them has ever said to each other. They both hear it this time.

Beat. Then Kara, with an impish smile breaking through the solemnity: *"...but you know, we never did get a chance to finish celebrating my birthday."*

Ruthye's face — the cold, serious, code-citing girl who won a barfight at thirteen — cracks open. She giggles. For the first time in the film, the weight lifts. The load comes off her shoulders and the audience feels it go.

They take off in Kara's ship — her own, collected from the dock after the antidote handoff. Kara yells back to Ruthye's mother: *"We'll be back tonight — tomorrow at the latest!"*

**Final image:** Ruthye laughing, free, as their ship flies across the fiery red sky. The adventure that was always supposed to be fun, finally is — after it earned it.

*The cold open began with a girl shielding her brother, eyes full of rage. The film ends with the same girl, giggling, choosing the life.*

---

## Mid-Credits — Home

Metropolis. A window. Kara looking out at the skyline — the opposite of Argo, gleaming and alive.

Her face in the reflection: sad, but present. Not numb. Not drinking. Feeling it all at once, the way the whole film has been asking her to.

*"Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer this time."*

A breath.

*"I mean, it's good to be... home."*

Looking at Krypto in the reflection being a terror (who her true home is).

She's not sure yet. She might not be sure for a long time. But she's here, and she's choosing to stay — not hiding on a red-sun world, not dodging Clark's calls, not reaching for the bottle before her eyes open.

The audience leaves with the sense that she's going to be all right.

## DCU, not DCEU (the guardrails)

- **Hope and wonder are the destination**, even though grief is the substrate. *All-Star Superman* warmth; *For All Seasons* coming-of-age melancholy; goodness as kindness and restraint.
- **The ethic and the aesthetic must agree.** The DCEU's fatal incoherence is spectacle-death beside a sacred code the world makes hollow. In our story, death and violence happen, but killing in cold blood is a moral choice.
- **"Be good" is a wager, not a proof you win** — made without certainty, against your own justified rage. The most adult thing the film can own is that Kara isn't sure she's right. She just knows that it leaves a mark, and you probably aren't able to see that until afterwards.

## Design Decisions — Why This, Not That

*Recording the reasoning behind key choices so we remember what we rejected and why.*

### Krem kills only the father, not the whole family
**Chose:** Father killed; mother and younger brother survive.
**Over:** The 2026 film's version where Krem's violence destroys the entire family.
**Why:** A living family gives Ruthye something to *return to*, which turns her quest into an active sacrifice — every day on the road is a day she's not home. It makes the ending possible ("let's go home" only works if home still exists) and it gives Kara a lever ("I would trade the world for what you have"). Killing everyone makes revenge the default; a surviving family makes revenge a *choice she's making over them*.

### Krem is motivated, not sociopathic
**Chose:** Survivor of a dead world who chose predation and kept choosing it. An evangelist of cruelty: "this is what the world is."
**Over:** The 2026 film's maximally monstrous, child-trafficking, irredeemable cartoon villain.
**Why:** A cartoon monster removes all moral tension from "should she kill him?" — the answer is obviously yes, so the dilemma the movie spends two hours building is deflated before it arrives. A motivated Krem is *more* frightening because he's comprehensible: he's a memento mori, the literal end of Ruthye's road. The question was never "does he deserve it" — it's "who do you become if you're the one who does it." That question only has weight if Krem is a person, not a symbol. Crucially, motivated ≠ sympathetic. Origin explains, never mitigates.

### The cold open shows the off-ramp
**Chose:** Things cool down. Krem could leave. He kills the father anyway — a deliberate choice, eye-contact with Ruthye.
**Over:** Krem arriving as an unstoppable force of violence, killing immediately.
**Why:** A berserker is scary but tells you nothing about the theme. The off-ramp establishes that Krem *chose* to be this — the audience watches a man decide to be a monster in real time. That's the "offered an exit, refuses it" beat the bible requires for Krem to work as a mirror. It also makes him an evangelist rather than a predator: he kills to *teach*, not just to take. The father dies not because Krem needs him dead but because Krem saw Ruthye's defiance and wanted to break it.

### Ruthye is cold, not helpless — and cites a code
**Chose:** Serious, composed, unsettling to adults. Cites her people's legal code demanding she hunt her father's killer.
**Over:** A weeping, traumatized child who needs rescuing.
**Why:** A helpless Ruthye makes the movie a rescue story with Kara as the wise mentor. A cold Ruthye makes it a *mutual* story — two people at different mile-markers on the same road. The code is doing double work: it gives a thirteen-year-old standing and dignity (she has a legal claim, not just feelings), and it's armor over grief — a way to process unbearable loss as obligation instead of feeling. The code also becomes the thing she has to lay down at the climax: dropping the sword means admitting the code was grief all along.

### Evelie — Ruthye's homeworld is the red-sun world
**Chose:** The cold-open homestead, the port bar, and Kara's hiding place are all one planet: Evelie, a red-sun farm world. Ruthye tracks the Brigands overland to the port, and they are still there because she stole the drive crystal / fuel core their ship needs to leave.
**Over:** Ruthye crossing interstellar space to hunt Krem before the film starts.
**Why:** A thirteen-year-old tracking raiders across the galaxy raises questions the film would have to spend scenes answering (how does she travel, eat, follow a ship between stars?). Local is simpler and stronger: her competence is plausible — she knows her own world — and the bar collision stops being luck. The Brigands are pinned in port *by her theft*, so the villain walking through the door is a consequence of her agency, not a coincidence. It also tightens the film's geography: the cut from Ruthye's rage to Kara's hangover is two lives on the same world not yet touching, and the final image — the ship crossing Evelie's fiery red sky — reclaims the same sun that lit Kara's avoidance as the ordinary light of Ruthye's home.

### No kryptonite, no Kryptonian-knowledge requirement
**Chose:** Krem doesn't know what Kara is. He's exploiting a drunk, depleted woman on a red-sun world.
**Over:** The 2026 film where Krem apparently knows Kryptonian weaknesses, uses kryptonite, and engineers countermeasures.
**Why:** If the villain conveniently knows the hero's exact weaknesses, the movie becomes gadget chess — why not just stack kryptonite + green sun + red sun? It's also deeply implausible: all Kryptonians are dead, there's no reason anyone in this part of the galaxy would know Kryptonian biology. The simpler version is scarier: Krem is a raider who walked into a bar and saw opportunity. He doesn't need to be a scientist. He needs to be cruel and prepared.

### Kara's trauma front-loaded, not mid-film reveal
**Chose:** Nightmare/voicemail sequence immediately after the cold open, before Kara meets Ruthye.
**Over:** The 2026 film revealing Kara's grief in a mid-film flashback.
**Why:** The "go home" scene needs the audience to already understand what "I have a mother and a brother" does to Kara. If the grief is hidden until act two, the first meeting plays flat — you don't know why Kara cracks. Front-loading the nightmare and the unanswered Clark voicemail means the audience flinches before Kara does. It also establishes her avoidance as a daily practice (reaching for the bottle before her eyes open), not a backstory reveal.

### Act II escalation: protected → restrained → saved → trusted
**Chose:** Four relationship states — Kara shields Ruthye; Kara restrains Ruthye by stepping into the revenge swing; Ruthye saves Kara by forcing Lobo to take them to the sun; then, at the climax, Kara trusts Ruthye to choose.
**Over:** Interchangeable fight scenes where Kara is arbitrarily weakened.
**Why:** The 2026 film's middle is a string of detachable set pieces that could be reordered without changing anything. Each of our beats changes the relationship irreversibly: Ruthye goes from protected child (stop protecting me), to restrained avenger (stop stopping me), to active rescuer (I choose to save you), to trusted agent (she chooses, Kara doesn't intervene). Fusing restrained and saved inside the Lobo sequence makes the reversal immediate and devastating — Ruthye almost kills Kara and then has to save her in the same encounter. The escalation tracks Ruthye's agency: she starts as someone things happen *to* and ends as someone who *decides*.

### Never show if Krem lives or dies
**Chose:** The camera stays with Ruthye as she walks away. Krem's fate is never revealed.
**Over:** Explicit death (by his own system, by collapse) or explicit survival (Phantom Zone, imprisonment).
**Why:** Showing his death — however it happens — makes it the catharsis. The audience gets the kill-satisfaction whether Ruthye swings the sword or not; the movie just laundered it through a mechanism. Not showing it denies that satisfaction entirely and puts the emotional weight on the choice Ruthye made, not the fate Krem received. The story was never about him. The camera's refusal to follow him is itself the point.

### Ending on Ruthye's world, not Earth — Earth in mid-credits
**Chose:** Main ending is the birthday on Ruthye's homestead. Kara's Metropolis resolution is a mid-credits stinger.
**Over:** Ending with Kara back on Earth, resolved, integrated.
**Why:** Their resolutions are at different scales. Ruthye's is communal — family, laughter, home, warmth. That's the emotional payoff the whole film has been building toward and it should be the last thing the audience *feels*. Kara's is solitary and quieter — she's not healed, she's decided to stop running. That's a different register, and putting it in mid-credits gives it its own space without undercutting the warmth of the birthday. It also separates Kara's integration into the DCU from Ruthye's personal closure.

### Lobo as a single encounter, not a companion — and Kara takes the slash
**Chose:** Lobo appears once in Act II, saves them, offers Krem to Ruthye on a platter. When Ruthye swings, Kara steps between them and gets slashed. Lobo exits disgusted by the emotion.
**Over:** (A) Lobo as a recurring companion/comic relief throughout the film. (B) Lobo changing his mind from a sad look and throwing Krem away ("catch him yourself"). (C) Lobo getting captured by Krem (the 2026 film).
**Why:** Recurring Lobo creates the "why don't they just let him handle it" problem — Main Man fights Superman to a standstill, so any scene where Krem is a threat to him is absurd. Option B has Lobo making the choice, which makes both Kara and Ruthye passengers in their own story. Option C (captured by Krem) diminishes both characters. The slash version works because: (1) it makes mercy cost blood — Rule 7 paid in full; (2) "that's my code" mirrors and inverts Ruthye's code in a single line; (3) Lobo's exit is character-motivated — emotions repel him, codes intrigue him, and whatever is happening on the ground is beneath him; (4) the yellow-sun recharge happens as survival, not choice, which lets power return without Kara having decided to re-engage yet; (5) the strongest being in the scene exits because vulnerability is the one thing he can't do. Lobo's genuine shock at Kara's sacrifice ("well, that was fucked up") is important — it tells the audience this act was extraordinary, not routine heroism.

Some more: 
- We drop Lobo in and get some action beats, maybe it's the blood-pumping superhero fighting that some audiences expect and want. But we're definitely going to have it quickly go ultra-violent (on-brand for Lobo). Played for laughs, but grisly all the same, it *should* be too much by the end of the scene. (Punches the explode guys, killing them while they beg, "don't promise me a good time," "nothing personal" quips.)
- We should get a Superman-style homage (lifting a truck or tank over his head, glorious backlighting from an explosion, which he then rips apart/uses to crush people — bloodily). Lobo shows us what a Supergirl movie would look like if she *didn't* adhere to her code. It'd be grisly. It'd suck.
- Lobo explicitly is not there as deus ex machina. The core machinery: he purifies, literally and thematically clears the table from the normal messy qualifiers and practical hedges for revenge as cold-blooded murder (there's fighting back, there's danger, etc.). Lobo delivers Krem on his knees, gift-wrapped, with his blessing. When the sword swings, it is 100% Ruthye's choice, and when Kara steps in, the intervention is 100% hers as well. Lobo functions as an *agency concentrator* for our decisive moment (the opposite of deus ex machina).
- Same logic on the back half: the sun rescue is transported by Lobo but caused by Ruthye (the ultimatum, in his one respected currency) and enabled by Kara's earlier care (the bubble-suit callback). Nobody is saved by Lobo's goodwill. But his choice, driven by his well established code-adherence also pretty much assures the win...


### Krem's begging breaks his own philosophy
**Chose:** Krem sniveling before Lobo hollows him out. By the climax, he's not the cold-open evangelist — he's tired, used up. "Just do it." Not a taunt. Not a lesson. Just done.
**Over:** Krem maintaining his philosophical menace through the climax (laughing, taunting, "take your reward, you earned this").
**Why:** The sustained-menace version keeps Krem as the active antagonist through the final beat, which means Ruthye's refusal is still framed as defeating *him* — rejecting his creed, denying his graduation. That's satisfying but it's still about Krem. The hollowed-out version is harder and truer: Ruthye looks at the man who murdered her father and sees... nothing. No monster. No philosopher. Just a tired person who stopped believing in his own cruelty the moment it was applied to him. There's nothing left to rage against, and killing him wouldn't prove or disprove anything. It would just be killing. That's the more devastating version of "choosing life over death" — not turning away from a compelling villain, but turning away from an empty one, and realizing the revenge she's been chasing was never going to fill the hole. It also completes Krem's arc as a mirror: he showed her the end of her road in the cold open, and now she sees the real end — not a regal monster, but a husk.

### "Be good" is a promise, not a philosophy — and it's held back until near-death
**Chose:** The full Argo flashback (the fight, the forced departure, "you must live," "promise me you'll be good") plays during Kara's near-death moment after the Lobo scene slash. The morning nightmare shows only fragments.
**Over:** (A) Revealing the full memory early (morning sequence or Act I). (B) Revealing it in a mid-film flashback as the 2026 film does. (C) Never showing it explicitly.
**Why:** The morning fragments plant the trauma without explaining it — the audience knows Kara is haunted but not by what, specifically. Holding back the full memory until she's dying does three things: (1) it lands at maximum emotional impact, when the audience has spent the whole film watching Kara avoid this exact feeling; (2) it reframes "be good" from a theme into a *promise to a dying mother*, which retroactively charges every act of restraint in the film; (3) the yellow-sun healing that follows becomes not just physical recovery but an answer to the promise — her body insisting on living the way her parents insisted she live. The mid-credits stinger ("it's good to be... home") then completes the arc: she's finally doing what her mother asked.

### The final slash misdirect — sword to chain, not neck
**Chose:** Ruthye raises the sword, the audience holds its breath, and she slashes the chain holding the antidote around Krem's neck. Takes the cure. Turns away.
**Over:** (A) Ruthye simply lowering the sword and walking away. (B) Kara intervening to stop the kill (as in the Lobo scene). (C) Ruthye killing Krem and then finding the antidote.
**Why:** Option A is passive — Ruthye *not* doing something is weaker than Ruthye *doing* something that isn't the kill. Option B repeats the Lobo scene and undermines the whole Act II escalation (protected → restrained → saved → trusted). Option C is the 2026 film. The slash misdirect works because: (1) it's a *choice made physical* — one gesture, sword to chain, life instead of death; (2) it weaponizes the audience's own bloodlust against them — you wanted the kill, you thought you were getting it, and the denial lands in your gut; (3) it completes Kara's arc of trust — she's standing behind Ruthye, didn't step in, didn't protect by force, and her look of relief when the blade hits chain instead of neck is the culmination of their entire relationship. The antidote around Krem's neck also means the cure and the kill are *in the same gesture* — she has to choose which target the sword hits.

### The medbay gives a countdown, so the bus becomes the road movie
**Chose:** Kara's ship is useful only as the nearest desperate option: it gets Krypto off the red-sun world, identifies poison, and gives them a countdown. Kara redlines it toward the closest yellow sun. They cannot make it. A drive component burns out at the deep-space intergalactic refuel/rest stop, and the ship still cannot cure Brigand poison. Krypto stays behind in the rest-stop medbay's liquid stabilizing tube while Kara and Ruthye take the bus after Krem.

**Over:** (A) Krem stealing Kara's ship and forcing arbitrary backtracking. (B) Krem attacking her ship for no reason when he already has his own. (C) A clean ship chase that makes the bus/rest-stop material detachable. (D) A bartender-family betrayal/redirect detour.
**Why:** This answers the practical question cleanly: Kara goes to her ship because Krypto is dying and it is the nearest thing to a hospital and a route to sunlight, not because the Brigands care about it. Krem leaves in his own ship because that is simpler and smarter. The medbay countdown and failed yellow-sun sprint leave us exactly where we need to be — Krypto still needs the antidote — and the burned-out drive component makes the bus structurally necessary instead of quirky. Leaving Krypto in the clinic also keeps the dead-world section focused on Kara and Ruthye instead of dog logistics. The mechanic's answer is almost comic in its cruelty: the part can be replaced/regrown, just not within Krypto's window, even with the tube buying a short reprieve. The rest stop lets the tracker, the public spacemap, and the travel advisory point them cleanly toward the Brigand dead world. No betrayal mechanics, no extra family, no detachable atrocity subplot — just logistics that force Kara and Ruthye into proximity while moving them toward Krem.

### Bilki, the Brigand homeworld, is the nuclear wasteland destination
**Chose:** The tracker leads to Bilki, the Brigands' dead/nuclear-winter homeworld, now used as depot, hideout, graveyard, and dare.
**Over:** A generic villain lair, second bar, redirect planet, or exposition-heavy atrocity infrastructure.
**Why:** It gives Krem's worldview a physical location without requiring a speech. He can say something as small as *"Look around you"* or *"There was no one to save us"* and the landscape does the work. It keeps him bitter, tired, and nihilistic rather than cackling or operatic. The bus/drop-shell gag also solves the surface problem with character: public transit will technically drop them there, but no sane driver will land or promise pickup. And it makes the Lobo scene's yellow-sun ascent elegant: we already saw the glint of life above the cloud cover as they fell, with Kara pressing her hand to the shell as if she could absorb enough sun to cope; later, she rises back through that same dead atmosphere into the sun. The unidentified flaming blue arc during the drop also plants Lobo's bike without explaining it — a visual breadcrumb that only resolves when the Main Man arrives.

### Bonding through proximity, not speeches
**Chose:** Three road spaces — Kara's ship under the medbay countdown, the rest stop washroom/errand sequence, and the bus transit/drinking-confession scene ("I've done what you're planning to do") — carry the relationship from uneasy alliance to something approaching trust.
**Over:** (A) A single "I know how you feel" speech from Kara. (B) A montage of fun adventures. (C) No bonding scenes — jump from First Meeting to the Lobo scene.
**Why:** Option A violates Rule 10 (persuasion is not argument) and makes Kara the wise mentor, which she isn't. Option B makes the tone wrong — this isn't a buddy comedy. Option C leaves the relationship unearned, so the Lobo scene emotional core (Ruthye caring enough to save Kara) comes from nowhere. The three-space approach lets the bond build in different registers: on the ship, Ruthye watches Kara fight panic under the countdown; at the rest stop, star maps and logistics preserve the competence lane while the washroom tear-wipe lets Kara offer comfort instead of instruction; on the bus, Kara's drinking confession costs her something by admitting she's been where Ruthye is going. None of these scenes resolve the relationship; each just shifts the ground.

### Ruthye's code ultimatum forces Lobo's hand
**Chose:** Ruthye demands Lobo take them to the sun — *"Take us or kill me now. The code demands it."* — and Lobo complies because codes are the one thing he respects.
**Over:** (A) Lobo helping out of pity or sentimentality. (B) Kara making the request. (C) Lobo deciding on his own.
**Why:** Option A violates Lobo's character entirely — he doesn't do pity. Option B makes Kara the agent at a moment that should be Ruthye's — this is beat 3, Ruthye saving Kara. Option C makes Lobo the decision-maker in someone else's story. The code ultimatum works because: (1) it weaponizes Ruthye's code against the one person who respects codes; (2) it's Ruthye's most dangerous moment of agency — she's offering her own death to the most dangerous man alive, not as desperation but as a demand; (3) it completes the code motif: Ruthye's code demanded vengeance, Kara's code demanded mercy, and now Ruthye's code demands life; (4) Lobo can't refuse without betraying the principle he just praised. He doesn't help out of kindness — he helps because a thirteen-year-old just held him to his own standard.

## Visual Language — The Universe Is the Spectacle

*We deleted the CGI beat-em-ups. The wonder has to come from somewhere. This is where.*

**North star: Evely, not cantina.** The comic's visual identity — Bilquis Evely's
teeming vistas, impossible fauna, a universe that is enormous, mostly indifferent,
and occasionally hilarious — is the target. Silver-age invention with modern craft.
The register is closer to *Rick and Morty*'s economy of weirdness than *Star Wars*'
rubber-mask realism: every background alien is a one-gag high concept (the drive
component that annoyingly *does* grow — in a vat, over days or weeks; the drop-shell rated
for "toxic atmosphere and plausible deniability"), never forty masks holding drinks.
Weirdness as invention, not set dressing. One tone check governs all of it: **the
universe is funny; the grief is not.** The comedy lives in the world, never in the
characters deflecting.

**The whimsy lives in the connective tissue.** The rest stop, the bus, and the drop
point are exactly the sections the 2026 film rushed through to reach its next fight —
and exactly where this version spends its reclaimed minutes. That's not a
coincidence; it's the delivery mechanism. The film breathes in the same scenes where
the universe gets to be strange.

**The palette is the argument.** Five movements:

1. **Dead red** — Evelie, the opening world. Flat, sodium-lamp light that makes everything look
   embalmed. Kara's avoidance rendered as color grade.
2. **Riotous life** — the rest stop and the bus. Maximum saturation, maximum
   invention, life insisting on itself in ridiculous forms. The film at its most
   alive, deliberately, right before —
3. **Gray** — Bilki, the Brigand homeworld. Every drop of color drained. Nuclear winter,
   lightning under cloud. The dead world lands *because* we just spent twenty
   minutes in a universe bursting with absurd creatures. This is what Krem's worldview
   looks like when it wins.
4. **Blinding yellow** — the rebirth. One transition, held long. The sun does what
   the sun does.
5. **Warm homestead** — the birthday. Golden-hour domesticity. And the final image
   reclaims the film's first color: Ruthye laughing as the ship crosses a *fiery red
   sky* — the same red sun that lit Kara's avoidance, now the ordinary blaze of a
   living evening over Ruthye's home. The palette forgives its own opening.

**Light behaves like a character.** The suns are the central conceit, so light gets
blocked, filtered, chased, and denied on screen: Kara's hand pressed to the
drop-shell glass, the glint of yellow on its curve before the cloud swallows it, the
red porthole in her morning quarters. The rule the audience absorbs without being
told: barriers filter the sun's gift. That's why the rebirth must happen in open
space, on a bike, unshielded — and why it can only happen once.

**The jacket is avoidance made costume.** For most of the film Kara wears a battered,
oversized leather jacket: too big, too old, a little cool until it isn't. It is armor,
blanket, disguise, hangover uniform — something to disappear inside. The dead-world
trap shreds it; the Lobo slash turns it into a ragged death shawl around her on the
bike; the yellow-sun rebirth lets it float away. She does not dramatically take it
off. It simply cannot come with her into the person she is choosing to be.

**The camera loves life and refuses to love death.** The ethic and the aesthetic
must agree (see: DCU, not DCEU), and the camera is where they meet. Creatures,
markets, food, weather, Krypto's ears in wind — shot with delight. Violence — shot
with weight. Lobo's ninety seconds are staged as horror-through-ease: no needle
drop, no slow-mo glory, just the awful frictionlessness of it. The most fun
character in the movie doing the least fun thing in it, and the frame declining to
celebrate. If a death in this film looks cool, the shot is wrong.

**Kara is the alien.** Superman's story is an alien becoming human; his palette is
Kansas. Kara was born in space, raised in a floating city, and is *comfortable* in
the weird — the bus terminal is her element, she knows which vendor to trust and
which line moves. Earth is the strange planet. Her ease in the universe's chaos is
character rendered as blocking and production design — and it's why the mid-credits
"it's good to be... home" costs something. Home is the one place in the universe
where she's the alien.

**Recurring motif: hands.** Krem's hand helping itself to pie. Kara's hand on the
drop-shell glass. Her hand on Ruthye's shoulder in the washroom. Ruthye's hands
aligning the tracker with the public spacemap, proving competence without a speech.
The sword in two hands, and the chain, not the neck. The film's thesis is about what
you choose to do with capacity; hands are capacity in close-up.

**The production trade, stated plainly.** This direction is cheaper than the
fight-heavy version in VFX shot count and more expensive per shot in design —
creature work and world-building over destruction sims. That is the *Guardians*
trade, not the *Man of Steel* trade, and it is the trade this film's studio is
already tooled for. The money is on screen either way; here it buys wonder instead
of collision.
