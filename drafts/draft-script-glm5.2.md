# SUPERGIRL: WOMAN OF TOMORROW
### A Screenplay

*Based on the DC Comics graphic novel "Supergirl: Woman of Tomorrow" by Tom King and Bilquis Evely. Story bible by the author.*

## COLD OPEN — "THIS IS WHAT THE WORLD IS"

### 1. EXT. EVELIE HOMESTEAD — ESTABLISHING — DAWN

A farmhouse. Not grand — built by hands that knew what they were doing but weren't showing off. Fences. A vegetable garden, well-tended. A pen with two draft animals, six-legged and placid, steaming in the cool morning.

The sky is a flat, sodium-lamp red. Not sunset, not sunrise. Just red. Evelie's sun sits at the horizon like a coal that won't quite go out, and everything on this world — the grass, the walls, the animals — looks embalmed under it.

A NORMAL LIFE. The kind that exists because someone chose to build it.

Through a window: warm yellow lamplight. Movement. A family inside.

### 2. INT. HOMESTEAD KITCHEN — MORNING

A table. Breakfast mostly finished. The remains of something good — bread, preserves, a protein that came from somewhere on this farm.

FATHER (40s, strong-armed, kind-faced) eats standing, already half-dressed for the field. He's the kind of man who eats standing because sitting down to eat feels like claiming something you should be grateful for.

MOTHER (40s, watchful, the spine of the household) pours something hot into a cup. She moves like someone who has thought about every object in this room once and put it in the right place.

RUTHYE (13) sits at the table. She eats slowly, deliberately. She has her father's build — compact, practical — and her mother's eyes: the ones that notice everything and give away nothing. There is something coiled in her stillness. Not trouble. Just... readiness.

YOUNGER BROTHER (5, maybe 6) is under the table, playing with something — a carved figure, a toy spaceship, the kind of thing a kid makes from scrap. He makes engine noises with his mouth. Nobody tells him to stop.

The scene is ordinary. Almost aggressively ordinary. The audience should feel the warmth of it — the weight of the mundane — because we are about to watch it get taken apart.

A SOUND outside. Hoofsteps. Or — not hoofsteps. Something heavier. Mechanical.

FATHER glances at the window.

Through the glass: figures dismounting from vehicles. Four, five. Armed. Moving with the loose, practiced ease of people who do this for a living.

The father's face changes. Not fear — not yet. Recognition. He knows what armed strangers at your door means on a world like this.

FATHER
(to his family, calm)
Stay inside.

He sets down his cup. Walks toward the door.

MOTHER
(calling after him, quiet)
Take the long gun.

He pauses. Nods. Takes it.

RUTHYE watches. She has stopped eating. Her hand has moved, unconsciously, to the hilt of a short blade on her belt — a tool, not a weapon, but she touches it the way you touch a railing on a high place.

### 3. EXT. HOMESTEAD — CONTINUOUS

FATHER steps out, the long gun held low — not threatening, but present.

KREM (40s) stands in the yard. He is not what you'd expect from a warlord. He's lean, weathered, dressed in the mixture of practical and stolen that suggests someone who has been taking what he needs for a long time. His face is the kind that might have been open once — you can see the ghost of the man he was underneath the man he chose to become. Behind him: FOUR BRIGANDS, armed, fanning out lazily around the yard.

Krem looks at the farmhouse like he's reading it. The fences, the animals, the well-kept garden. He sees a life someone built.

KREM
Nice place.

FATHER
What do you need?

Krem smiles. Not a villain's smile — something more tired. He appreciates the directness.

KREM
Supplies. Weapons, if you have them. Protein. Water cells. We'll take fuel if you've got stores.

(beat)

I'm not here to make your day worse. I'm here to make a trade. Or accept a gift. Whichever way you want to remember it.

FATHER
We don't have weapons to spare.

KREM
(gently)
You have the one in your hand. And the one on your daughter's belt. And the one I'm guessing is mounted above your kitchen door, because that's where I'd put it.

He knows the layout. He's done this before.

KREM
I'm going to come inside. My men will take what we need. You're going to let them, because the alternative is worse, and we both know it. I'd rather not do the alternative. It's a nice morning.

FATHER grips the long gun. For a moment — a real moment — the audience doesn't know if he's going to use it. He could. He's a farmer, not a soldier, but Krem is close enough.

MOTHER
(from the doorway)
Let them take what they need.

A beat. FATHER looks at her. She looks back. There is a whole conversation in that look — the kind that married people have, the kind about what matters and what doesn't.

FATHER lowers the gun. Slowly.

FATHER
Take what you need.

### 4. INT. HOMESTEAD KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS

KREM enters the house like he's been invited to dinner. Which, in a way, he has — because MOTHER, thinking faster than anyone, has put the kettle on and is setting out cups.

This is the move: normalcy as defense. If this is a transaction, let it be a transaction. If they are guests, let them be guests. The mind reaches for protocol because the alternative is screaming.

KREM sits at the table. He looks at the breakfast remains — the bread, the preserves — and helps himself to a piece of bread. He tears it, chews, nods appreciatively.

KREM
Good bread.

He eats. The BRIGANDS move through the house — opening cabinets, checking rooms, inventorying with professional efficiency. One goes upstairs. One checks the outbuildings. This is a team that has a system.

RUTHYE sits at the table. She hasn't moved. Her brother is under the table, frozen now, the toy spaceship in his hand forgotten. His eyes are on Krem's boots.

Krem notices Ruthye. He studies her for a moment — the coiled stillness, the hand near her blade. He sees something. The corner of his mouth tightens, not in anger. Recognition.

KREM
(to Ruthye)
You look like someone who has questions.

RUTHYE says nothing. Her face is a wall.

KREM
Good. Keep them. Questions are useful.

(he chews)

For a while.

### 5. INT. HOMESTEAD KITCHEN — LATER

The Brigands have gathered supplies into packs. Weapons — the family's few defenses. Protein. Water cells. A fuel canister.

KREM is still at the table. He has eaten more of the bread. He's talking. Not to anyone in particular — or maybe to the room.

KREM
There was a world. Mine. A long time ago.

(beat)

It was like this. Farms. Markets. People who built things and thought the things they built would last.

He looks out the window, at the flat red sky.

KREM
And then one day someone decided it was worth more broken than whole. And everyone around us — the traders, the neighbors, the ones who shook our hands at harvest — they decided the same thing. Or they didn't decide. They just... didn't decide otherwise. Which is the same thing.

MOTHER stands by the stove. She's listening. Her face is carefully neutral — the face of someone who knows that what comes next depends on how this man feels when he's finished talking.

FATHER stands by the door. The long gun is gone — taken by the Brigands. He is, for the first time, visibly diminished.

KREM
(continuing)
I was like you once.

(he's looking at FATHER now)

I believed in — what do you believe in? The work? The family? The idea that if you do things right, the world will let you keep them?

FATHER
(carefully)
Something like that.

KREM
Yeah.

(beat)

It's not true.

The room goes still. The only sound is the kettle, starting to whistle.

KREM
The world doesn't let you keep anything. The world takes. And the only question — the only honest question — is whether you're the one taking or the one being taken from.

MOTHER
(turning down the flame)
That's not the only question.

KREM looks at her. For a moment, something flickers — the ghost of the man he might have been, briefly visible. Then it's gone.

KREM
No. I suppose you'd say it isn't.

### 6. INT. HOMESTEAD KITCHEN — MOMENTS LATER

The off-ramp.

The Brigands are done. The supplies are packed. Krem could leave right now. He has what he came for. The family is shaken but alive. The transaction is ugly, but survivable. Everyone in the room can feel the exit — it's right there, wide open, the way out of this story with everyone breathing.

KREM stands. Buttons his coat. He looks around the kitchen one last time.

And then — a sound.

RUTHYE has spoken. Low, flat, not a question.

RUTHYE
You don't have to be this.

Silence. Even the Brigands stop.

KREM turns. Slowly. He looks at Ruthye — this thirteen-year-old girl sitting at her family's table, looking at him with the absolute conviction that the world is fair and this is an aberration and he could, right now, choose to be something else.

He sees it. The defiance. The pride. The thing in her that still believes.

And something in Krem — the thing that chose predation and kept choosing it, the thing that cannot tolerate being shown the person he used to be — refuses to let it stand.

KREM
(quietly)
Yes. I do.

He crosses to the mother. Backhands her across the face. Hard. Not to injure — to test. To see what happens.

FATHER moves. Instantly, instinctively — he's across the room, hands reaching for Krem. But two BRIGANDS are already on him, pinning his arms. They've done this before. They were waiting for it.

MOTHER is on the floor, hand to her face. The YOUNGER BROTHER screams — a high, thin sound.

RUTHYE is on her feet. The blade is in her hand — she drew it without thinking. She's shaking, but her grip is correct. She looks like she might actually use it.

KREM looks at her. Holds up a hand — to his men, to the room. *Wait.*

He walks to Ruthye. Slowly. He doesn't reach for the blade. He doesn't need to. He gets close enough that she could cut him, and they both know she won't, because his men have her father and the math doesn't work.

KREM
(softly, to Ruthye)
Put it away.

She doesn't.

KREM
Put it away, and your father lives. Swing it, and he doesn't. That's not a threat. That's arithmetic.

A long beat. Ruthye's eyes burn. But the math is the math.

She lowers the blade.

KREM
(nods)
Good.

He turns. Walks toward the father. The Brigands hold him — arms pinned, facing Ruthye.

The off-ramp is still there. The door is still open. Krem could walk through it right now.

But he doesn't.

He looks at Ruthye. One last time. Eye contact. He wants her to see this.

KREM
This is what the world is.

He draws a sidearm — a heavy, ugly thing — and shoots the father in the chest.

The sound is enormous in the kitchen. The mother screams. The brother screams. Ruthye —

Ruthye doesn't scream. She makes a sound — something between a gasp and a shout, the sound of something breaking inside her that won't be put back — but she doesn't scream. She catches her father as the Brigands release him and he falls. She gets her arms under him. She lowers him to the floor.

He's still alive. Barely. His eyes find Ruthye's face. His mouth works — he's trying to say something. Maybe her name. Maybe her mother's name. Maybe nothing. His hand finds hers and grips, hard, and then —

He's gone.

KREM watches this. He's not smiling. He's not savoring it. He looks tired. Like a man who has done something he believes in, and is weary from the conviction.

KREM
(to Ruthye, almost gently)
Now you know.

He turns and walks out. His men follow. The screen door bangs shut behind them.

The vehicles start. The sound fades.

### 7. INT. HOMESTEAD KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS — SILENCE

MOTHER is on the floor beside her husband. Her hands are on his chest, pressing, as if pressure could undo what a bullet did. She's not screaming anymore. She's not making any sound at all. Her face is a thing the audience will remember: grief so total it hasn't found its shape yet.

The YOUNGER BROTHER is under the table. He saw. He saw all of it. He's curled into a ball, hands over his ears, keening — that high, lost sound a child makes when something has happened that their mind can't hold.

RUTHYE is kneeling. Her father's blood is on her hands, her clothes. The blade is still in her hand — she never put it away, not really, just lowered it. Her eyes are on the door Krem walked through.

She isn't crying. She isn't in shock. She is somewhere else entirely — a place the audience hasn't been yet, and won't fully understand until the end of the film.

CLOSE ON RUTHYE'S FACE. Not grief. Not shock.

*Rage.* The kind that has already decided something, before the mind catches up.

She pulls her brother to her. Wraps her arms around him. He huddles into her, shaking — he knows, even if he hasn't understood. She holds him, and her arms are a cage of protection around the life she should choose, and her eyes are fixed on the road Krem took.

The quest begins here. Not when she meets Kara. Not when she picks up a sword. *Here* — in this image: a thirteen-year-old girl holding the life she should choose, staring at the road she will.

## KARA'S MORNING — THE NIGHTMARE AND THE VOICEMAIL

### 8. INT. KARA'S SHIP — SLEEPING QUARTERS — DAWN

SILENCE.

Then — a sound. Not a scream. A gasp. The sound someone makes when they surface from a dream that was drowning them.

KARA ZOR-EL wakes up. She's on her ship — a small, well-used vessel, lived-in the way a coat gets lived-in. The sleeping quarters are cramped but not sloppy. There are signs of someone who once kept things in order and has mostly stopped.

Kara is — what? She looks early twenties. The leather jacket she'll wear through most of the film is draped over a chair, too big, battered. She's in an undershirt. She looks like she slept in her clothes, which she did, which she usually does.

Her hand goes to the bottle before her eyes are fully open. An automatic, practiced motion — the reach of someone who has done this every morning for a long time. The bottle is on the floor beside the bed. She finds it. Drinks. Sets it down.

*That* tells you everything about how long this has been going on.

### 9. INT. KARA'S SHIP — SLEEPING QUARTERS — FLASHBACK FRAGMENTS

The nightmare comes back in fragments — not a full memory, just the images that won't let go.

— A city in the sky. Argo. Towers of crystal and light, impossibly beautiful, impossibly doomed. The sky is wrong — a crack in it, a fracture, something fundamental breaking.

— GROUND SHAKING. Days of it. The tremor that doesn't stop. Things falling off shelves. The sound of crystal splitting.

— A face. A woman. Her mother. Young, beautiful, frightened but trying not to show it. Saying something we can't quite hear.

— A pod. Small. Child-sized. The door open.

— HANDS pushing her. Pulling her. A man's voice — her father — *you have to go.*

— The face again. Closer now. Her mother's lips moving. Three words we almost hear, almost — and then the pod door closes and —

### 10. INT. KARA'S SHIP — SLEEPING QUARTERS — PRESENT

KARA sits up. Breathing hard. The fragments recede.

She's on Evelie. The same world as Ruthye. The red sun sits at the horizon, visible through a porthole, painting everything in that flat, sodium-lamp light. Two lives on the same world, not yet touching — Ruthye's rage and Kara's hangover, neighbors in the same dead light.

On a device beside the bed, a notification pulses. A voicemail. She reaches for it — her hand passes the bottle again, and for a moment it's unclear which one she'll pick up.

She picks up the device. Taps the message.

CLARK KENT'S VOICE (V.O.)
(warm, concerned, trying not to sound concerned)
Hey. It's me. Again.

(beat)

Happy birthday, by the way. I know I said it in the last one, but — I wanted to say it again. Twenty-one. That's — that's a real one, Kara. That's the one where — 

(he stops, tries a different approach)

I'm not — I'm not going to ask you to call back. I just wanted to hear your voice. Or — I guess hear you're — I don't know. Kara, it's been a while. And I worry. That's — that's my thing, I worry. You know that.

(beat)

The farm is good. Ma is — she's good. She asks about you. I don't know what to tell her.

(a longer pause)

You don't have to come here. You don't have to be anywhere. I just — if you could tell me you're okay. That's all. Just — tell me you're okay.

(beat)

Okay. I love you. Call me.

The message ends. The device goes dark.

KARA stares at it. Her face does something complicated — a flicker of the person she is underneath the numbing. Then it's gone. She sets the device down.

She picks up the bottle. Drinks.

She stands, moves to the porthole. Looks out at Evelie's red sun — that coal that won't quite go out.

Through the porthole, we can see: the port town in the distance. Ships at dock. A market, small, functional. And beyond the town, the fields and farms of a world that is, to everyone but Kara, a home.

She has a way off this world. She has family — Clark, the Kents, a whole life on Earth — who love her and want her back. She's choosing the bottle and the dead light of a star that makes her feel nothing.

*Avoidance isn't something that happened to her. It's a practice she maintains every morning.*

Behind her, in the ship's main compartment: KRYPTO. A white dog — not quite a dog, not quite a wolf, something in between, the last living thing from a dead world. He's old, he's loyal, and he is the only thing in Kara's life she hasn't pushed away. He lifts his head from his paws, watches her. Thumps his tail once.

KARA
(without turning)
Yeah. I know.

She takes another drink.

HOLD ON THE RED SUN through the porthole. The same sun that lights Ruthye's world. The same dead coal. And somewhere under it, a girl is sharpening a blade.

## FIRST MEETING — "GO HOME"

### 11. EXT. EVELIE PORT TOWN — STREET — DUSK

The port town. Not a city — a town that exists because ships land here and people need to eat and drink and forget. The buildings are low, functional, made from the same reddish stone as the soil. The red sun sits lower now, painting everything in deeper amber. The light is tired.

KARA walks down the main street. The leather jacket over her shoulders, hands in pockets, moving with the loose, slightly unsteady gait of someone who's been drinking since morning and has achieved the functional numbness that gets you through the day. She knows this town. She's been here for weeks — maybe months. Time blurs under a red sun.

Krypto trots beside her. He's the one thing that gets her out of the ship. He needs to walk. She needs to drink. The town has a bar. The math works.

### 12. INT. PORT BAR — "THE BROKEN COMPASS" — CONTINUOUS

The bar. Not a cantina — a *bar*. Farmhands, dock workers, a few off-world crew killing time between hauls. The usual population of a port town at the end of a workday. Fluorescent lighting buzzes overhead. Someone's got a screen showing a game. The air is close with smoke and the smell of something fried.

KARA enters, moves to the bar, takes a stool. The bartender — a heavy woman who has seen everything and judged most of it — pours without being asked. Kara's a regular.

KRYPTO settles under Kara's stool, chin on his paws. He's been here before too.

At a table near the door, three CREWMEN — off-world haulers, rough-looking — are playing cards and drinking. One of them looks up as the door opens again.

### 13. INT. PORT BAR — CONTINUOUS — RUTHYE'S ARRIVAL

The door opens. RUTHYE walks in.

She's been traveling. Overland, alone, from her family's homestead to this port. She's dusty, tired, and absolutely, terrifyingly composed. She carries a sword on her back — not ceremonial, functional. The blade her father taught her with. She scans the room with the eyes of someone who has been looking for one thing for a long time.

She crosses to the bar. Sits — one stool between her and Kara.

KARA glances at her. A kid. In a bar. In a port town. Alone. Kara's instinct flickers — the old instinct, the one that used to care — and she files it away. Not her problem. She takes a drink.

The BARTENDER looks at Ruthye.

BARTENDER
We don't do food for — 

RUTHYE
I'm not here for food.

(beat)

I'm looking for someone. A man. Travels with a crew. They have a ship.

BARTENDER
(wiping a glass)
This is a port. Lots of ships.

RUTHYE
This one's been here longer than the others. Because they can't leave.

The bartender's hand slows on the glass. She's starting to understand who she's talking to.

One of the CREWMEN at the card table — the one who looked up — has been watching. He stands. He's big, bored, and the kind of stupid that sees a kid alone and thinks *easy mark*. He crosses to the bar, leans into Ruthye's space.

CREWMAN
Hey, kid. You lost?

RUTHYE
(doesn't look at him)
No.

CREWMAN
This isn't a place for — 

RUTHYE
I said no.

CREWMAN
(grins, reaches for her shoulder)
Come on, let's get you —

Ruthye moves.

### 14. INT. PORT BAR — RUTHYE'S BRAWL

It happens fast. Not movie-martial-arts fast. *Practical* fast. The kind of fast that comes from a girl who's been taught by a father who believed she should know how to handle herself.

The crewman reaches. Ruthye catches the hand — pivots — uses his weight. He goes into the bar face-first. His nose makes a sound. His friends are already standing.

Ruthye has the crewman's arm locked behind his back. She's not even breathing hard. Her face is stone.

RUTHYE
(to the crewman, calm)
I'm not lost. I'm not scared. And I'm not interested in whatever you were about to do. So sit down, or I'll put you down.

She releases him — a shove, not a throw. He stumbles back. His two friends are on their feet, but they're hesitating now. They saw the move. They're doing math.

The crewman wipes his nose. Blood. He looks at his friends. They look at each other. The math doesn't work. They sit down.

THE BAR has gone quiet. Everyone saw. A thirteen-year-old girl just won a barfight. The room recalculates.

KARA saw. She's been watching over the rim of her glass. Her expression has changed — not much, but enough. Interest. Or recognition.

Ruthye turns back to the bar. Smooths her hair. Looks at the bartender.

RUTHYE
The ship I'm looking for. Brigand crew. Their leader has a mark on his neck — old burn, in the shape of —

She traces a shape on the bar. The bartender's face tightens.

BARTENDER
I know who you mean.

RUTHYE
Are they still here?

BARTENDER
(slowly)
Camped at the east dock. They've been having — engine trouble. 

RUTHYE
(nods)
I know. That's why they're still here.

Ruthye reaches into her pack and produces a few coins — carefully counted, not a lot.

RUTHYE
Water. And whatever information costs.

### 15. INT. PORT BAR — KARA AND RUTHYE — CONTINUOUS

KARA slides onto the stool next to Ruthye. She's interested despite herself — or maybe she recognizes something.

KARA
That was clean.

Ruthye looks at her. Sizes her up in one glance: the leather jacket, the smell of alcohol, the slightly unfocused eyes. Dismisses her.

RUTHYE
Thanks.

KARA
Where'd you learn that?

RUTHYE
My father.

A beat. The word *father* sits between them. Kara doesn't know yet what it means in this context.

KARA
You're a long way from a farm to be looking for Brigands.

RUTHYE
(eyes narrowing)
How do you know I'm from a farm?

KARA
(gestures vaguely)
Dirt. Under your boots. Red. That's the eastern agricultural belt. And you've got the tan of someone who works outside but not at a port.

Ruthye recalculate. There's more to this drunk than she assumed.

RUTHYE
You're observant for —

KARA
(smiles, a bit bitter)
For a drunk? Yeah. It's one of the few skills that survives the bottle.

A beat. The bartender sets water in front of Ruthye. Ruthye drinks — she's thirsty, she's been traveling, but she drinks with control.

KARA
What's your name?

RUTHYE
Ruthye.

KARA
Kara.

(beat)

You want to tell me why a thirteen-year-old girl from the farm belt is alone in a port bar hunting Brigands?

RUTHYE
(a long pause, then)
They came to my home. My family's farm. They took supplies. Weapons.

(beat)

The leader — Krem — he killed my father.

Kara goes still. The glass in her hand — she sets it down.

KARA
(quiet)
I'm sorry.

RUTHYE
(level)
Don't be. I'm going to kill him.

The words are delivered without heat. A statement of intent. As factual as the dirt on her boots.

KARA
(re: the code)
And that's — what, your right?

RUTHYE
By the code of my people, yes. A father's murder demands blood. It falls to the eldest child to deliver it. The code is — 

KARA
The code. Your people's code.

RUTHYE
(challenging)
You've heard of it?

KARA
I've heard of a lot of codes.

Ruthye looks at her. There's a challenge in it — *don't mock me.*

KARA
(sighs, signals for another drink)
You have family? Besides your father?

RUTHYE
(carefully)
My mother. My brother. They're alive.

KARA
They know you're here?

RUTHYE
No.

KARA
You left them.

RUTHYE
The code demands —

KARA
(quiet, not arguing)
The code can demand whatever it wants. You left them.

A long beat. Ruthye doesn't answer. The truth of it sits there.

KARA looks at her. And something shifts in Kara's face — the kind of crack that alcohol lets through. Because she's looking at a girl who has everything Kara lost, and that girl is *leaving it behind*.

KARA
You have a mother.

RUTHYE
Yes.

KARA
And a brother.

RUTHYE
Yes.

KARA
(voice thinning)
And they're alive. And they're waiting for you.

RUTHYE
(after a beat)
Yes.

KARA
(roughly)
Go home.

RUTHYE
What?

KARA
You heard me. Go home. Your mother needs you. Your brother needs you. Whatever — whatever code you're citing, whatever this is —

(she gestures at Ruthye, at the bar, at the sword)

This isn't where you belong. You belong on that farm. With them. Go home.

RUTHYE
(cold)
You don't know me. You don't know what he did.

KARA
I know what it costs.

RUTHYE
You don't know —

KARA
(sharper)
I know what it *costs*. 

A beat. Kara's voice has cracked. She pulls it back. Drinks.

KARA
(quieter)
Look. I'm — I'm the last person who should be telling anyone anything. But you have something. Don't throw it away on — on this.

Ruthye studies her. The drunk telling her to go home is not what she expected.

RUTHYE
I can't.

KARA
You can. You just walk —

RUTHYE
*No.* I can't. The code is — it's not a choice. It's a law. My people's law. My father's law.

(beat, hard)

He died because of Krem. I *saw* it. You don't walk away from that.

KARA
(bitter)
You'd be surprised what you can walk away from.

Silence between them. The bar noise fills back in around them.

RUTHYE
(measured)
You can tell me to go home. But I'm not going to. So either help me, or don't. But don't waste my time.

### 16. INT. PORT BAR — THE TRACKER

Ruthye reaches into her pack and produces two objects. She sets them on the bar.

The first: a BASEBALL-SIZED METAL-AND-GLASS CASE, grungy, battered. Inside it, something GLOWS — a crystal, pulsing with a faint, arterial light. This is a DRIVE CRYSTAL — a ship's fuel core. The heart of an engine.

KARA
(impressed despite herself)
Is that —

RUTHYE
Their drive crystal. I took it from their camp the night after they — after Krem —

(she steadies)

They aren't leaving without this. They've been stuck in this port because I took their fuel core. They're *here* because of me.

Kara looks at the crystal. Then at Ruthye. This thirteen-year-old girl snuck into a Brigand camp and stole their ship's heart the night after they murdered her father.

KARA
(slowly)
You stole their —

RUTHYE
Yes.

KARA
And you've been waiting for —

RUTHYE
For someone to help me use it. 

(beat)

The code demands blood. I intend to deliver. But Krem has men, and I have — 

(she gestures)

This sword. And this.

She taps the second object: a small, flat TRACKER — a device with a faint, directional display. It's dark right now.

RUTHYE

(a long, tired exhale)
It drops you off.

How do we get picked up?

ATTENDANT
(doesn't look up)
You don't.

We don't.

ATTENDANT

Kara looks at Ruthye. Ruthye looks at Kara.

KARA
(to Ruthye, quietly)
Your tracker. Is he there?

(nods)
The signal is consistent. He's on that world.

Non-refundable.

(dry)
Gee, thanks.

### 34. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — MAIN CONCOURSE — LATER

They have forty minutes. They need supplies.

The mundane logistics of a road trip through space. Kara knows how this works. Ruthye doesn't.

KARA
(walking)

(looks at Ruthye)

Does that sword work in cold?

Right. Okay. So: food, water, masks.

They move through the concourse. Ruthye is still absorbing the wonder of it all — the creatures, the smells, the sheer overwhelming *variety* of life — but she doesn't show it the way a kid would. She shows it the way Ruthye does: by going very still at every stall, by touching something briefly before moving on, by looking just a beat too long.

### 35. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — FOOD STALL — CONTINUOUS

At a food stall, Ruthye's eyes linger on something: a fried pastry, some kind of local street food, golden and steaming. She doesn't ask for it. She doesn't even point. But she looks at it the way someone looks at something they want and have decided they can't have.

Kara buys two. Hands one to Ruthye.

RUTHYE
(surprised)
I didn't —

KARA
I know.

They eat. Ruthye's expression when she bites into it — she tries to suppress it, but the corner of her mouth turns up. Just a fraction. It's the first time she's looked like a kid since the cold open.

### 36. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — VENDOR STALL — CONTINUOUS

At another stall, Kara is picking up thermal masks. She sees something on a rack — small, silly, practical. A SELF-DEPLOYING BUBBLE SPACE-SUIT RING. A little device you wear on your finger; tap it and a personal environmental bubble snaps around you. Rated for vacuum, thin atmosphere, and brief toxin exposure.

Kara picks one up. Tosses it to Ruthye.

RUTHYE
(catches it, examines it)
What is it?

KARA
Self-deploying bubble suit. Tap it, it snaps open. You won't die in space.

RUTHYE
(deadpan)
I wasn't planning to die in space.

KARA
Now you won't. Bonus.

Ruthye looks at it. Puts it on her finger. It sits there — a small, silly, lifesaving thing. A callback planted so deep the audience won't remember it until it matters.

### 37. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — WASHROOM — CONTINUOUS

The washroom. communal, lit by the same fluorescent buzz as everything else. Long mirrors over a metal basin.

Kara and Ruthye stand side by side, cleaning up. Washing hands, faces.

Kara looks at herself in the mirror. She looks wrecked. She looks at the wreck and doesn't recognize it, or does and wishes she didn't.

Beside her, Ruthye washes her face. When she looks up, dripping — she looks smaller than she has all movie. The competence drops for a moment, and underneath it is a thirteen-year-old girl. A girl who misses her mother. A girl who is tired and scared and on a bus to a dead world with a drunk woman she just met, chasing the man who killed her father.

Ruthye's face crumples. Just for a second. A tear leaks out.

Kara sees it. In the mirror. And Kara — who has been angry, and brittle, and drunk, and focused — does something gentle.

She puts her hand on Ruthye's shoulder.

No words. No platitudes. Just her hand, steady on the girl's shoulder. And she holds it there.

Ruthye's face steadies. She reaches up, wipes the tear away. Takes a breath. And the mask goes back on — the cold, serious, composed Ruthye.

She walks out, composed.

Kara follows, decidedly less composed.

### 38. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — BAY 7 — CONTINUOUS

Bay 7. The BUS.

The bus is — a bus. An intergalactic transit shuttle, functional, unglamorous, shaped like something between a caterpillar and a train car. Painted in faded transit-authority colors. Porthole windows. Seats that look like they were designed by a committee that didn't care.

Passengers are boarding. Funky aliens. The weird and mundane, side by side: a businesswoman with four arms and a briefcase; a family of slug-like creatures with luggage; an old man reading a paper; something that might be a robot or might be a very still person.

Ruthye is looking at the bus the way you look at the thing that's about to take you somewhere you can't come back from.

KARA
(after a beat)
You ready?

RUTHYE
(quiet)
No.

(beat)

Let's go.

They board.

### 39. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — CONTINUOUS

Inside: rows of seats, a ceiling-mounted screen showing the route, the hum of the drive. Seating is mixed — some pods, some benches. Kara and Ruthye find a pair of seats near the back.

Ruthye settles in. She's tense. She's never done this. The closest she's come is the trip from the farm to the port — and that was on Evelie, on the ground, in a vehicle she understood. This is different. This is space.

Kara straps in. Looks at Ruthye. Something flickers on her face — the ghost of a person who used to be someone, who used to take care of people.

KARA
Hey.

RUTHYE
(looks at her)

KARA
This is going to be okay.

She doesn't believe it. Ruthye doesn't believe it either. But it's the first kind thing Kara has said to her.

The bus doors close. The drive hums. The bus lifts off.

### 40. EXT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — CONTINUOUS

The bus pulls away from the rest stop, swings into a transit lane, and accelerates. It shrinks to a point of light among many points of light.

HOLD ON the rest stop — the glowing, absurd, teeming structure in the black. Life insisting on itself.

PAN to a BULLETIN BOARD near Bay 7 — one of the public boards where bounties and wanted posters are pinned. The bus shrinks behind it.

On the board: faces. Lots of faces. Wanted: dead or alive. Rewards. One of the faces is KREM. The poster is faded, pinned over older posters, forgotten.

A HAND enters frame. A LARGE HAND. White. Grabs Krem's poster. RIPS IT OFF THE BOARD. Behind the poster, a chain — heavy, metallic — swings briefly into frame.

The hand crumples the paper. Drops it. We hear the sound of footsteps moving away — heavy, confident, the walk of someone who does not hurry.

We don't see who it belongs to.

## THE ROAD, PART TWO — "I'VE DONE WHAT YOU'RE PLANNING"

### 41. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — IN TRANSIT — LATER

Time has passed. Hours. The bus hums through transit — the strange, smooth motion of a ship in a jump-lane, stars smearing in the portholes. The cabin is dimmed — the fluorescent buzz lowered to a night-register. Passengers sleep, read, eat. The old man reads his paper. The slug-family is a pile of resting slugs. The businesswoman with four arms is typing on four devices.

Kara is asleep. Her head against the window, mouth slightly open, the leather jacket pulled around her. She twitches. She's dreaming.

RUTHYE is awake. She watches Kara — the twitching, the furrowed brow. She can tell the dream isn't good.

### 42. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — FLASHBACK FRAGMENTS — KARA'S DREAM

Brief. Shards. Argo.

— The crack in the sky. Wider now. Things falling.

— A voice: *You have to go. You have to go NOW.*

— A hand on her face. Her mother's hand. Saying something she can't —

— The pod door. Closing. The last sliver of her parents' faces through the narrowing gap.

— Silence.

### 43. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — CONTINUOUS

Kara GASPS awake. Sits up. Eyes wide, breathing fast.

The bus hums. Passengers sleep. Ruthye watches.

Kara orients. She's here. She's on the bus. She's fine. She's not fine.

Her hand goes to her jacket pocket. Pulls out a FLASK. Not the bottle from the ship — a flask, personal, portable. The gesture is automatic: the practiced reach for the bottle, the thing she does to stop feeling.

She unscrews the cap. Raises it.

RUTHYE
(quietly)
You should stop that.

Kara freezes. The flask at her lips.

Ruthye is looking at her. Not judging. Not pleading. Just — stating. The way a doctor says "that's going to get infected." The way a friend says "you've had enough." Matter-of-fact. A controlled demand dressed as an observation.

RUTHYE
You should stop that.

A long beat. The bus hums. Kara and Ruthye look at each other.

Kara lowers the flask. Slowly. Screws the cap back on. She doesn't put it away — she holds it in her lap. But she stopped.

KARA
(rough)
Yeah.

(beat)

Thanks.

Silence. The bus hums. The stars smear in the portholes.

Kara puts the flask back in her pocket. She stares at the seat in front of her. And then — because the silence is too much, because the dream is still in her, because this girl just did something no one has done in a long time (told her to stop, and meant it, and Kara listened) — she starts talking.

### 44. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — KARA'S CONFESSION

KARA
(not looking at Ruthye)
There was a world.

(beat)

Ar—

(she stops, starts again)

I had a world. It was — it was beautiful. It was — it doesn't matter what it was. It was my home.

Ruthye listens. She doesn't interrupt. She doesn't move.

KARA
It ended. Not — not fast. Not like — people think it was fast. They think, you know, a planet explodes, and it's over. It wasn't like that. It was slow.

(she's speaking to the seat back, but Ruthye is hearing every word)

The sky went wrong first. We didn't know why. The light changed. And then the ground started shaking. Days. Weeks. At first you'd feel it and you'd think — oh, it's a tremor. And then it didn't stop, and after a while you stopped noticing it, because you can get used to anything. You can get used to the ground being wrong.

(beat)

People started dying. Not all at once. Ones and twos. Buildings would come down. A bridge. A school. You'd hear about someone you knew — not close, just someone, the woman who sold you bread, the man who fixed the gutters. And you'd think — oh. And then you'd go home and try to have dinner.

Ruthye's face is still. She's hearing her own story in different words. The farm. The dinner. Krem.

KARA
And then it got faster. The dying. The shaking. The cracks in the — 

(she gestures, at nothing, the memory of a sky)

— the sky got wider. And the sound — the sound was the worst. Not a sound, exactly. A feeling. Like the world was — grinding. Like something at the center was trying to get out.

(beat)

My parents.

(she stops)

They — they knew. Before I did. They knew it was over.

Ruthye is barely breathing.

KARA
They put me in a pod. A little one. One person. And they —

(her voice cracks)

They stayed.

Silence. The bus hums.

KARA
I didn't want to go. I fought them. I screamed. I was — 

(she laughs, a broken laugh)

— I was so angry. I said terrible things. Because I was — I thought they were giving up. I thought they were choosing to die and making me leave. I didn't understand.

RUTHYE
(very quietly)
What did you understand?

KARA
(beat)
Later. Much later. I understood that they were saving me. That the only thing they could do — the only thing left — was send me somewhere I could live. And everything else — their world, their lives, their — 

(she stops)

Everything else was already gone. They were just waiting for it to finish.

She puts her hands in her lap. The flask is there. She looks at it.

KARA
I promised my mother I'd —

(she stops, swallows)

I promised her something. And then I spent — 

(she gestures, at the bus, at herself, at everything)

— a long time not keeping that promise.

The bus hums. The businesswoman types. The slug-family snores softly.

Ruthye looks at Kara. She doesn't compare grief. She doesn't say "I know how you feel." She just — recognizes. The way Kara listened to the code speech in the bar, recognizing something without trying to fix it.

A long beat. And then Kara, quiet, her voice almost gone:

KARA
I've been down your road, Ruthye.

RUTHYE
(beat)
What do you mean?

KARA
Revenge. Anger. The thing where you're so sure that if you just — if you can just make someone pay, it'll be okay. That the hole will close.

(beat)

It doesn't.

RUTHYE
(quiet)
You've —

KARA
(cuts her off, not harshly)
I'm not going to tell you about it. I'm not going to give you the details. Because that's — that's mine. And it doesn't matter.

(beat)

What matters is: I've been where you are. Exactly where you are. And I'm telling you — 

(she looks at Ruthye, finally)

— it doesn't work. It won't make you feel better. It'll make you feel less, which is different, and worse.

Ruthye looks at her. Something shifts — the faintest tremor. Not agreement, not concession. Just — the recognition that Kara isn't speaking from theory. She's speaking from the other side of the thing Ruthye is about to do.

Ruthye doesn't respond. The confession just sits between them.

### 45. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — CONTINUOUS — SILENCE

The bus hums. The stars smear. The passengers sleep.

Kara looks down. She has pulled the flask out again — unconsciously, without thinking, the reflex still working even after the words she just said. She looks at it. Long and hard.

She puts it away.

Ruthye watches this. She doesn't comment. But the audience sees: Kara heard herself. And for a moment — just a moment — she listened to her own advice.

The bus hums. The night passes.

### 46. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — MORNING (OR WHATEVER PASSES FOR IT)

Ruthye is looking out the porthole. Outside: SPACE. But not the flat, empty black of the transit lane. They've arrived somewhere.

OUTSIDE THE PORTHOLE: NEBULAS. Vast, luminous, impossible clouds of gas — pinks and blues and golds and deep reds, light years wide, glowing with the light of stars being born inside them. Stars — thousands, millions, scattered like salt on black velvet. And between them, the cold emptiness — the dark that goes forever.

It's beautiful. It's terrifying. It's enormous, and the bus is tiny, and Ruthye has never seen anything like it.

She presses her hand to the porthole. The glass is cold. Her reflection looks back at her — superimposed over the nebula, a small face in a vast light.

Kara stirs. Opens her eyes. Sees Ruthye at the porthole. Sees what Ruthye is seeing.

KARA
(quietly)
First time seeing one?

RUTHYE
(whisper)
What is it?

KARA
Nursery. 

(beat)

Where stars come from.

Ruthye stares. The nebula turns, slowly, as the bus moves past it. Colors shift. The light moves across Ruthye's face.

KARA
(continuing, watching Ruthye more than the nebula)
The universe is mostly empty. And cold. And doesn't care.

(beat)

But sometimes it does that.

The nebula glows. The bus moves. Ruthye watches, her face unreadable — not the coldness of the code, but the stillness of someone seeing their own smallness for the first time.

### 47. INT. BUS — INTERIOR — APPROACHING DROPPPOINT — LATER

The PA SYSTEM chimes. The ATTENDANT'S voice — a bored, professional drawl.

ATTENDANT (V.O.)
Attention passengers. Approaching Bilki orbital drop-point. All passengers for Bilki, prepare for disembarkation at the orbital shell. Bilki is currently under a Class-7 environmental advisory. The transit authority does not recommend surface travel. The transit authority is not responsible for — 

The announcement continues, cycling through legal disclaimers. Kara and Ruthye gather their things.

The bus decelerates. Through the portholes: a PLANET. Gray. Completely, utterly gray. The atmosphere is a uniform blanket of cloud — dark, heavy, occasionally lit from within by LIGHTNING. Patches of dull orange glow mark fires that have been burning since before anyone can remember, eating the dead surface underneath.

Bilki. The Brigand homeworld. A world that died.

Kara stares at it. For her, this isn't just a planet — it's Argo, scaled up. A whole world at the end. Her face goes tight.

RUTHYE
(checking the tracker)
He's there.

The tracker's pulse is steady, pointing down.

### 48. INT. BUS — CARGO BAY — THE DROP SHELL

The bus has descended into low orbit. Below: Bilki's dead clouds. Above: the black.

In the cargo bay, the DROP SHELL waits. It's a clear bubble — half escape pod, half luggage chute. Bolted to the underside of the bus. Rated for toxic atmosphere and plausible deniability. It has exactly enough room for two people, standing, if they don't mind each other.

The DRIVER — an alien who has done this route too many times and is just waiting for retirement — stands by the controls.

KARA
(re: the shell)
We get in that?

DRIVER
You get in that.

KARA
And it drops us —

DRIVER
To the surface. 

KARA
How do we get picked up?

DRIVER
(shrugs)
Return trip. Tomorrow. Same time. You get in the shell on the ground, signal the bus, bus picks you up.

(beat)

If you're at the shell.

KARA
If we're at the shell.

DRIVER
(deadpan)
If the shell is there.

KARA
Is there a reason the shell wouldn't be there?

DRIVER
(moving to the controls)
Lots of reasons.

(beat)

You don't want to go down there.

RUTHYE
(firm)
We do.

DRIVER
(shrugs, hits the door)
Your funeral.

Kara and Ruthye step into the shell. It's cramped. The clear bubble closes around them. Inside: two harnesses, a manual release, and nothing else.

### 49. INT./EXT. DROP SHELL — THE DESCENT

The shell detaches from the bus with a CLUNK. The bus swings away, upward, gone. The shell hangs for a moment — suspended in the black above Bilki's dead atmosphere.

And then gravity finds them, and the shell falls.

For one clean second as the shell drops, the YELLOW SUN of this system — distant, blocked, but present — catches on the shell's curved surface. A tiny, impossible glint of yellow light, there and gone, a fragment of the sun they need and can't reach.

Kara sees it. She presses her hand against the clear shell. Not because it will work — a shell of glass won't feed her solar energy — but because she needs it to. A reflexive, almost childish gesture. The gesture of someone reaching for the one thing that could fix everything, through a barrier that won't let it through.

Ruthye sees Kara's hand on the glass. She sees the yearning. She doesn't comment. She files it away.

The shell enters the atmosphere. The clear surface darkens with cloud. The yellow glint vanishes. Turbulence. The shell shakes, rattles, drops. Lightning flashes outside — close, enormous, turning the cloud into a brief, strobing white.

And through a break in the clouds: the surface. Gray. Ruined. Structures — old ones, broken — barely visible in the murk. Fires burning in the distance. The bones of a civilization.

And — faint, far, below — a BLUE FLAME ARK. Something streaking toward the surface, faster than the shell, leaving a trail of blue fire in the dead air. It's moving toward the same coordinates the tracker points to.

Kara and Ruthye see it. They look at each other.

RUTHYE
What was that?

KARA
(doesn't know)
I don't know.

The blue flame disappears into the murk below. The shell follows.

### 50. EXT. BILKI SURFACE — THE DROP

The shell lands. Not gently — a hard, jarring impact on broken ground. The clear bubble cracks but holds. Dust and debris settle.

Kara opens the shell. The atmosphere is thick, acrid, warm in the wrong way — the heat of a world that burned and never quite stopped smoldering. The air tastes like ash and metal and old death.

Ruthye pulls a mask on. Kara does too. They step out.

BILKI. The Brigand homeworld. A nuclear wasteland.

Gray. Gray ground, gray sky, gray structures. The architecture is visible — Brutalist, industrial, functional. Depots, bunkers, transit spines, warehouses. The bones of a civilization that learned to move supplies and weapons after it stopped knowing how to make a home. Everything is cracked, scorched, abandoned. Vegetation: none. Water: none. Life: none that we can see.

The only motion: wind. Lightning in the clouds. And, in the distance, the dull orange glow of fires that have been burning for decades.

Ruthye looks at this world. Her face is hard. This is where Krem came from. This is what made him.

Kara looks at this world. Her face is — somewhere else. She's seeing Argo. She's seeing the end of a world. Her jaw is tight. Her hands are shaking. The medbay countdown is in her head. Krypto is dying. She's on a dead world under a dead sky and the air is full of ash and everything she's been hiding from is right here.

She takes a breath. Steadies.

KARA
(crisp, all business)
The tracker?

Ruthye checks it. Points.

RUTHYE
That way. Maybe two hours on foot.

KARA
Then we move.

They move.

## THE BRIGAND TRAIL — BILKI, THE DEAD WORLD

### 51. EXT. BILKI SURFACE — THE APPROACH — CONTINUOUS

They walk. The landscape is unrelenting — gray on gray on gray. Broken roads. The hulks of vehicles, rusted, stripped. Utility poles, snapped. The wind moves ash across the ground in low drifts.

The tracker pulses steadily. The dot isn't far. Close, actually — which is worse. Close means they don't have the luxury of waiting.

Kara and Ruthye move carefully. Kara's eyes are everywhere — she's been in combat zones, she knows how dead ground works. She steers them along cover, around open areas, past structures that might or might not be occupied.

Ahead: a COMPLEX. The old infrastructure of Bilki — a cluster of depots, bunkers, transit spines, built into a shallow canyon. This is where the tracker points.

RUTHYE
(reading the tracker)
He's in there. Somewhere in the complex.

KARA
Then we go in. Carefully.

RUTHYE
(grim)
Carefully.

KARA
(looks at her)
Ruthye. I mean it. Carefully. You stay behind me. If there's trouble, you get down. You don't —

RUTHYE
I know how to —

KARA
(sharper than she means)
You don't fight. Not here. Not now. I can't — 

(she stops, regroups)

I can't do what I need to do and worry about you. Stay behind me. Let me go first.

Ruthye bristles. The code doesn't have a "stay behind" clause. But she nods.

### 52. EXT. BILKI SURFACE — THE COMPLEX EDGE — CONTINUOUS

They reach the edge of the complex. The structures are old, built to last — reinforced concrete, steel, the kind of construction that survives the end of a civilization because it was built for exactly that.

And they're not empty.

Kara sees the signs: fresh tracks in the ash. Recently moved debris. Lights — dim, red — in a window. Krem's crew has been here. They've reactivated something.

She signals Ruthye: *Stop. Stay.*

Kara moves forward, low, careful. She rounds a broken wall — and STOPS.

DEFENSE SYSTEMS. Automated turrets — old, dirty, but active. They've been reactivated, their sensors sweeping in slow arcs. Pressure mines, visible in the ash — small, dark discs. Perimeter guns mounted on the structures, tracking.

This isn't a trap. It's not a plan. It's just what Bilki *is* — a world built to defend itself, and the defenses are still running, because no one turned them off. Machines with no ideology and no mercy.

Kara pulls back. Reports to Ruthye.

KARA
(quiet)
Automated defenses. Turrets. Mines. Perimeter guns.

RUTHYE
Krem's men?

KARA
(shakes her head)
Doesn't matter. The guns don't care who they shoot. They're just on.

She looks at the complex. There's no clean way through — the coverage is too good. The machines have angles on every approach.

KARA
I go first. I draw fire. You follow my path. Exactly my path.

RUTHYE
Kara —

KARA
*I go first.*

### 53. EXT. BILKI COMPLEX — THE DEFENSE TRAP — CONTINUOUS

They move. Kara goes first — low, fast enough, not fast enough. She's depleted. The red sun of Evelie is gone, but there's no yellow sun here either — just the dead gray sky, the cloud cover choking off any light. She's running on fumes.

The first turret detects her. The GUN SPINS, locks. Opens fire.

Kara MOVES — a burst of speed, the kind she can only manage for a moment, the kind that costs her. Rounds spark off the concrete behind her. She makes cover.

But the turret has awakened the others. A PERIMETER GUN swings toward her position. A second TURRET locks on. The complex lights up.

KARA
(to Ruthye)
*STAY DOWN!*

Ruthye is pinned behind a wall. She can see Kara — pinned, moving, trying to find a path. And she can see the path ahead — the route the tracker points to, the route to Krem — and it's *open*. The defenses are focused on Kara. Ruthye could move.

She starts to move.

KARA sees her. From across the kill-zone, under fire, Kara sees Ruthye start to move into the open.

KARA
*NO!*

And then Kara is moving — not away from the fire, but *toward Ruthye*. She crosses the kill-zone. Takes shrapnel — a ricochet catches her across the back, tearing the leather jacket, drawing blood. She doesn't slow down.

She reaches Ruthye. WRAPS HER IN HER ARMS. Turns her back to the turret. Shields her with her own body.

The gun fires. Rounds hit Kara — not clean hits, not penetrating, but impacts. She grunts with each one. The leather jacket shreds further. Blood on the concrete.

KARA
(breathing hard, to Ruthye)
*Are you okay?*

RUTHYE
(furious, muffled against Kara's chest)
*Let go of me.*

KARA
No. You move when I say —

A MINE detonates. Close. They're thrown — Kara takes the impact, keeps Ruthye under her. They hit the ground, roll, come up behind a broken wall.

### 54. EXT. BILKI COMPLEX — THE PIGGYBACK RUN — CONTINUOUS

The ground is detonating. The mines are going off — not all at once, but in a chain, one triggering the next, the kill-zone turning into a landscape of craters and fire.

Kara grabs Ruthye.

RUTHYE
What are you —

KARA
*Hold on.*

She hauls Ruthye onto her back. A piggyback. Ruthye's legs around her waist, arms around her neck.

And Kara RUNS.

Not clean superspeed. Not flight. A ruined, depleted approximation of speed — Kara stumbling through the minefield, shielding Ruthye with her own body, getting thrown by concussions, catching herself, running on. Shrapnel in her legs. Blood on her face. The leather jacket — her armor, her blanket, her disguise — ripped to ribbons on her back.

She runs through the gunfire. She runs through the explosions. She runs on fumes, on stubbornness, on the thing inside her that will not stop while someone she's protecting is on her back.

RUTHYE holds on. Her face is pressed against Kara's neck. She can feel Kara's heartbeat — hammering, irregular, the heartbeat of someone who is past her limit and still going. And Ruthye — who has been cold, who has been composed, who has been the girl with the code — feels something she hasn't felt since the kitchen.

Fear. Not for herself. For someone else.

They clear the minefield. Kara collapses behind a concrete barrier, breathing in huge, ragged gasps. Ruthye slides off her back.

RUTHYE
(crouching beside her)
Kara —

KARA
(waving her off, gasping)

The audience has waited the whole movie for this. The movie gives it to them.

Kara lands in the depot courtyard. The carnage is still here — Lobo's work, the bodies, the burned vehicles. But it's quiet now. The fires are lower. The wind moves ash.

Kara sets Ruthye down. She's different. Not just physically restored — unburdened. The promise recommitted. The weight off. She moves like someone who has stopped hiding.

Her eyes scan the courtyard. Take in the bodies. The destruction. And beyond it — the complex. The structures. She can see it all now, in a way she couldn't before. Every detail. Every heat signature. Every sound.

KARA
(still looking)
There.

### 78. EXT. BILKI COMPLEX — THE APPROACH — CONTINUOUS

A row of BRIGAND SHUTTLES, half-buried in the depot wreckage. Most are damaged, burned, dead — Lobo's work. But one is WARM. Engines hot. Loading bay open. The ride Krem is trying to use to run again.

KARA
(to Ruthye, calm)
He's at the shuttle.

RUTHYE
(moving)
Then we —

KARA
(puts a hand on her arm)
Stay back.

RUTHYE
(starts to argue)
Kara, I —

KARA looks at her. Not words. Just a look. The look of someone who is asking — not telling. Asking Ruthye to trust her. The same Ruthye who just saved Kara's life in the bike ascent, who chose her over revenge. They're past words now.

Ruthye holds Kara's gaze. And she nods.

### 79. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE SHUTTLE — CONTINUOUS

KREM is at the shuttle. He's gotten it together — barely. He's moving fast, the frantic efficiency of a man who knows his time is up. 

He has a huge DUFFEL BAG — gold, jewels, currency, whatever he could grab. Loot from the depot. He throws it into the shuttle.

And he has a weapon. A GIANT ENERGY CANNON — too big for a man his size, jury-rigged, mounted on a harness. He picks it up from the ground — from beside the body of one of his men, the man who was carrying it — and grunts as he hefts it. He grimaces as the harness attaches to his left shoulder and arm with a mechanical *thunk* and a grisly, wet sound, like it's interfacing with him directly.

He's bleeding. His eyes are still red from the Lobo encounter — burst capillaries, tear tracks. He looks like a man who has been crying and running and is trying very hard to do neither.

He turns to board the shuttle —

And Kara is there.

Not between him and the shuttle. Not blocking his path. Just — *there*. Standing in the courtyard. Hovering, slightly, her feet off the ground. In the full, easy, unconscious flight of a Kryptonian under a yellow sun.

Krem sees her. He freezes.

KARA
(quiet)
Krem.

### 80. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE NON-FIGHT — CONTINUOUS

Krem looks at her. At this woman — the drunk from the bar, the woman he dismissed. Except she's not drunk now. And she's not on a red-sun world. And she's floating.

His face does something complicated. Fear, yes. But also — something like understanding. He sees what she is. Not the specifics — he doesn't know the word *Kryptonian* — but the category. She is something bigger than him. Bigger than anything he has.

And he's looking at the Antidote. It hangs around his neck — a vial on a chain, small, glowing faintly. The thing Kara needs. The thing that will save Krypto.

Krem stands. Slowly. He's a man making a decision.

KREM
(quiet, looking at her)
Look around you.

Kara looks. The dead world. The ruins. The fires, burning in the distance.

KREM
There was never anyone to save us.

He could be talking about Lobo. He could be talking about his dead world. He's not a mustache-twirling villain. He's a bitter, tired man, almost bored with his own pain. This is the place that taught him his creed, and he has mistaken surviving it for understanding the universe.

KARA
(saying nothing, her eyes on the vial)

KREM
(continuing)
This is what's left. When everyone decides to look the other way. When everyone decides it's someone else's problem.

(beat)

You think you're different. You think you can — 

(he gestures at her, at the floating, at the power)

— fix it. With that. With whatever you are.

(beat)

But you're just the next thing that'll go wrong.

He raises his arm — the giant energy cannon. He aims it at Kara.

KARA is already moving.

### 81. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE ABSURDITY OF POWER

Krem FIRES. A bolt of energy — enormous, the kind of weapon that levels walls.

Kara is already off the ground, flying toward him. The bolt moves *so slowly*. She sees it, tracks it, moves around it. The absurdity of the power disparity — she is so far beyond this weapon that it might as well not exist.

Krem fires again. And again. Each bolt is enormous, devastating, would kill anything else. Kara flies through them, around them, untouched. She's not dodging — she's just *there*, and the bolts aren't.

She closes on Krem. In flight, she reaches out — and plucks his next bolt out of the air. Holds it for a moment. The energy crackles in her hand, and she closes her fist, and it's gone.

She slams the cannon — the whole giant rig — out of his hand. Maybe rougher than she needed to.

TIME RESUMES.

A SHOCKWAVE. The displaced air from Kara's flight hits Krem. It shouldn't be possible — the shockwave of a person moving — but Kara moved so fast, with such force, that the air itself became a weapon. Krem is BLASTED off his feet. He flies backward, meters, tens of meters, and hits the ground — hard.

He HOWLS. His right arm — the arm that was holding the cannon, the arm that took the brunt of Kara's slap — is not pointing the right way. Something inside it is broken.

Kara stops. She's standing over Krem, her hand still extended from the slap. She looks at her own hand, then at Krem, crumpled on the ground.

She's taken aback. She either didn't expect him to be so weak — or she didn't expect herself to be so angry.

### 82. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE OVERLOAD — CONTINUOUS

KARA takes a breath. Holds her fingers to her temples for a moment — the gesture of someone pulling themselves back. The red haze of anger receding.

KARA
(level, but you can hear the effort)
Listen. I don't want —

A SOUND. A high-pitched whine. Building fast.

Kara looks up.

KREM'S ARM-CANNON. It's sparking. Overloading. The rig is going critical — the weapon was already damaged, already jury-rigged, and Kara's slap pushed it over the edge. The energy core is destabilizing.

Krem's eyes go wide. He looks at his own weapon — the thing he jury-rigged onto his body — and he knows. He's staring at his own death.

And he grins.

A terrible, final grin. The grin of a man who has one move left, and it's the last one, and he's going to use it.

KREM
(softly)
This is what the world —

He triggers it.

### 83. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE BLAST — CONTINUOUS

RED. The screen fills with red. The energy cannon, overloaded, detonates — a kamikaze kill shot, everything the weapon had, all at once, directed at Kara.

Then WHITE. The blast is enormous. Concussive. A shockwave rolls across the depot, flattening structures, kicking up ash, extinguishing fires with the force of the displaced air.

A red arc across the sky — the concussive energy blasting past the horizon, a line of red light burning across the dead clouds.

Supergirl is — nowhere to be seen.

Silence.

The smoke clears. Slowly. Dust settles.

### 84. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — RUTHYE'S APPROACH

RUTHYE walks through the smoke.

The audience hasn't seen her since Kara told her to stay back. But she's here now. She walks through the ruin of the depot, past the debris, through the ash. The sword is in her hand.

She reaches Krem.

He's on his back. Bleeding. Burnt. The arm-cannon is a charred, smoldering ruin — still attached to his body, but barely. His left side is a mess. He's — barely alive. Barely.

And the ANTIDOTE — the vial on a chain — hangs around his neck. Unharmed. Sitting on his chest, right where it always was.

RUTHYE stands over him. The sword at her side. Her face is — the face from the cold open. The face from the bar. The face of the girl who disarmed a ruffian, who cited the code, who crossed worlds to stand exactly here.

Nothing in her body language telegraphs mercy. Nothing suggests hesitation. The audience should be in genuine suspense — because this girl has been building toward this moment for the whole film, and everything about her says she's going to finish it.

KREM looks up at her. His eyes are open. He can see her.

And his face — it's not the evangelist from the cold open. Not the philosopher who killed her father with deliberate spite. Not the man who said *this is what the world is* with the satisfaction of a teacher.

It's the face of a man who begged before Lobo and watched his own creed collapse. He's tired. Hollowed out. The version of himself that believed in something — even something monstrous — died on his knees in front of the Main Man.

KREM
(barely a whisper)
Just do it.

Not a lesson. Not *this is what the world is.* Just — a man who doesn't care anymore whether he lives or dies. He's not offering her graduation. He's not recruiting. He's just... done.

### 85. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE CHOICE

RUTHYE looks at him.

She looks at the thing she's been hunting for — however long it's been. Since the kitchen. Since her father's blood. The monster who killed her father.

And the thing she sees isn't a monster.

It's just a tired man. The regal cruelty that murdered her father is gone. There's nothing left to rage against — no creed to defeat, no philosophy to reject. Just a used-up person in the rubble. Killing him wouldn't prove anything. It wouldn't disprove anything. It would just be killing.

RUTHYE
(quietly, to herself or to him)
...there's nothing here.

Krem doesn't respond. He's past responding.

Ruthye raises the sword.

The audience holds its breath. The sword is up. The angle is right. Krem's neck is exposed.

She raises it higher.

And slashes.

### 86. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — THE CHAIN, NOT THE NECK

The blade comes down — and the SOUND it makes is metal on metal. The CHAIN around Krem's neck parts. The vial — the antidote — falls.

Ruthye catches it on the blade. Lifts it. The chain slides down the steel. She takes it in her hand.

She turns around.

### 87. EXT. BILKI DEPOT — "LET'S GO HOME"

KARA is standing behind her.

She's — fine. Not a scratch. The yellow sun healed her, and the blast that looked catastrophic was nothing to full-power Supergirl. She's been standing there. Watching.

How long has she been there? She didn't intervene. She *could* have — at any speed, from any distance, she could have been between Ruthye and Krem in an instant. But she *didn't*. She stood there, and she watched, and she let Ruthye choose.

The look on Kara's face as Ruthye turns around — the antidote in her hand, not blood on the blade — is relief. Mostly relief. But also pride. She's watching a thirteen-year-old step off of Krem's path. Maybe avoiding her own as well.

The full arc of Kara's relationship to Ruthye, completed in one beat of *inaction*: she protected her (the minefield), she restrained her (the slash), she was saved by her (the Lobo ultimatum) — and now she *trusted* her. The last and hardest thing.

Ruthye looks at Kara. Kara looks at Ruthye.

Ruthye holds out the antidote. Their hands meet around the vial — Ruthye's small, steady; Kara's strong, trembling.

KARA's face crumples. The tears come. She pulls Ruthye into a hug — fierce, tight, the hug of someone who almost lost everything and didn't.

KARA
(whispering)
Thank you.

RUTHYE
(whispering back)
I had to save you. That's my code.

Kara laughs. A wet, broken laugh. She holds Ruthye tighter.

RUTHYE
(pulling back, looking at the antidote)
Let's go home.

KARA
(nods, wiping her eyes)
Yeah.

(beat)

Yeah. Let's go home.

Behind them: KREM. On the ground. Still alive, still barely. They don't look back. The camera doesn't follow him.

They walk toward the shuttle — the warm one, the one Krem was going to use. Kara hotwires it with absurd ease, her full powers restored. The engines flare.

The shuttle lifts off. Rises through the clouds. Into yellow light.

The camera stays with the depot courtyard. Krem is there. On the ground. Alone.

He lies there. He breathes. He doesn't get up.

We never see what happens to him. The camera doesn't care. It pulls back, up, away — following the shuttle as it climbs toward the sky.

The story was never about him.

## RESOLUTION — THE BIRTHDAY

### 88. INT. DEEP-SPACE REST STOP — CLINIC — CONTINUOUS

The shuttle docks. Kara and Ruthye run through the concourse — past the stalls, past the funky aliens, past the fluorescent commerce. They have the antidote. The clock has been ticking.

They burst into the clinic. The DOCK-HEALER is there. Krypto is still in the stabilizing tube — his breathing shallow, his body still, the readout on the tube blinking urgent amber.

DOCK-HEALER
(seeing them)
You have it?

Kara holds up the vial.

DOCK-HEALER
(taking it, examining it)
This is it.

(beat)

This is the real thing.

They move fast. The dock-healer draws the antidote into a syringe. The tube opens — the liquid drains away, revealing Krypto, limp, barely breathing.

The dock-healer finds the vein. Inserts the needle.

Pushes the plunger.

Nothing happens.

A long, terrible silence. Ruthye grips Kara's hand. Kara is rigid. The dock-healer watches.

And then — a sound. A breath. A *real* breath. Not the shallow, assisted rasp. A full, deep, dog-breath. The kind that fills the lungs.

Krypto's eyes open. He lifts his head. Looks around.

KARA
(voice breaking)
Krypto.

The dog sees her. His tail — thump, thump, thump — beats against the table. He struggles up, weak but *alive*, and Kara catches him, pulls him to her, buries her face in his fur.

KARA
(crying, laughing)
You stupid, beautiful dog. You stupid —

Krypto licks her face. Ruthye is there too, her hand on Krypto's side, feeling the breath go in and out.

Ruthye looks at Kara. And Ruthye smiles — a real smile, the first one, the one we haven't seen. Small, but real.

The dock-healer watches this with the satisfied expression of a job well done, and goes back to their notes.

### 89. EXT. EVELIE — RUTHYE'S HOMESTEAD — GOLDEN HOUR — LATER

Evelie. The same world. But the light is different — or we're different. The red sun sits low, and the light it casts is deep, warm, amber. Not dead. Just evening. The light of a world going to sleep, not a world that's already gone.

The farm. The house. The vegetable garden, the pen with the draft animals. The fence.

Kara's ship — her ship, repaired, a salvaged Brigand drive crystal installed — sits in the yard, next to Ruthye's mother's vehicle.

### 90. EXT. EVELIE — RUTHYE'S HOMESTEAD — THE REUNION — CONTINUOUS

EXTREME CLOSE-UP: Krypto, LEAPING into frame. Full-body, ears flying, the joy of a dog who is alive and knows it and wants everyone else to know it too. He hits Kara — who is ready, laughing — and knocks her back a step. She catches him, spins him, buries her face in his fur.

KRYPTO
(barking, tail going like a metronome)

PULL BACK: it's not just Kara. RUTHYE is beside her. And behind Ruthye — 

Her MOTHER. And her BROTHER.

The brother — 5, 6 — is staring at Ruthye. His face is doing something complicated: the face of a kid who has been waiting for his sister to come home and has been told, in the careful way adults tell children things, that she might not.

RUTHYE sees them. She stops.

Her mother is there. Standing. Her face — the face from the kitchen, the face of grief so total it hadn't found its shape — has found its shape. It's the face of a woman who thought she'd lost her daughter and hasn't. Her hand is over her mouth. She's crying.

RUTHYE
(very small, very young)
Mom.

MOTHER
(voice breaking)
Ruthye.

Ruthye crosses to her mother. And the mask — the cold, composed, code-citing mask she's worn since the kitchen — comes off. She walks, then runs, and she crashes into her mother, and they hold each other, and the brother is there too, small arms around Ruthye's waist, and they're a family.

Kara watches from the ship. Krypto in her arms. Her face is — the face from the yellow-sun rebirth. Open. Feeling. The person underneath the numbing, visible at last.

### 91. EXT. EVELIE — RUTHYE'S HOMESTEAD — LATER

Later. The golden light has deepened. The family is together — mother, Ruthye, brother, and Kara, sitting at the kitchen table. The same table where Krem sat. The same table where the father died.

But the table is different now. There is food on it. Simple, good, farm food. The mother has made a meal. Because that's what you do when someone comes home.

The father's absence is present — an empty chair, a pause when the mother sets five places and then stops. Ruthye sees it. Her mother sees her see it. They don't speak about it. Not now.

Krypto is under the table, being fed scraps by the brother, who has decided the dog is the best thing that has ever happened to him.

### 92. EXT. EVELIE — RUTHYE'S HOMESTEAD — PORCH — CONTINUOUS

Ruthye and Kara, on the porch. The red sky deepening to purple at the edges. The first stars coming out — and among them, smeared nebulas, visible now that Ruthye knows to look.

The farm is quiet. The draft animals are settling. Somewhere inside, the mother is putting the brother to bed.

RUTHYE looks at Kara. Kara looks at Ruthye.

RUTHYE
(after a long, comfortable silence)
I wish I could go with you.

KARA
(surprised, pleased)
Yeah?

RUTHYE
(nods)
But I have — 

(she gestures back at the house, at the mother, at the brother, at the life inside)

—I have this.

(beat)

This is where I belong.

KARA
(solemn)
I know.

(beat)

I would trade the world for what you have.

She means it. The line that started as a drunk's plea in a bar — *go home* — is now the truest thing either of them has ever said to each other. They both hear it this time.

RUTHYE
(quiet)
I heard you. The first time. In the bar.

KARA
(smiles)
I know.

RUTHYE
I just couldn't — not yet. I had to —

KARA
I know.

Silence. The kind that doesn't need filling.

Then Kara's face shifts. An impish grin breaking through the solemnity.

KARA
You know... we never did get a chance to finish celebrating my birthday.

RUTHYE
(blinks)
Your... what?

KARA
My birthday. You know, the reason I was in that bar in the first place? I was — it was my birthday. And I was — 

(she gestures, the drinking, the mess)

— yeah.

RUTHYE
(stunned)
That was your *birthday?*

KARA
It was. I spent it in a bar, and then your sword was on my table, and then Krem walked in, and then — 

(she waves at the whole movie)

— all of this.

Ruthye stares at her. And then — 

She giggles.

Not a laugh. A *giggle*. The first one. The sound the whole movie has been building toward: a thirteen-year-old girl, who has been cold and composed and code-citing and grief-armored since the first frame, *giggling*. 

The weight lifts. The load comes off her shoulders, and the audience feels it go. It's the sound of someone who has chosen, finally, to be a kid again.

RUTHYE
(giggling)
Your *birthday*.

KARA
(laughing now too)
My birthday.

RUTHYE
(wiping her eyes)
I almost killed a man and it was your *birthday*.

KARA
Best one in years.

They laugh together. On the porch. In the red light. And the sound of it — the laughter — is the opposite of the cold open. The opposite of the kitchen, the blood, the scream. It's the sound of life, chosen.

### 93. EXT. EVELIE — RUTHYE'S HOMESTEAD — THE DEPARTURE — CONTINUOUS

Kara's ship is warming up. The drive hums — cleaner now, the Brigand crystal purring in a way her old one never did.

Kara stands at the base of the ramp. Ruthye is there. The mother is at the door. The brother — supposed to be in bed — is at the window, watching.

KARA
(yelling to the mother)
We'll be back tonight — tomorrow at the latest!

MOTHER
(waving, tearful, smiling)
You'd better!

Kara turns to Ruthye. Holds out her hand.

RUTHYE takes it. They stand there — hand in hand — and then Kara pulls her into one last hug. Brief, tight.

KARA
(in Ruthye's ear)
Be good.

The echo of Alura. The promise, passed on.

Ruthye steps back. Kara goes up the ramp. Krypto is already on the ship, tongue out, ready.

### 94. EXT. EVELIE — SKY — CONTINUOUS — THE FINAL IMAGE

The ship lifts off. Rises above the farm. Above the fields. Above the red-sun world.

In the yard, RUTHYE watches it go. And she's — *laughing*. Her face is up, her hair is blowing, her hand is raised, and she's laughing. Free. The girl who shielded her brother against her body and stared at the road she would take — that girl is gone. This one — the one laughing in the red light — chose differently.

The ship flies across the sky. A bright streak against the fiery red — the same red sun that lit Kara's avoidance, now the ordinary blaze of a living evening over Ruthye's home. The palette forgives its own opening.

The ship shrinks to a point of light, among the other points of light — the stars, the nebulae, the vast and indifferent and occasionally hilarious universe.

RUTHYE stands in the yard, watching, until the light is gone.

## MID-CREDITS — HOME

### 95. INT. METROPOLIS — KARA'S APARTMENT — WINDOW — NIGHT

TITLE CARD: *Metropolis. Earth.*

A window. A city beyond it — METROPOLIS. Gleaming. Alive. The opposite of Argo. Towers of glass and steel, lit from within, going up and up and up. Traffic — flying traffic, ground traffic, the hum of a city that never stops. Somewhere out there, the S on a building. The Daily Planet globe.

KARA stands at the window. Krypto beside her — healthy, alert, ears up. She's out of the battered leather jacket. She's in — something simple. A sweater. Something a person wears when they're not hiding.

Her face is in the reflection, superimposed over the city. And her face is — sad. But present. Not numb. Not drinking. Not reaching for the bottle before her eyes open. *Feeling it all at once*, the way the whole film has been asking her to.

She looks at her reflection. At the city. At the dog.

KARA
(quietly)
Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer this time.

A breath.

KARA
(a small, uncertain smile)
I mean... it's good to be...

(she stops, tries again)

...home.

In the reflection behind her — Krypto, being a terror. Chewing something he shouldn't. Tearing around the apartment with the manic energy of a dog who has been healed by a yellow sun and has a lot of feelings about it.

KARA turns. Sees the chaos. Closes her eyes.

KARA
(a different kind of sigh)
Krypto. *Krypto.*

(beat, looking at the mess)

...good boy.

She's not sure yet. She might not be sure for a long time. But she's here. And she's choosing to stay — not hiding on a red-sun world, not dodging Clark's calls, not reaching for the bottle before her eyes open.

The audience leaves with the sense that she's going to be all right.

In the window, reflected over Metropolis, the first light of morning is starting — a yellow sun, rising. The same sun that healed her. The sun that will keep healing her, every day, as long as she chooses to stand in it.

**SMASH CUT TO BLACK.**
