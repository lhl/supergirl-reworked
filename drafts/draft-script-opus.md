# SUPERGIRL
### *Woman of Tomorrow*

**First-draft screenplay — "opus" draft**

---

> *A coming-of-age story about grief, in which a woman must win an argument about what it means to be good — for herself as much as for the child she is trying to save.*

**Tone:** *All-Star Superman* warmth, *For All Seasons* melancholy, *Woman of Tomorrow* lyricism — an off-kilter road-trip / coming-of-age drama that happens to cross deep space. The universe is funny; the grief is not. Action is punctuation, never the sentence. Wes Anderson precision in the connective tissue; earnestness, sincerity, *unirony* throughout. The camera loves life and refuses to love death.

**Principal cast:**

- **KARA ZOR-EL / SUPERGIRL** — last daughter of a dead world, hiding under a red sun. Numb → engaged → integrated. She agrees with the revenge; her restraint is barely held. The more dangerous of the two, because she could actually do it.
- **RUTHYE KNOLL** — thirteen. Cold, serious, unsettling to adults. Cites her people's Writ, which is armor over grief. Protected → restrained → rescuer → trusted. She must *choose* life, not be argued into it.
- **KREM** — survivor of a dead world who chose predation and kept choosing it. An evangelist of cruelty, not a cartoon. A finished tragedy. The end of Ruthye's road, made flesh.
- **KRYPTO** — the last of Argo that still breathes. The clock the whole film runs on.
- **LOBO** — the Main Man. The universe personified: capricious, not malevolent. What Supergirl looks like without a code. He is in the wrong movie, and he knows it.

*Format note: em-dashes and bold character cues throughout. Scene numbers omitted at this draft stage.*

FADE IN:

**EXT. EVELIE — FARM COUNTRY — DUSK (WHICH IS ALWAYS)**

A red sun the color of a cooling coal sits low and enormous on the horizon. It does not seem to be setting. It never seems to be doing anything.

Its light is flat, sodium-lamp orange. It lands on everything — the rows of squat blue-leafed crops, the irrigation wheels, a scarecrow stitched from feed sacks — and makes all of it look faintly *embalmed*. Beautiful, in the way a photograph of someone dead is beautiful.

A farmhouse stands alone in the fields. Warm yellow light in the windows — the only warm light in the frame. Someone in that house is burning good lamp oil against the red.

Smoke from the chimney. A dog would bark. There is no dog.

**INT. KNOLL HOMESTEAD — KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS**

The warm light has a source: an oil lamp, a hearth, a table laid for four.

A family mid-meal. This is the last time we will see them like this, and the film knows it, so it holds on them a beat too long.

ALDER KNOLL (40s, sunburned, big careful hands) ladles stew. His wife, NELL (30s, sharp-eyed, quicker to laugh than he is), passes bread. RUTHYE (13) sits very straight and eats very seriously, as she does everything. And PIP (6), her brother, kicks the table leg and narrates his food.

**PIP**

*(of a dumpling)* This one is the king. This one is the king's *dog.* The king is going to eat his dog.

**NELL**

Pip.

**PIP**

The dog said it was *fine.*

Alder taps his cup against the table — a small ritual, a household grace. The others quiet. He doesn't close his eyes. He looks at each of them instead.

**ALDER**

For the light we kept. For the debt we owe it.

**RUTHYE / NELL / PIP**

*(together, easy, worn smooth by repetition)* For the light we kept.

They eat. Pip resumes the execution of the king's dog. Nell steals a look at Alder that means *we made this.* It is, genuinely, a good life. Someone chose to build it.

The door opens.

Not kicked. *Opened* — the way you'd open your own door.

KREM stands in it. Late 30s, lean, travel-grimed, a face that was handsome before it decided not to bother. Behind him, THREE BRIGANDS — armed, unhurried, already looking at the pantry.

Nobody at the table moves. Pip's fork stops halfway.

Krem takes off his hat. An old courtesy. He looks at the table, the lamp, the stew, the way a man looks at a fire he's cold enough to want.

**KREM**

Don't get up.

He does not wait to be invited. He pulls the empty fourth chair — *Pip's spot has been given to a guest, and Pip has been quietly folded onto Nell's lap* — and sits. Reaches past Alder, without asking, and takes the pie cooling on the sideboard. Sets it in front of himself.

His men fan out through the house. We hear them — a cupboard, a chest, boots on the stairs. Krem doesn't watch them. He watches the family watch him.

**KREM**

*(cutting pie)* We need what a house like this has. Powder. Dry stores. Fuel cells, if you've got them. Rope. Whatever's in the barn worth carrying.

*(a bite)* This is good. Did you make this?

He asks Nell. She doesn't answer. He nods as if she had.

**ALDER**

*(carefully, hands flat on the table)* Take the stores. Take the barn. We won't stop you.

**KREM**

I know.

He says it kindly. That's the thing about Krem — the kindness is real, which is what makes it unbearable. He eats another bite. Sets the fork down. Something in him settles into a subject he's clearly returned to before.

**KREM**

You know, I ate at a table like this once. My mother's. Better than this — no offense — but like this. Warm. Everybody sure the world was going to keep being the world.

*(he gestures at the lamp with the fork)* Good light. You people love your light out here.

Alder says nothing. Krem takes the silence as encouragement.

**KREM**

My world had a sky like a held breath. Cities you'd have wept to see. And when the end came for it — and the end comes for all of them, that's the one law, you'll learn it — do you know who came to help us?

Beat. He looks around the warm kitchen as if the answer might be hiding in it.

**KREM**

Nobody. The neighbors we traded with. The powers we paid tribute to. The good, decent, *lawful* people, exactly like you, at tables exactly like this — they closed their doors and they turned up their lamps so they wouldn't have to see it, and they let us die.

*(gently)* I was like you, once. So sure. So — *haughty.* Before I understood how things actually are.

He isn't threatening them. He's *teaching* them. He's almost tender about it.

**ALDER**

That's not — 

*(he chooses his words like stepping on ice)* — that's not us. My people never had truck with any of that. We're farmers. We keep to our own.

It is not true. It hangs in the room like a held breath. Nell doesn't look at Alder. Ruthye does — a flicker, a child clocking a lie in a parent for the first time.

Krem registers all of it. The lie, the flinch, the daughter catching the flinch. He lets it pass. He goes back to the pie.

**KREM**

Sure.

Upstairs, something breaks. Pip flinches into his mother.

For a while nobody speaks. Krem eats. The scrape of his fork is enormous. The Brigands drift back into the kitchen doorway, arms full — sacks, a coil of rope, a strongbox.

**BRIGAND (VORN)**

Barn's got fuel cells. Enough.

**KREM**

*(not turning)* Good.

He could stand up now. He has what he came for. The off-ramp is *right there,* wide and paved, and the film makes sure we see it: the transaction is ugly but survivable. He wipes his mouth. He reaches for his hat.

And then Pip, who is six, and who has been holding it in with everything a six-year-old has, whispers into his mother's collar:

**PIP**

*(barely audible)* I don't like him.

Krem stops. Not angry. Interested.

He looks — not at Pip — at Ruthye.

Because Ruthye is looking at *him.* Has been the whole time. And in her face is the thing Krem crossed a dead sky to stop existing: the flat, unbroken certainty that this is *wrong.* That the world is fair and good and this man is an aberration in it. That he is a bad weather that will pass and be *forgotten,* and she and her lamp and her table will still be here.

Thirteen years old, and she is not afraid of him. She is *unimpressed* by him.

Something in Krem — the thing that chose predation on a dead world and has chosen it every day since — cannot let that stand.

**KREM**

*(softly, to Ruthye)* You think it's not true.

Ruthye doesn't answer. That's the answer.

Krem stands. Sets his hat back down on the table, deliberately — *he isn't leaving yet after all.* Alder is up too, instantly, putting his body between Krem and the children — and Vorn and a second Brigand have him by the arms before he's taken a full step, easy, bored, they've done this in a hundred kitchens.

Nell is on her feet with Pip. Ruthye stands.

The room goes loud. Alder straining, Nell shouting, Pip wailing, chairs over —

Krem crosses to Nell and strikes her. Open hand. Not to hurt her, really — to *test.* To see who moves.

Alder detonates. Roars, throws a man off, gets one step — and is hauled back down, pinned, a knee in his spine, his cheek to his own kitchen floor.

The room drops to just breathing. Nell on the ground, hand to her face. Pip screaming with no sound coming out. Alder pinned, wrenching, useless.

And it *cools.* It genuinely cools. Krem straightens his coat. Nobody is dead. The mother is struck but alive, the father restrained but alive, the children untouched. He has his fuel cells and his rope. He could pick up his hat and walk out into the red evening and this would be a story the Knolls told for the rest of their long lives, the terrible night the raiders came, *and everyone lived.*

Krem looks down at Alder, pinned, glaring up at him with a father's whole hatred.

Then he looks at Ruthye.

She has put herself in front of Pip. Her arm is back, holding him behind her body. And she is still — *still* — looking at Krem the way you'd look at a stain. Not pleading. Not afraid. Waiting for him to be over.

Krem holds her eyes. And we watch him decide.

**KREM**

*(quietly, almost sad)* I really was like you.

He draws a short blade from his belt. Kneels by Alder's pinned head. And — never once looking at the father, eyes locked on the *daughter* the entire time — he does the worst thing he came to do.

We do not see the blade land. We see Ruthye's face. We see the exact instant the world stops being fair.

Nell screams. Pip is turned away, sobbing into Ruthye's back, spared the sight — Ruthye's hand has pushed his face into her shoulder. She did not look away herself. She watched, because he wanted her to, and because something in her had already decided not to give him the mercy of her flinch.

Krem stands. Cleans the blade on his own coat, unhurried. He picks up his hat. At the door he pauses, and delivers it not like a threat but like a gift — like he's the first honest man she's ever met, doing her the favor no one did him.

**KREM**

This is what the world is.

He puts on his hat and steps out into the red. His men follow with the family's stores. The door swings, not quite shutting, on its hinge.

Nell has crawled to Alder. Pip is a siren against Ruthye's spine.

**FINAL IMAGE — hold:**

Ruthye, standing. Her brother huddled into her, shaking — he knows, but he did not see. Her arms are around him. And her eyes are fixed on the door Krem walked through.

Not grief. Not shock. Not even yet the tears, which will come later, alone.

*Rage.* The kind that has already decided something before the mind has caught up to what.

Hold on her.

**CUT TO:**

**BLACK.**

**KREM (V.O.)**

*(a whisper across the cut, warm, awful)* This is what the world is.

The quest has already begun. Not when she meets Kara. Not when she picks up a sword. Here. In a doorway, holding the life she should choose, staring at the road she will.

---

**INT. KARA'S SHIP — SLEEPING BERTH — "MORNING" (RED)**

From that whisper — into silence. Real silence. The recycled hum of an old ship.

A cramped berth. Bottles. A star-scout's cockpit gone to seed — laundry over the instruments, a mug growing something, a single good jacket (battered, oversized, cracked brown leather) thrown over the pilot's chair like a body.

And a woman asleep, not peacefully. KARA ZOR-EL (late 20s, though the light makes her older). She twitches. Her mouth works. And behind her eyes —

**FLASH — ARGO. (FRAGMENTS. NOT A SCENE. WOUNDS.)**

— a sky the wrong color, cracking like glazed pottery —

— a woman's face, ALURA, saying something we can't hear, her hand reaching —

— the floor, tilting, everything on it sliding —

— a small pod, a hatch, a hand on the glass from the inside —

— and *silence,* the specific silence of after —

Kara wakes with a gasp, sitting straight up.

For one second she doesn't know where she is. Then she does, which is worse.

Her hand is already moving — before her eyes are fully open, before she's upright, pure practiced reflex — to the bottle beside the berth. She finds it. Drinks. Lies back down.

That single gesture tells you how long this has been going on.

Across the berth, on the pilot's chair, a shape lifts its head: KRYPTO. A big, absurd, snow-white dog, the only clean thing in the ship. He looks at her with the patient concern of an animal who has watched this every morning and still hopes today is different.

Kara doesn't look at him. She can't, yet.

A soft chime. A panel light. A device on the shelf, patient, blinking. Kara stares at the low red ceiling.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

*(gently, an old ship's AI, worn to politeness)* Good morning, Kara. You have one saved message. Would you like to —

**KARA**

No.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

It's four days old now.

**KARA**

*(closes her eyes)* Ship.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Playing.

She didn't say yes. The ship played it anyway. It's been trying for four days.

A hologram flickers up over the shelf — small, blue, imperfect. A man. Kind face, dark curl of hair, the reception bad enough that he glitches. CLARK. He's clearly recording this into a phone, somewhere loud and bright and *alive.*

**CLARK (V.O. / HOLO)**

Hey. It's me. Um — again. *(a laugh at himself)* You'd think I'd get better at these.

Kara doesn't get up. She listens to it lying down, the bottle on her chest, the way you'd let rain fall on you.

**CLARK (V.O. / HOLO)**

I don't — I'm not calling to lecture. I promise. Lois says hi. Ma says more than hi, she says a lot, I'm not gonna repeat all of it.

*(beat, softer)* I just — I know where you are. Roughly. I know what that light does, Kara, I know why you picked it. And I'm not gonna come drag you off some rock, that's not — you'd hate me, and you'd be right.

*(the smile goes)* But you don't have to be nowhere. That's all. There's a — there's a whole planet here with your name on the buzzer. Whenever. No expiry.

*(he glances off-frame, someone's calling him)* — I gotta — okay. Call me back. Or don't, but — you know. Rao's light, cuz. Come home.

The hologram folds up and dies. Blue gone. Just red left.

Kara lies there. On the shelf, next to where the light was, there's a small framed image — too dim to make out, but she's turned it face-down at some point. We can guess.

Krypto pads over. Puts his enormous head on the edge of the berth. Whines, very quietly — a request.

Now she looks at him. And her face does the only soft thing it will do for a while.

**KARA**

*(hoarse)* Yeah. Yeah, okay. Breakfast.

She sits up. Winces. Reaches past the bottle — pauses — and, small victory, small defeat, takes it with her as she stands.

**INT. KARA'S SHIP — GALLEY / PORTHOLE — CONTINUOUS**

She pours something into a bowl for Krypto, who inhales it. She doesn't eat. She stands at the porthole with the bottle.

Through the glass: the red sun. Enormous, low, unmoving. The port town below — squat structures, a landing field, the flat orange nothing of Evelie.

She has a ship. She has a cousin with a planet and a buzzer with her name on it. She has a family who says more than hi.

She lifts the bottle. Looks at the red light through it, the world gone bloodier through the glass.

**KARA**

*(to the sun, or the dog, or no one)* Just a little longer.

She drinks.

Krypto looks up from the empty bowl, at her, at the bottle, at the red porthole. His tail does not wag.

She scratches his ears. She does not call Clark back.

Avoidance isn't something that happened to her. It's a thing she does every morning, with her own two hands.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. EVELIE — PORT TOWN — "DAY" (RED)**

Establishing. A frontier port under the coal-red sun. Landing pads, fuel gantries, a customs shed with a queue of the strangest travelers you've seen — and none of them remarkable to anyone here. A vendor sells something on sticks. Signage in three alphabets, one of them apparently just dents.

Down a side row: a bar. Low, long, dug half into the ground against the heat. A hand-lettered sign over the door.

**THE LONG DUSK**

*(and beneath, smaller)* *always open — the sun sure isn't going anywhere*

**INT. THE LONG DUSK — CONTINUOUS**

Dim, close, the red light coming in slats through shutters. A dozen patrons — offworld haulers, a card game between two things with too many hands, a musician playing an instrument that is mostly gourd. The bartender, GORL (a broad, patient, four-eyed being who has heard everything), polishes a glass that will never be clean.

At the end of the bar: Kara. The battered leather jacket now on her back. She is drunk. Not charmingly. Not as a bit. She's an elbow on the bar and a glass she's stopped bothering to lift. Krypto lies at her boots, alert in a way she isn't. She is, very quietly, disappearing.

She's not bothering anyone. That's almost the saddest part.

The door opens. Red light spills in, and a small silhouette in it.

RUTHYE. Thirteen. A traveling coat too big for her — her father's, we understand without being told. A wrapped bundle across her back that is very obviously a sword and that she carries like it's a fishing pole. She is filthy from the road and composed as a judge.

She walks in like she's reading the room off a page. Scanning faces. Hunting.

A RUFFIAN (big, drunk, the kind of bored that goes looking) steps into her path, delighted.

**RUFFIAN**

You lost, sweetpea? This ain't a —

Ruthye doesn't break stride and doesn't raise her voice.

**RUTHYE**

Move.

**RUFFIAN**

*(to the room, grinning)* She says *move* — 

He reaches for her shoulder. What happens next is not a superhero fight. It's fast, economical, and slightly horrible: she catches the wrist, turns it the wrong way, drops her weight, and puts three hundred pounds of ruffian face-first onto a card table, which folds. His own knife spins across the floor and stops at her boot. She puts a foot on it. She has not changed expression once.

The card game does not restart. The whole bar has recalibrated.

Ruthye picks up the knife, considers it, sets it on the bar with the handle toward Gorl — *your house, your rules, I'm not a thief* — and keeps scanning.

At the end of the bar, Kara has turned her head. Watching. Something in the fog lifts, an inch. She is intrigued despite herself. Or maybe she recognizes something.

Ruthye's scan reaches Krypto — the one clean, strong thing in the room — and travels up the leash of him to Kara. She reads Kara head to toe: the jacket, the glass, the disappearing. Files it. Walks over. Climbs onto the stool beside her with the gravity of a diplomat.

**RUTHYE**

You're not from here.

**KARA**

*(after a moment, amused)* Neither are you.

**RUTHYE**

*(to Gorl)* Water. Clean, if you have it.

Gorl looks at Kara. Kara gives a small nod: *she's with me, I guess.* Gorl pours water.

They sit. Kara studies the kid. The kid studies the door.

**KARA**

That was a nice throw.

**RUTHYE**

He was in the way.

**KARA**

Where'd a kid learn that?

**RUTHYE**

I'm not a kid. I'm thirteen.

**KARA**

*(a ghost of a smile)* Right. My mistake.

Kara pours herself another. Ruthye watches the pour the way an auditor watches a number.

**KARA**

What's a thirteen-year-old doing in a port bar on the ugliest planet in the sky?

Ruthye is quiet a moment, deciding how much this drunk woman is worth. Then, because the code gives even confession a formal shape, she recites it. Flat. Practiced. Armor.

**RUTHYE**

Six days ago, men came to my family's house. Their leader took our stores and killed my father. Deliberately. After it was over. As a — 

*(the smallest hitch, then past it)* — as a lesson.

*(she straightens)* By the Writ of my people, the debt is mine to collect. The law is not a feeling. It doesn't ask whether I'm ready. It says the blood is owed and I am the one who is owed it. So.

*(to the door, not to Kara)* I tracked them here. Overland, six days. They're still on Evelie — I made sure of that. And I need someone who can fight, because I can throw a drunk man onto a table but I can't fight ten.

She turns and looks at Kara directly for the first time. It is a job interview.

**RUTHYE**

You look like you used to be able to fight. Before.

*(beat)* Before whatever this is.

Kara huffs a laugh — got me — and drinks to it.

**KARA**

I'm not muscle for hire, kid.

**RUTHYE**

Ruthye.

**KARA**

Kara.

**RUTHYE**

Kara. Will you help me or not?

Kara looks at her a long moment. The kid is serious in a way that unsettles adults, and Kara is an adult, and it unsettles her. But there's a question she can't not ask. She asks it lightly. She has no idea it's a trapdoor.

**KARA**

You said your father. What about — you've got family back home still? Somebody who knows where you are?

**RUTHYE**

*(matter of fact)* My mother. And my brother. He's six.

*(a fact, filed under logistics)* They're waiting for me. When it's done, I'll go back.

And there it is.

Kara goes very still. The glass stops halfway. The fog in her eyes doesn't clear so much as *split,* and something underneath shows through — raw, sudden, entirely without armor. The audience already knows what this sentence does to her, because we watched her not answer Clark. So we don't need her to explain. We just watch it land.

**KARA**

*(quietly)* You have a mother. And a brother. Alive. Waiting.

**RUTHYE**

Yes.

**KARA**

And you left them. To come do this.

**RUTHYE**

*(the first crack of impatience — she wants a soldier, not a counselor)* The Writ —

**KARA**

*(not loud — worse than loud)* I heard you about the Writ.

Beat. Kara sets the glass down. When she speaks it isn't a speech. It's the thing the alcohol lets through the door before she can shut it.

**KARA**

My whole family's gone. My whole — everyone I ever ate dinner with is gone. My *world* is gone, the actual planet, there's no — there's no house to track anybody back to.

*(she looks at Ruthye like she's looking at a photograph of something lost)* And you've got all of it. Right now. Tonight. A mother who's going to be scared when you're not at breakfast. A little brother who thinks you hung the moons. And you got on the road to chase a *dead man's* worth of dying, and you left the living behind you to do it.

Her voice doesn't break. It does something quieter and worse. It goes gentle.

**KARA**

Go home.

The two most honest words she's said in weeks, and she said them drunk, to a stranger, because she couldn't say them to herself.

Ruthye receives this the way she receives everything: without moving. But it costs her something to hold still, and we see the cost.

**RUTHYE**

I can't. The debt —

**KARA**

The debt'll be there. The *mother* won't be, forever. Trust me on the order those two things go in.

**RUTHYE**

You don't understand.

**KARA**

I understand better than anybody in this bar. Go home, Ruthye.

Ruthye's jaw sets. And because she is thirteen and cornered and *right,* by her own lights, she does the thing children do when adults won't take them seriously: she produces evidence.

She reaches into her too-big coat and puts something on the bar between them. Hard, with a click.

A grungy metal-and-glass case, about the size of a baseball. Inside it, a crystal turns slowly, glowing a deep steady color — a *drive core,* a ship's heart, clearly not local, clearly stolen.

**RUTHYE**

This is theirs. Their ship's fuel core. I took it out of their raider while they slept off my father's wine. This is why they're still on this planet — they cannot leave without it.

*(she sets it precisely, like a chess piece)* They are not out there somewhere. They are *here,* looking for this, and for me. I didn't wander in hoping. I baited a hook and I'm sitting on it.

Kara looks at the crystal. Then at the kid. A flicker of real alarm cuts through the fog — this is not a runaway's fantasy. This is a thirteen-year-old who has, competently, put herself on a killer's hunting ground.

**KARA**

Ruthye. Take that, and go home tonight, and throw it in a river on the way.

**RUTHYE**

I have one more thing.

She sets a second object beside the first. Smaller. A battered directional finder, a needle behind cracked glass, dark now.

**RUTHYE**

This was my father's. It's keyed to his hunting rifle — the good one, the one they took. So I can find *it,* wherever they carry it. It's faint. It only points when I wake it up.

Her hand goes to the switch.

**KARA**

*(a beat too slow, the drink slowing her)* Don't —

**RUTHYE**

I want you to see it's real.

She turns it on.

The needle swings. The glass floods with light — not a distant, hunting glow. An *arrived* glow. Bright, and getting brighter, and the needle isn't pointing out the door toward the horizon.

It's pointing at the door. And then it swings — tracking — toward it.

Kara reads it a full second before Ruthye does, because Kara has been hunted before.

**KARA**

*(low)* Rao.

Ruthye's tracker isn't the only tracker in the story. Krem keyed his own finder to the stolen drive core — the very thing sitting on the bar. Two hooks, one line, both ends now pulled taut. Ruthye baited a trap. So did he.

**KARA**

The crystal. They can track the *crystal —*

The door of the Long Dusk slams open. Red light, and men in it.

**KREM** — and behind him, the Brigands. Six, seven. Vorn among them. They have come for their ship's heart, and it is sitting on a bar next to the girl from the kitchen and a drunk in a leather jacket.

For one frozen instant, Ruthye and Krem see each other. Recognition — hers total, his a beat slower, then landing: *the daughter. The one who didn't flinch.* Something almost like a smile.

**KREM**

*(to Ruthye, delighted, terrible)* You came all this way.

The bar erupts.

**THE BRAWL — fast, ugly, and NOT a superhero fight:**

Ruthye is already moving for the crystal — Kara sweeps it off the bar and into her own jacket first, shoving Ruthye behind her with the other arm. *Protecting her,* the reflex that will define the whole film, starting here.

Kara comes off the stool. And here is the power rule, made flesh, no arithmetic required: she is *strong* — she catches the first Brigand's club and it stops like it hit a wall, she throws him and he goes *too far,* through a shutter, red light blasting in — but it costs her. She's slow to recover. She stumbles on the follow. Under a yellow sun this would be a comedy of easy dominance. Under this dead red one, she's a very tough, very drunk woman fighting seven ready men in a small room. She has residue. She does not have command.

Krypto is magnificent and idiotic — a white blur, latching onto arms, dragging men down, taking a chair across the back and not caring. He fights like loyalty with teeth.

Krem hangs back by the door. He's not here to brawl. He's here for the crystal, and he's watching to see where it went — his eyes on Kara's jacket.

A Brigand at the broken shutter raises a crossbow — a squat, ugly thing, its bolt tipped with something that beads wet and dark. He sights on Kara's back as she's tangled with two others.

**RUTHYE**

*(seeing it)* Behind — !

The bolt fires.

Kara turns — half a beat too slow, the drink, the red sun, her own hidden weeks costing her exactly this half-beat — 

— and Krypto is already in the air.

He takes it. The bolt meant for Kara buries in the white of his shoulder. He yelps — a sound that goes through the whole bar, through Kara, through everything — and drops, twisting, snapping at the shaft.

The world stops for exactly one person.

**KARA**

*No —*

Something that isn't drunk and isn't tired comes up out of her. She grabs the nearest thing — a heavy oak table, six-seater — and *throws* it. Not at a man. Through the *wall.* The whole side of the Long Dusk blows out into the red street, dust and splinters and daylight. It is one clean, terrifying flash of what she is, and it empties her — she staggers, catches herself on the bar, gasping.

It's enough. And it's not. Because the table that emptied her leaves her swaying, one hand on the bar — and when Krypto yelps again on the floor, her whole world collapses down to the dog. She drops to him, hands on the wound, everything else gone.

And that is when Krem moves.

He crosses the room, unhurried, stepping over a groaning Brigand. He crouches beside the gasping woman on her knees, close enough to be intimate, and he reaches into her torn jacket and takes the crystal — plucks his ship's heart back out from under the one person alive who could have stopped him, if she weren't a wreck of her own making.

Kara feels the hand. Half-turns. Reaches — and has *nothing.* No speed, no strength, the red sun and the bottle and the thrown table all coming due in the same instant. Her fingers close on air.

**KREM**

*(pocketing the drive core, almost gentle, crouched there between the dog and the broken woman)* There it is. That's all I came for.

*(he looks across at Ruthye, on her knees on the other side of the fallen dog)* Told you.

He stands. To his crew:

**KREM**

Out. We're done here.

They go — fast, disciplined, a raid crew's practiced withdrawal — out through the new hole in the wall, Krem last, the crystal in his coat. They don't need Kara's ship; they have their own — faster, crewed, already pointed at the sky, and now with its heart back in it. They got exactly what they came for: the one thing that had them pinned on Evelie. And they leave behind the only thing that will make the rest of the movie happen:

A dog on the floor with a Brigand bolt in him, and a woman who chose weakness bent over him, too weak by her own hand to have stopped any of it.

**INT. THE LONG DUSK — WRECKAGE — MOMENTS LATER**

Dust settling. Red light pours through the hole where a wall was. The patrons are gone or hiding. Gorl stands slowly behind the bar, all four eyes on the ruin.

Kara is on the floor with Krypto's head in her lap. The bolt is still in him. He's panting wrong — too fast, then too slow. Around the wound, the white fur is going a color fur shouldn't.

**KARA**

Okay. Okay, buddy, I've got you, don't — don't do that, keep breathing, that's an order —

She reaches for the bolt. Ruthye's hand catches her wrist. The kid is suddenly very calm and very fast.

**RUTHYE**

Don't pull it. If it's barbed you'll tear him worse. And it's poisoned — see the bead? You pull the shaft, you leave the poison and open the vein. Cut around it or leave it and get him somewhere that can.

Kara stares at her. The kid who baited the trap is also the kid who knows field medicine, because her people are farmers on a hard world and a hard world teaches you both.

**KARA**

*(a broken laugh, near tears)* Who *are* you.

**RUTHYE**

Someone whose father was a hunter. Where's the nearest bay?

Kara looks at Krypto. At the wound. At her own hands, which minutes ago threw a table through a wall and could not throw it half a second sooner.

**KARA**

My ship. It's close. It's got a medbay — it's junk, but it's a medbay.

She gets an arm under the dog. He's enormous; she's depleted; it's a struggle that should be nothing and isn't. Ruthye takes the weight of his hindquarters without being asked. Between the two of them — a drunk alien and a thirteen-year-old — they carry the last living piece of a dead world out through a hole in a wall, into the red.

Gorl watches them go. Looks at the knife Ruthye left him, handle-first. Looks at the wrecked bar. Sighs the sigh of a man adding it to a very long ledger.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. KARA'S SHIP — MEDBAY — CONTINUOUS**

Cramped. Half the panels dark. But it's a medbay. Krypto is on the slab, a scanning ring dropping over him, running amber and red. Kara works fast — she knows this machine, her hands are sure even when the rest of her isn't. Ruthye stands back, out of the way, watching an adult work.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Foreign compound in the bloodstream. It's binding to his cells faster than his body can clear it. I can slow it. I cannot stop it.

**KARA**

Slow it how much.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

*(a pause that is the ship being kind)* Enough to give him time. Not enough to give him a lot of it. He is a very strong animal, Kara. That's the only reason there's a number at all.

A readout resolves on the wall. Not a clean digital countdown — a *decay curve,* a line sloping down toward a shaded band marked, in three alphabets, the universal grammar of *too late.* No tidy seventy-two hours. Just a slope, and the pressure of a slope.

Ruthye reads it without being told how.

**RUTHYE**

It's not a clock. It's a hill.

**KARA**

*(grim)* Yeah. And we're on the wrong side of it.

She braces both hands on the slab, over the sleeping dog. Closes her eyes. And here she explains the rule — to Ruthye, and to us — not as a lecture but as a plan forming out loud in a panic.

**KARA**

Okay. Listen. Where I'm from — my kind, and him, he's from the same place — a red sun like this one, it makes us weak. Empties us out. Slower we go, weaker we get. It's why I'm — 

*(a bitter gesture at herself, the whole state of her)* — it's why I'm like this. I *picked* this light. Long story, bad reasons.

*(and now the hope)* But a *yellow* sun fills us back up. It's not medicine, exactly, it's more like — it's like he's a plant that's been in a closet. You put us under the right light and we don't just heal, we come *back.* If I can get him under a yellow sun, his body might burn that poison out on its own. It might just — fix him. Us.

**RUTHYE**

Where's the nearest yellow sun.

Kara's already at the cockpit door.

**KARA**

Far. Too far. But maybe not too far if I'm stupid about it.

**INT. KARA'S SHIP — COCKPIT — CONTINUOUS**

Kara drops into the pilot's seat, sweeps laundry off the console. Ruthye buckles in behind, the sword bundle across her knees. Krypto visible through the medbay hatch, the decay-curve glowing over him.

The console lights are a museum of problems. One in particular has clearly been blinking for a very long time, unheeded, with the specific patience of a thing that told you so.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Before we do anything ambitious, I feel I should note — as I have noted on eleven previous occasions — that the drive-seed is past service life and the reserve is —

**KARA**

I know.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

— low. It's very low, Kara.

**KARA**

*(strapping in)* I *know,* I've been meaning to —

**SHIP (V.O.)**

You've been meaning to for some time.

**KARA**

Plot me the closest G-type. The yellowest thing you've got. Then redline the jump.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

*(a beat)* The closest suitable star is well beyond our safe range on a healthy seed. On this one —

**KARA**

Redline it.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

*(quietly, doing as it's told)* Redlining. For the record, I'd like it known I raised concerns.

**KARA**

Noted. Loudly. Go.

She grabs the yoke. Through the canopy, the red sun. She points the old ship's nose *away* from it — away from the light that empties her — and lights the drive.

**EXT. SPACE — CONTINUOUS**

The little ship claws off Evelie and climbs, engine glow bruising from healthy orange toward a hot, sick, straining color. It leaps — a rough, ugly jump, not the clean silver leap of a good ship, more like a stone skipped by a tired arm.

**INT. KARA'S SHIP — COCKPIT — CONTINUOUS**

Everything shakes. Warnings stack up faster than the ship can voice them. Kara flies it like she's holding a wound closed with her hands. Behind her, Ruthye is white-knuckled but silent — reading Kara, not the instruments. Learning her.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Drive-seed temperature is climbing past —

**KARA**

Come on. Come on, old girl, one good jump, that's all I'm asking —

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Kara.

**KARA**

Don't.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

We are not going to make the star.

**KARA**

*Don't —*

Something under the deck lets go. A deep, wrong *crunch,* felt more than heard. The straining engine-color guts out. The whole ship yaws, the star ahead — a tiny, indifferent yellow point, so far, *right there and impossibly far* — sliding out of the canopy as they lose the line.

Kara fights it, fights it, and there is nothing to fight it with. The drive is dead. She sags. Behind her Ruthye watches an adult run out of options and not fall apart — file that, too.

Silence, except alarms.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

*(gently)* The drive-seed is gone. I'm sorry. There's a service waypoint within reach on maneuvering thrusters. It's the only thing within reach on maneuvering thrusters.

Kara stares at the little yellow star they didn't get to. In the medbay behind them, the decay-curve keeps sloping. She presses the heels of her hands into her eyes.

**KARA**

*(hollow)* Course to the waypoint.

**SHIP (V.O.)**

Setting course. It's called Meridian.

*(a pause, an old ship trying to help)* They'll have a doctor.

Kara doesn't answer. She turns and looks back through the hatch at Krypto, at the hill they're on the wrong side of. Ruthye is looking at her.

**RUTHYE**

*(not unkind — an observation)* You did everything right. It just wasn't enough.

**KARA**

*(a breath of a laugh, no humor)* Kid. Story of my whole life.

The dead ship drifts, then swings, limping on thrusters toward a distant smudge of fluorescent light in the middle of absolutely nowhere.

**DISSOLVE TO:**

**EXT. MERIDIAN WAYSTATION — ESTABLISHING**

And the palette *detonates* into color.

After the embalmed red of Evelie and the black of space — a riot. MERIDIAN WAYSTATION hangs in the void like a truck stop that ate a carnival: a great fluorescent snarl of docking arms, neon vendor signage in forty languages, refuel booms, a rotating ring that is either a hotel or a hospital or both. Ships of impossible shapes queue at pumps. A tow-drone hauls a dead freighter past a sign that says, cheerfully, in the universal font of institutions:

**WELCOME TO MERIDIAN — "THE HALFWAY EVERYWHERE" — MIND THE GAP, MIND THE GRAV, HAVE YOUR CHITS READY**

There is no sun here. No red, no yellow. Just commerce, buzzing, in the dark. Life insisting on itself in the most ridiculous forms it can manage.

Kara's sad little ship limps into a dock cradle among giants.

**INT. MERIDIAN — DOCKING BAY / REPAIR APRON — CONTINUOUS**

Grease, steam, the shriek of tools. A MECHANIC — VESK, a large, unhurried, many-jointed being who speaks slowly because everything about them is slow — runs a scanner along Kara's opened drive housing. Kara hovers. Ruthye takes in the enormity of the place with wide eyes she's trying to keep narrow.

**VESK**

*(slow, almost gentle, terrible news delivered like weather)* Mm. Yeah. Seed's cooked. Cooked all the way. See how it's gone glassy? That's cooked.

**KARA**

Okay, so replace it. I'll pay — I'll figure out the pay, just — how fast?

**VESK**

Oh, we don't *stock* 'em. Nobody stocks 'em. You don't *keep* a drive-seed, they go off.

**KARA**

Then where —

**VESK**

We *grow* it.

*(he says it like it's the good news)* Got a vat right over there. Beautiful vat. You put a starter in, you keep it warm, you keep it fed, and in — 

*(he considers, a being for whom time is soup)* — oh. Nine days? Eleven, if she's fussy. Then she's ready. Then you fly.

Kara closes her eyes. The cruelty of it is almost comic: the part *does* grow. On trees, practically. Just not in Krypto's window.

**KARA**

I don't have nine days. I have — 

*(she doesn't have a number and that's worse)* — I have a lot less than nine days.

**VESK**

*(sympathetic, immovable)* Vat doesn't care what you have. Vat has its own opinions.

*(he pats the housing, not unkindly)* I'll start one growing. Costs the same whether you're here for it or not. Clinic's up the ring. You'll want the clinic.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. MERIDIAN — DOCK CLINIC — CONTINUOUS**

Cleaner than the bay, but not by much. Fluorescent, humming, a row of stabilizing tubes along one wall — great vertical cylinders of faintly glowing amber fluid, a couple occupied by shapes we don't look at too closely. A DOCK-HEALER — DR. SILT, brisk, six-fingered, a bedside manner sanded smooth by ten thousand doomed travelers — reads Krypto's chart off a floating slate. Krypto lies sedated on a gurney, breathing in little assisted hitches.

**DR. SILT**

Your ship's diagnosis is correct as far as it goes. Poison, binding, decay. But your ship couldn't name it. I can.

*(a look at Kara that has done this before)* This is Brigand work. The bolt-tip compound. I'd know it anywhere — I pull it out of people who've been on the wrong end of a raid about twice a month.

**KARA**

Then you've got the — you can treat it. You have the —

**DR. SILT**

No.

*(flat, kind, a professional's mercy of not dragging it out)* There's an antidote. There's exactly one. It's not something I can synthesize and it's not something a yellow sun burns out — I'm sorry, I can see you hoping, it isn't. The compound's engineered specifically so sunlight and strong constitutions don't clear it. That's the *point* of it. It drops things that are hard to drop.

Kara's last hope — the sun, the thing she understands — goes out. Ruthye watches it go out of her.

**KARA**

Then where's the antidote.

**DR. SILT**

On a Brigand. A *ranking* one. They carry doses for themselves — their own weapons are as dangerous to them as to anyone. Friendly fire, bad handling. A crew leader always has one on him.

*(she says the next part like closing a chart)* So if your dog was shot by a Brigand crew, the cure is walking around on the neck of whoever leads it. That's the whole prescription. I don't have a better one. Nobody does.

Kara and Ruthye look at each other. The same name in both their heads.

**RUTHYE**

*(quietly)* Krem.

**DR. SILT**

*(already moving on, gesturing at the amber tubes)* I can put the animal in stasis. Slow the hill to a crawl. It buys you time — real time — but understand what it buys and what it doesn't. It buys hours. Some hours. It does not buy nine days.

*(the exact, awful shape of it)* Your ship needs longer to heal than your dog has. That's your problem in one sentence. I didn't build the problem. I'm just the one who has to say it out loud.

Kara goes to Krypto. Puts her forehead down against his. He's out, but his tail moves once, weakly, at her smell. She stays there a moment. When she straightens, something in her has changed register — the drunk fog is burning off, replaced by something harder and more frightened and, for the first time, *sober.*

**KARA**

*(to Krypto, barely a whisper)* I'm gonna go get it. I'm gonna come back with it. That's a promise. I don't — I know I'm bad at those lately. This one I keep.

She turns to Dr. Silt.

**KARA**

Put him in the tube.

*(and, quieter, the first thing she's chosen instead of avoided in the whole film)* I'll bring back the antidote or I won't come back.

Dr. Silt nods, unsurprised — she's heard it before, from people who didn't mean it. She looks at Kara's face and revises: this one might.

They lower Krypto into the amber. The tube seals. The decay-curve, projected small on its side, flattens — not stopped. *Slowed.* Still sloping. Just less.

Ruthye stands beside Kara, both of them watching the white dog float in the amber light, the way you'd watch a fire you're not allowed to put out yet.

**RUTHYE**

So we go to Krem.

**KARA**

*(and this is the pivot — she doesn't say yes to revenge; she says yes to the dog)* We go to the antidote. It happens to be on Krem. I want that very clear, between us. I'm not here for a killing. I'm here for a cure.

**RUTHYE**

*(a fact, no argument yet)* The cure's on a man I have a Writ against.

**KARA**

*(tired, honest)* Yeah. I noticed the universe doing that. It does that.

*(she looks at the tube one more time)* Come on. If we're chasing him, we can't fly. So we do the stupidest thing two people ever did.

**RUTHYE**

Which is.

**KARA**

We take the bus.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. MERIDIAN — TRANSIT CONCOURSE — CONTINUOUS**

The whimsy engine, at full throttle. If the clinic was the film's dread, this is its delight — and the two share a wall, which is the whole trick: comedy against a deadline, not instead of one.

A vast fluorescent bazaar-terminal. A DEPARTURE BOARD the size of a barn flips through destinations with a satisfying analog clatter — half of them place-names, half of them apparently just warnings. Travelers of every possible design queue, nap, argue, eat. A six-armed vendor works a noodle wok with all six arms and a seventh appendage we decide not to identify. A family of small round beings rolls past in a tidy line, each holding the one in front. Somebody's luggage is alive and unhappy about it.

Ruthye has never been off her own world. She is trying, valiantly, to look unimpressed, and failing at the seams — her eyes going everywhere, snagging on a creature here, a sign there, a smell she can't place. It's all instantly recognizable — a bus station is a bus station across the whole universe — and all completely, wonderfully strange.

Kara moves through it like a local. This is the tell the whole film plants: she is *comfortable* here. The chaos of the universe is her element. She knows which line moves and which vendor to trust. She is the alien everywhere except the one place she's from.

**KARA**

*(over her shoulder, threading the crowd)* Stay close, don't make eye contact with the tall thin ones, and if a customs drone asks if you're carrying fruit, the answer is no even if the answer is yes.

**RUTHYE**

*(hurrying to keep up)* Why —

**KARA**

*(already elsewhere)* Information desk. We need a route.

**INT. MERIDIAN — INFORMATION DESK — CONTINUOUS**

A long counter under a colossal, backlit PUBLIC SPACEMAP — a glowing web of routes, stars, transit lines, advisories in cheerful hazard-yellow. Behind it, an ATTENDANT — POE, a weary, deadpan being in a transit-authority vest, who has given every possible piece of bad news to every possible traveler and found it changes nothing.

Ruthye sets the tracker on the counter and thumbs it awake.

The needle swings. Ruthye, with the unshowy competence that is her whole character, lines the tracker's bearing up against the great glowing map — no gloating, just hands and geometry, proving something without a word. The bearing crosses the map and lands on a single point.

A point ringed in hazard-yellow. With a small, insistent, blinking advisory icon.

Poe watches the tracker point at it. Poe's whole body sighs.

**POE**

You don't want to go there.

**KARA**

What is it?

**POE**

*(not even looking; they know it by heart)* Bilki. Was. Depending who you ask, it *was* the Brigand homeworld. Advisory's been standing so long the advisory's got an advisory.

*(reciting)* Collapsed biosphere. Nuclear winter. Atmosphere rated do-not-breathe, surface rated do-not-land, biosphere rated do-not-hope. No permanent population. No services. No reason.

Ruthye puts one finger on the map, on the route line that threads out toward the hazard-ringed dot.

**RUTHYE**

Is it still on the drop-off route?

Poe hates the question because Poe knows the answer. Poe looks at the thirteen-year-old asking it. Poe looks at the drunk-looking woman beside her. Poe experiences the specific despair of an employee who cannot, technically, refuse.

**POE**

*(pained)* ...Technically.

Reaction beat — held a good three seconds longer than plot efficiency demands, Wes Anderson patience:

Ruthye, expectant, wide-eyed, vindicated.

Kara, beside her, breaking into a triumphantly goofy, wildly inappropriate grin — the grin of someone whose terrible plan has a terrible next step, which is the only thing worse than no next step at all.

**POE**

*(flatly, sliding two paper chits across)* Loading in forty minutes. Gate nine. The drop-shell's rated for toxic atmosphere and plausible deniability. Please retain your chit. There are no refunds and there is, obviously, no return leg.

**KARA**

*(taking the chits, still grinning like a lunatic)* Perfect. Great. We'll take two.

**POE**

*(a small human mercy, to Ruthye, quietly)* ...Kid. You don't have to.

Ruthye picks up the tracker. Switches it off. Pockets it.

**RUTHYE**

*(not unkind)* I know.

She walks toward gate nine. Which is the whole tragedy — she *knows,* and she goes anyway, because the Writ said so, and the Writ is easier to carry than the grief it's standing in for.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. MERIDIAN — WASHROOM — A LITTLE LATER**

Fluorescent, tiled in some ceramic that isn't ceramic. A row of basins and mirrors. The universe's plumbing is, blessedly, universal.

Kara and Ruthye at adjacent basins, washing off the road. Kara looks *wrecked* in the flat light — every mile of the last week on her face. She splashes water, drinks from the tap, catches her own reflection and looks away from it.

Beside her, Ruthye scrubs her hands, her face. And in the mirror, without the door-scanning, without the code, without an audience she's aware of — for the first time in the whole film — she stops looking like a soldier.

She looks thirteen. She looks like a kid who is a very long way from home and would like her mother.

The armor drops in the reflection, and Kara — who has been not-looking at her own face — sees it happen in the glass.

No speech. Kara doesn't turn. She just reaches over and puts a hand on Ruthye's shoulder, in the mirror-world and the real one at once.

Ruthye doesn't lean into it. Doesn't pull away either. A single tear gets loose and runs. She lets it, because she doesn't seem to have noticed it, because she's very far away.

Kara reaches over and wipes it away with her thumb. Still says nothing. There is nothing to say that wouldn't make it worse.

A beat. Two people in a mirror, a long way from anywhere.

Then Ruthye blinks, and the door-scanning comes back into her eyes, and the mask goes on — not cynically, just the way you pick up a tool you need. She dries her hands. Squares up. Walks out, composed as a judge.

Kara watches her go. Looks back at her own reflection — decidedly less composed. Splashes one more handful of water. Follows.

**INT. MERIDIAN — CONCOURSE / VENDORS — CONTINUOUS**

Errands. The mundane holy work of a road trip. Kara pays for things with a dwindling handful of chits and a wince at each price — the dock, the diagnosis, the tickets, and now supplies, and she is visibly nearly out.

The dynamic is parent-dragging-teenager-through-a-terminal — except the teenager has a sword bundle on her back and a Writ in her heart.

**KARA**

Water. Ration bars — the gray ones, not the blue, the blue ones are a mistake I've made. Something that isn't a ration bar —

**RUTHYE**

*(chewing, guarded)* ...These are acceptable.

**KARA**

High praise.

At the next stall — a bin of small, cheerfully-colored plastic rings, a bored vendor, a handwritten sign: **PERSONAL ATMOSPHERE — 1 RING = 1 EMERGENCY.** Kara plucks one out, checks the seal date with a practiced eye, pays, and tosses it to Ruthye, who catches it on reflex.

**RUTHYE**

What's this.

**KARA**

Bubble suit. You crack the ring, it deploys a personal atmosphere around you. Toxic air, no air, whatever. One use.

*(a glib little shrug, already walking)* Now you won't die in space. You're welcome.

**RUTHYE**

*(to Kara's back, quietly, testing the shape of the words)* Thank you.

Kara, three steps ahead, doesn't hear it, or pretends not to. But something in her walk eases. The ice cracks — not through a heart-to-heart, but through two people becoming *useful* to each other before they're ready to be kind.

Ruthye pulls out the great glowing spacemap chit and reads it while they walk, correcting Kara's turn twice without comment. She reads star maps better than Kara does, and she doesn't gloat about it. Kara notices, and lets her navigate.

**INT. MERIDIAN — GATE NINE — MOMENTS LATER**

A boarding gate like any boarding gate. A tired rope line. A vehicle beyond the window that is, unmistakably, a *bus* — long, dented, many-windowed, a great public omnibus of deep space with a route number lit on its brow and a company name down its flank:

**THE LONG LINE — "WE GO WHERE THE MAP GOES"**

**INT. MERIDIAN — GATE NINE — BULLETIN BOARD — CONTINUOUS**

And we do not follow them.

Krem.

**HARD CUT — BLACK.**

**INT. MERIDIAN — INFORMATION DESK — LATER (UNSPECIFIED)**

Poe, at the desk. Poe looks up. And keeps looking up. And up.

**LOBO (O.S.)**

*(a voice like a truck downshifting, genial as a landslide)* These guys.

**POE**

*(swallowing)* ...The, ah. The Brigands. Bounty's still open, if you've got —

**LOBO (O.S.)**

Where.

**POE**

**LOBO (O.S.)**

**POE**

**CUT TO:**

**INT. THE LONG LINE (BUS) — TRAVELING — LATER**

The interior of an intergalactic bus, which is to say: exactly like every bus, and nothing like any bus. Rows of mismatched seats. Overhead nets stuffed with alien luggage. A flickering sign that dings and lights a symbol that might mean STOP REQUESTED or might mean PRAY NOW. Fluorescent tubes, one of them buzzing.

Kara is asleep against the window, jacket pulled up like a blanket, the way she sleeps everywhere — badly. Beside her, in the aisle seat, Ruthye sits bolt upright, moon-figs long gone, watching the strange passengers with the vigilance of a bodyguard.

Behind Kara's eyes —

**FLASH — ARGO. (STILL FRAGMENTS. STILL WITHHELD.)**

— the sky like cracking glaze —

— Alura's mouth, shaping a word, no sound —

— a hand on the inside of pod-glass —

Kara startles awake with a small strangled sound. For a second, that lost look — *where* — then the buzzing fluorescent, the lavender steam, the bus. She scrubs her face.

And, reflexively — the gesture we know from the very first morning, the one the whole film has taught us to read — her hand goes into the jacket and comes out with a flask.

She thumbs it open. Lifts it.

**RUTHYE**

*(not looking at her, looking out at the aisle)* You should stop that.

Not a plea. Not a lecture. A flat, matter-of-fact statement, the way you'd tell someone their shoelace was untied. The child, wiser than the adult, again.

Kara stops. Not quite mid-swig. She lowers the flask, caught, a little embarrassed — a grown woman corrected by a thirteen-year-old and knowing the thirteen-year-old is right.

She caps it. Holds it in her lap. And in the silence that follows — the bus humming, the lavender steaming, the strange dark rolling past the windows — she talks.

Not a monologue. Not the whole thing. Pieces, offered into the middle distance, because it's easier to say to a bus window than to a face.

**KARA**

They tell it like it was fast. When they tell it — my cousin, the ones who write it down. Like the world just — *went,* one bright second, everybody brave.

*(a small, wrong laugh)* It wasn't fast.

**RUTHYE**

*(quietly, not turning)* No?

**KARA**

The sky started going wrong first. Weeks. You'd look up and it was the wrong — you can't even say what's wrong about a sky until it's your own sky doing it. Then the ground. It shook for *days.* Not one big — days. You'd get used to it and then be ashamed you'd gotten used to it.

*(the flask turning over and over in her hands)* People I knew went in ones and twos. A neighbor. Then a street. You'd think, it's bad, but it's not — and then it was all at once, the last part, all at once. The *sound* of it. The — 

*(she stops. Some things don't have words and she doesn't reach for them)* — you don't need the rest.

Ruthye has turned now. She's watching Kara's profile. She does not interrupt. She does not compare her father's death to a planet. She just *listens* — the exact way Kara listened to the code-speech in the bar, the way you listen to recognize a thing, not to fix it.

**KARA**

My parents put me in the pod. I didn't want to go. I wanted to — 

*(and here, right at the edge of the full memory, she pulls back; the film is still saving it)* — it doesn't matter. They made me go. And there's a silence after a pod launches. You're out, and it's quiet, and it stays quiet, and you understand the quiet is going to be permanent.

*(she finally looks at Ruthye)* That's the part nobody warns you about. Not the fire. The quiet after.

Beat. The bus hums. And then Kara says the thing she came on this whole trip to be able to say — quiet, direct, no theory in it.

**KARA**

I've been down your road, Ruthye.

Ruthye goes still.

**KARA**

Revenge won't make you feel better.

She doesn't explain it. Doesn't say who, or when, or whether it was deserved, or how it hollowed her out and left the drinking and the red light where a person used to be. The audience doesn't need the file. What matters is that this isn't philosophy. She's not on the moral high ground telling a child a rule.

She's a woman on the *other side* of the thing, sending a message back across the water: *don't.* I went. It's not what you think is over there.

Ruthye says nothing. She has no answer, because there isn't one, because you can't be argued out of grief. But she can't unhear it, either. The category she'd filed Kara in — *drunk with opinions* — doesn't fit anymore, and she knows it.

Unconsciously — she's not even aware she's doing it — Kara has taken the flask out again. She looks at it. A long, hard look. The whole weight of the morning-ritual, the whole practice of not-feeling, sitting in a dented steel flask in her palm.

She puts it away. This time on purpose.

Neither of them marks it out loud. But Ruthye saw. And the scene doesn't resolve — it just sits there between them, the confession, the road going on underneath.

After a while Ruthye turns back to her window. And we finally look where she's looking.

**RUTHYE'S POV — THE WINDOW:**

We hadn't noticed. It's *beautiful.* The bus is threading a nebula — great slow curtains of color, rose and gold and deep electric blue, stars scattered through it like seed. A universe absolutely lousy with wonder.

And, held in the same frame: the cold black emptiness between all of it. The distance. The indifference. Beauty and void in one window, and you don't get to have one without the other.

Ruthye watches it. Thirteen years old, off her world for the first time, a sword on her back and a Writ in her chest and a woman beside her who came back from the place she's going.

Hold on the window. The colors slide past. The bus hums on toward the dead world.

**DISSOLVE TO:**

**INT. THE LONG LINE (BUS) — FORWARD — "ARRIVAL"**

The buzzing fluorescents flicker. The STOP REQUESTED / PRAY NOW sign dings. The DRIVER-DRONE's voice comes over a speaker that has hated every word it's ever said.

**DRIVER-DRONE (V.O.)**

Final scheduled drop. Bilki. Passengers for Bilki, ready your descent shell. The Long Line reminds you that this stop is served on an advisory basis and that the Long Line assumes no responsibility for advisory-basis stops. Thank you for riding the Long Line.

The lavender couple across the aisle turn, slowly, and stare at Kara and Ruthye. Nobody gets off at Bilki. Nobody has ever gotten off at Bilki.

Kara and Ruthye stand, gather their things. Every eye on the bus follows them down the aisle — the way a whole diner watches somebody walk out into a storm.

**INT./EXT. THE LONG LINE (BUS) — DROP-SHELL BAY — CONTINUOUS**

A hatch in the belly of the bus. And bolted to the underside, a clear DROP-SHELL — a transparent bubble the size of a small room, half escape pod, half luggage chute, its interior spartan and its stenciling honest:

**DESCENT SHELL — RATED FOR TOXIC ATMOSPHERE & PLAUSIBLE DENIABILITY — OCCUPANCY: OPTIMISTIC**

Through its clear floor, far below: the planet.

Bilki looks dead from orbit. A whole world the color of a bruise — total gray cloud, no seam of blue anywhere, no lights of cities. Lightning walks around under the cloud-deck, lighting it from within like a rotten lantern. In two or three places, tiny and awful, the pinprick glow of old fires still burning in the dark, years on.

Ruthye looks at it through the clear floor. Even she is quiet.

Kara looks at the shell. Then out the bus's forward port, then back, doing the obvious arithmetic, and asks the driver the obvious question.

**KARA**

Wait. How do we get picked up?

The DRIVER-DRONE, already reaching for the eject control, gives the answer of every bus-terminal employee since the beginning of buses: a shrug. A pure, mechanical, load-bearing shrug.

Kara opens her mouth. The driver presses the control.

**EXT. DROP-SHELL — FALLING — CONTINUOUS**

The shell releases. Bus and shell separate, the great lit bus wheeling away above them into the dark, indifferent, on to somewhere with a return leg.

And for one clean, impossible second, as the shell falls free and turns —

— the light of a distant YELLOW SUN, somewhere out past Bilki's poisoned sky, catches the curve of the clear shell. A thread of gold. A tiny glint of *life,* riding on the surface of the thing carrying them down into death.

Kara sees it. And she does something small and childish and unbearably human: she presses her hand flat against the clear shell, against the glint, palm to the glass — not because it will work, not because a hand can drink starlight through a bubble, but because she *needs* it to, because she is depleted and frightened and there is the sun, right there, and she can't reach it.

Her hand, on the glass, reaching for a light she can't have. The recurring image. Barriers filter the sun's gift.

Ruthye watches her do it. Doesn't say anything. But she sees it — sees the woman who threw a table through a wall reduced to pressing her hand against a window for a scrap of light — and she files it, the way she files everything. It changes how she reads Kara. It's part of why, soon, she'll do the thing she does.

The glint slides away as the shell turns. The gray sky comes up to swallow them. Kara lowers her hand.

Ruthye takes out the tracker. Thumbs it awake.

The needle swings. Steadies.

It points straight *down.*

**RUTHYE**

*(quiet)* He's here.

And it's close. The dot isn't far, which is worse — close means no waiting, no plan, no catching their breath. Close means it happens now.

As they drop through the first tearing wisps of gray cloud, something crosses the sky beneath them — a fast blue arc, a line of that same impossible blue flame, streaking toward the same patch of dead world their tracker points at, faster than they're falling, faster than they can afford to think about.

Kara and Ruthye look at each other through the murk. Another mystery, moving faster than either of them can spend the worry on.

**KARA**

*(watching the blue arc go)* ...Please be on our side.

**RUTHYE**

*(watching it too)* Or his.

The shell falls into the gray. The last of the yellow glint is gone. There is only the bruise-colored world coming up, the lightning walking under the clouds, the old fires burning, and the two of them, dropping into it.

**FADE THROUGH GRAY.**

**EXT. BILKI — SURFACE — CONTINUOUS**

The shell settles onto dead ground with a hiss and cracks open. Its cheerful little chime — *arrival!* — is obscene here.

Kara and Ruthye step out into it, and the film drains of every color it had at Meridian. This is the gray movement of the palette, and it lands *because* we just spent twenty minutes in a universe bursting with absurd life. Here: none.

Ash for soil. A sky of low, boiling gray, lightning stalking through it, never quite arriving. The air is a filthy dusk that never resolves into day or night. Skeletal trees, or the mineral memory of trees. Somewhere, always, the low tectonic grumble of a world still dying by inches, years after the killing blow.

It looks, precisely, like Argo at the end — except Argo was a moment and this is a *whole planet,* a moment that won and kept the field.

Kara stops. Just for a second. It hits her like a wall — this is the inside of her own nightmare, rendered as landscape, as far as she can see in every direction. Everywhere she goes, worlds are ending.

And she is in the worst possible shape to be standing in it. No sun to draw on. The road's exhaustion. The flask put away — and the body noticing, a fine tremor in her hands that isn't only fear. The medbay hill still sloping somewhere behind her eyes. All of it.

Ruthye is already reading the tracker, already moving.

**RUTHYE**

This way. It's close.

**KARA**

*(catching her arm)* Slow. This isn't a place you *walk* into.

She's right. As they move into the fringe of it — old BRIGAND INFRASTRUCTURE resolves out of the murk: a supply depot half-swallowed by ash, a bunker line, the spine of some transit rail, all of it built by a people who learned to move cargo long after they forgot how to make a home. Everything here is for storage, transport, defense. Nothing here is for living.

And it isn't dead. Not all of it.

A dry click, somewhere ahead. Then a whine, spinning up.

**KARA**

Down —

An old PERIMETER TURRET, coughing back to life on ancient automatic protocols, opens up. Not a trap Krem set — a reflex of the dead world itself, a machine with no ideology and no mercy, doing the one thing it was built to do to anything warm that approaches.

And here it is — the beat the whole trail exists to house. **Kara protects Ruthye,** and it is not clean, and it is not super, and it costs.

Kara throws herself over Ruthye, wrapping the kid completely in her arms and her body and the leather jacket, and takes the turret-burst across her own back. She grunts — it *hurts,* the rounds don't bounce off cinematically, they hammer her, they tear the jacket, they draw blood. She's tough enough to survive what would shred a human. She is not tough enough for it to be nothing.

**KARA**

*(through her teeth, hauling Ruthye)* Move — behind the — go, go —

She drags Ruthye behind a slab of broken foundation. The turret chews the far side of it. Kara's back is a mess. The jacket is worse — a long tear opening down the shoulder, the tough old hide giving up.

**RUTHYE**

*(furious, shoving at her)* I had cover, I was *going* to —

**KARA**

You were going to run into a firing line.

**RUTHYE**

I can —

**KARA**

*Stay behind me.*

More guns wake — the whole perimeter picking up the first one's alarm, a line of old muzzles coughing awake down the depot wall. The ground ahead starts to jump, dirt-geysers walking toward them: pressure mines, seeded by nobody, triggering on their own decayed logic.

There's no clever way through it. There's only Kara's body between the kid and the fire.

Kara gets Ruthye onto her back — a piggyback, undignified, desperate — and *runs.* And this is the power rule made visible one more time: it is not superspeed. It's a ruined, staggering approximation, a depleted woman sprinting a gauntlet she could have ignored at full strength, stumbling, catching herself, shielding Ruthye's head with one hand while the ground detonates under them and shrapnel opens new lines across her arms and back.

She takes the hits so Ruthye takes none. She runs through a place that is trying, mechanically and impersonally, to kill them both, and she keeps one child alive across it with nothing but her own increasingly broken body.

They slam down behind a bunker wall. The mine-field spends itself behind them. The turrets lose the angle and quiet, hunting.

Kara is heaving. Bleeding from a dozen places. The jacket hangs off her in strips now — no longer a cool costume, just a ragged hide she's still, somehow, wearing, still clinging to.

Ruthye slides off her back. And she is not grateful. Her face is tight and hot and *ashamed,* which comes out as anger, because that's the only door it has.

**RUTHYE**

Stop doing that.

**KARA**

*(spitting grit)* You're welcome.

**RUTHYE**

I didn't come here to be *carried.* I came to do a thing. And every time you throw yourself in front of me you make me — 

*(the word costs her)* — small. You make me a *child.* I stopped being a child in my own kitchen.

Kara looks at her, chest heaving, blood on her teeth. There's a whole answer to that and no breath to give it. So she gives the short one.

**KARA**

Yeah, well. You are one. Both things are true.

Before Ruthye can fire back —

Ahead, past the depot, past the bunker line: a sudden orange bloom against the gray. A BOOM that isn't a mine or a turret — bigger, wetter, wrong. And on its heels, carried thin across the ash:

Gunfire. And screams. And — impossibly, in this dead place — *laughter.* Someone up there is having the time of their life.

Something alive is tearing through the Brigands. And it's between them and Krem.

Kara and Ruthye look at each other. The blue arc from the sky. The thing that overtook them falling. *That.*

Kara wipes her mouth. Gets Ruthye back onto her back — Ruthye lets her, this once, because the ground between here and there is still hostile and even Ruthye can do that math — and Kara stands, swaying, running on nothing but the promise she made to a dog in a tube.

**KARA**

*(to herself, a prayer, a joke, both)* Please be on our side.

She runs the last stretch — barely upright, heaving against the whole weight of the dead world, explosions licking the gray, flames throwing the only real color for miles — toward the noise, and the laughing, and whatever is making it.

**SMASH CUT TO:**

**EXT. BILKI — BRIGAND DEPOT — CONTINUOUS**

And here is the carnage the movie has, for two hours, refused to give us. Here it is, all at once, delivered by the most likable man in the picture.

**LOBO.**

Enormous. Chalk-white, red-eyed, black-clad, chain-and-hook slung over one shoulder, cigar clamped in a grin, boots planted in a courtyard of dead-world wreckage that he is *rapidly redecorating.* He is, at this exact moment, holding a screaming Brigand by the ankle in each hand, and he brings them together like cymbals.

He is having a wonderful time.

**LOBO**

*(to the sky, to the world, to no one)* Aw, come *on,* ya buncha nobodies, I came all this way — where's the fun ones? Where's the guy who fights *back?*

The Brigands — a dozen of them, dug into the depot, the same crew and the same numbers that pinned a full-strength Kara would've been nothing to — are, against Lobo, simply *insects being cleared.* They fire everything. It does nothing. He walks through it, cigar first, cheerful.

He rips through them the way a man swats flies. And the film shoots it exactly as the bible demands: no needle-drop, no slow-motion glory, no cool. Just the awful frictionlessness of it. The ease is the horror. The most fun character in the movie doing the least fun thing in it, and the camera declining, pointedly, to celebrate.

Kara staggers to the edge of it, Ruthye sliding off her back. They both stop. They both stare. Because a part of everyone watching — a part of *them* — thrills to it, and is meant to, and is meant to be ashamed of the thrill about ninety seconds from now.

**LOBO**

*(catching a charging Brigand's swing without looking, almost bored)* Nothin' personal.

*(he isn't lying and that's the horror — it genuinely isn't)*

**A ONE-LINER, A GRIN, A KILL. Then another. He works.**

Kara grabs Ruthye's shoulder, pulls her low behind cover at the courtyard's edge. Whatever this is, you do not walk into the middle of it.

**KARA**

*(low, wrecked)* Do not move. Do not — whatever that is, we wait for it to be over.

Ruthye doesn't answer. She's scanning the carnage. Looking for one face in it.

And then — **THE ACTION COMICS SHOT.** The homage. The one the trailers would use, if this were the wrong movie.

Lobo stoops and *lifts a Brigand troop-hauler over his head* — a whole armored transport, wheels spinning — and holds it there, arms locked, silhouetted against a slow-blooming explosion behind him, flame licking gold around his enormous frame. It is glorious. It is iconic. It is, for one held breath, *exactly* the image a hundred superhero posters have sold us as triumph.

He grins around the cigar.

He throws it down.

And the camera does not look away. The hauler comes down on the last knot of Brigands with a *grisly, wet, slick crunch* — and there were people in the cab. Blood sheets down the inside of the windshield. The glorious shot has a bill, and the film makes us pay it, right now, in the same breath as the thrill.

The thrill curdles. Exactly as designed.

He's working through the stragglers now. One Brigand, both legs gone, drags himself through the ash, weeping, reaching for nothing.

**BRIGAND**

*(barely)* Please. *Please —*

Lobo ambles over. Looks down at him the way you'd look at an ant on a picnic blanket. Almost kind.

**LOBO**

*(genially)* You're welcome.

He steps on him. It's over. He doesn't even watch it happen — he's already scanning for the next one, whistling something tuneless.

We are, by now, thoroughly queasy. The uncomfortable laughter has stopped being comfortable. The corpses are a *pile.* This is what the fight would look like if Supergirl didn't have a code. This is a Supergirl movie with the code taken out. It's grisly. It sucks. That's the entire point, and it's landed.

And now, at the far side of the courtyard, Lobo reaches the last one.

**KREM.**

Lobo has Krem by the collar, dangling him off the ground one-handed, the way you'd hold a kitten by the scruff — and Krem, the evangelist of cruelty, the man who taught a thirteen-year-old that the world is a slaughterhouse, is *sniveling.*

**KREM**

Please — please, I have — I can pay, I have gold, I have a whole — please, I don't want to — 

**LOBO**

*(mildly interested)* Y'know what, buddy? Nobody does.

And here is the turn the film has been setting up since a kitchen table: the instant Krem's own creed is applied to *him,* it collapses. The man who said *this is what the world is* like it made him strong is, it turns out, a coward. His strength was always a costume. When the world finally comes for Krem, he begs. He is not the monster from Ruthye's kitchen anymore, and he will never quite be that man again. Something in him has already died on the end of Lobo's arm.

Lobo cocks the hook back, casual, meaningless — one more corpse on a planet made of them.

**RUTHYE (O.S.)**

*(ringing across the courtyard)* You can't kill him!

Everything stops.

Lobo turns. Kara turns — *no, Ruthye, no* — too slow, too wrecked, still catching her breath at the courtyard's edge. Ruthye has stepped out from cover. Small, filthy, sword-bundle on her back, staring down the single most dangerous being on the planet.

**LOBO**

*(genuinely puzzled, Krem still dangling)* ...Say what now?

Ruthye walks toward him. Every instinct in the frame says *don't,* and she does it anyway, and she does it citing the one thing she has.

**RUTHYE**

He's mine. By the Writ of my people, that man's life is a debt owed to me. He killed my father. In front of me. On purpose. The law says the blood is mine to collect and *no one else's.*

*(she stops, feet planted)* So you can't kill him. He's *mine.*

A long, strange beat. Lobo looks at this thirteen-year-old who just told the Main Man to hold. And something shifts in his face — the goon-grin drops, and underneath it is something almost grave. Because of everything in the universe, the one currency Lobo actually respects is a code, and this child just spent it.

**LOBO**

*(setting Krem down without looking, keeping a boot on him — quiet, serious, no joke in it)* Kid. Lemme tell ya somethin'.

*(and he means every word)* Anybody looks at me funny, I kill 'em. That's my rule. Simple. Keeps the day movin'.

*(a beat)* But a *code?* Havin' a code — a real one, one you'd *bleed* for — that's worth more than all the corpses on this whole miserable rock.

He looks around at the pile he made, as if noticing it, as if measuring it against the small girl in front of him, and finding the small girl heavier.

Then — the grin comes back. The Main Man reasserts himself. But it's warmer now, weirdly, almost avuncular.

**LOBO**

You got *moxie.* I like moxie. And I can tell — 

*(a nudge of his boot at the whimpering Krem)* — this guy's been eatin' you up inside. Real bad. Little worm like him, livin' in your head rent-free? Nah. That's no way to go through the cosmos.

*(magnanimous, terrible)* So here. Today's your lucky day. I'm basically done here anyway — bounty's a bounty, dead's dead. This one's yours. All yours. On the house.

He grabs Krem and *throws* him — Krem sprawls, skids through the ash, and Lobo plants him on his knees in the open, facing Ruthye. Gift-wrapped. Delivered. On a platter.

**LOBO**

*(stepping back, gesturing like a maître d')* Go on. You'll feel *great.* Trust me. I do this all the time.

And this is the moment. The whole thesis of the film, standing on the edge of a sword.

Because Lobo has done the thing the movie has agonized over for two hours — and made it *effortless.* No danger. No fight. No qualifiers, no chaos, no self-defense, no messy heat-of-the-moment. He has stripped away every excuse and every complication and left only the pure, clean, cold act: a beaten man on his knees, and a girl with a right, and a sword. Lobo hasn't muddied Ruthye's choice. He's *purified* it. Everything that happens next is one hundred percent hers.

Krem, on his knees, looks up at Ruthye. Pleading. Pathetic. The regal cruelty gone, just a frightened man in the ash.

**KREM**

Girl — please — I didn't — 

Ruthye draws the sword.

The wrap falls away. It's her father's blade, too big for her, and she holds it like she's held everything: seriously. Her face steels — the exact face from the kitchen doorway, the rage that decided something before the mind caught up.

**RUTHYE**

*(low, to Krem, and it is his own words in her mouth)* This is what the world is.

She draws the sword back to swing.

**KARA — moves.**

Depleted, bleeding, the weakest she has been in the entire film, running on fumes and a promise — Kara pushes off the wall and *puts her own body between Ruthye and Krem,* in the path of the blade, because it is the only thing left she can do and she does not hesitate for even the length of a breath.

The sword catches her across the body.

A terrible sound. Kara folds around it. Drops to her knees, then over, into the ash. Bleeding — really bleeding, dark and fast.

Silence slams down over the whole courtyard.

**LOBO** — and this is important — Lobo is *dumbfounded.* Genuinely, wholly speechless. He has killed gods. He has fought Superman to a standstill. He has crossed the whole universe and seen every way there is to die. And he has never, not once, seen a person *voluntarily step into a blade to save someone from themselves.* It doesn't compute. There is a full beat where the most dangerous being in existence simply has no frame of reference for what he just watched.

**LOBO**

*(quietly, to no one)* ...Ooh. That's gonna leave a mark.

*(beat)* Didn't see that one comin'.

Ruthye stands frozen, sword still in her hands, staring at what she did. She didn't mean it. She wanted Krem. She has wanted Krem since a kitchen table. And the thing she wanted just cut the one person in the universe who gives a damn whether she lives or dies.

The sword falls out of her hands.

And in the chaos — nobody watching him, nobody *letting* him, everyone's world having just narrowed to the bleeding woman in the ash — Krem crawls. Away. Into the murk. Pathetic, scrabbling, alive.

And this is the cruel machinery of it, and the film makes sure we feel it: Krem escapes *because Ruthye tried to kill him.* Her revenge swing created the wound, the wound created the emergency, the emergency opened the gap. Her own vengeance is the reason the quest isn't over. The thing she reached for is the thing that let him slip away.

But she isn't looking at Krem. She has dropped to her knees over Kara.

**INT./EXT. BILKI — COURTYARD — CONTINUOUS (CLOSE)**

Close now. Just the two of them in the ash and the gray. Ruthye gathers Kara's head into her lap, her hands going red. Heart to heart.

**RUTHYE**

*(a whisper that is hiding a wail)* Why?

Kara's eyes find her. She's fading — the blood, the depletion, no sun, nothing left. But she smiles, a little. It's the truest thing she's had to give in the whole film, and it costs her the last of what she has.

**KARA**

*(barely)* I had to save you.

*(a breath)* That's my code.

Two codes, now, laid side by side in one image. Ruthye's Writ, which demanded she kill. Kara's, which demanded she prevent it. One sword between them. And Kara's code just cost her blood — Rule 7 paid in full, mercy made expensive in the only currency that counts.

The mirror the whole movie has been building — *Ruthye is Kara at month one; Kara is Ruthye at year ten* — collapses into this single frame: the older one bleeding out from the younger one's blade, not because the younger one is evil, but because grief almost made her into something she isn't.

Kara's eyes flutter. They drift up, past Ruthye's face, to the sky — the choking gray, and somewhere far behind it, impossibly, the memory of a yellow sun. The faint idea of light.

She's going.

Ruthye looks up too. At the dead sky. And then — she can't help it — a flick of her eyes to the side, to the murk where Krem crawled away, her whole quest, her father's debt, her Writ, disappearing into the gray with him.

She has to choose. Right now. On her knees, in the blood. Between the revenge crawling away into the dark, and the woman dying in her lap.

It is not, it turns out, a hard choice at all.

She looks up — at the enormous white shape standing over them, cigar smoldering, still trying to work out what he just saw.

**RUTHYE**

*(to Lobo)* Take us up to see the sun.

Lobo snorts. Recovers some of himself. Folds his arms.

**LOBO**

Listen, kid. Nobody tells the Main Man —

Ruthye stands.

She leaves Kara's head cradled in one arm and she *stands,* and it is the same iron she used at a kitchen table's memory, the same iron she used to cite her father's Writ, the same girl who told the Main Man to wait. Small, blood to the elbows, facing down the most dangerous being alive, and not one part of her is asking.

**RUTHYE**

Take us. Or kill me now.

*(and here it is — his currency, spent to the last coin)* The code demands it.

She means it. Every word. She will die on this dead ground before she watches Kara die on it. And she's not begging — begging is beneath the moment. She's *demanding,* holding the Main Man to the one standard he told her, ninety seconds ago, was worth more than all the corpses on the planet.

A long beat. Lobo's face is unreadable — the cigar-smoke curling, the red eyes on this thirteen-year-old who just accidentally stabbed someone she loves and is now offering the universe her own throat rather than back down an inch.

He can't refuse. Not without spitting on the one thing he just swore was sacred. She's got him, and — somewhere under the granite — he knows it, and maybe even likes it.

**LOBO**

*(a long exhale of smoke)* ...Fine. Get on.

Ruthye doesn't waste half a second wondering if he means it. Her thumb finds the cheap plastic ring on her finger — Kara's ridiculous gift, *now you won't die in space* — and she cracks it.

A bubble snaps open around her with an offensively chipper little chime:

**RING (V.O.)**

*Deploying!*

A personal atmosphere, practical and absurd and lifesaving, wobbling faintly rainbow in the gray. The throwaway gift becomes the thing that lets the child save the woman. Kara's earlier, half-drunk kindness, paying itself forward at exactly the right instant.

Lobo rolls his eyes at the chime — *codes are one thing, but a bubble that goes ding is a bridge too far* — stoops, and hauls Kara up and over one shoulder in a single motion, a sack of a dying woman. He stalks toward his bike.

And Kara — slung over the shoulder of the most dangerous being in the universe, bleeding, rising off a dead world — has a strange, calm look on her face. She has made her peace. If this cost her her life, it was worth it. She stepped in front of the blade on purpose and she'd do it again.

She closes her eyes.

And the film finally gives us the memory it's been holding since the very first morning.

**FLASHBACK — INT. ARGO — THE EL RESIDENCE — THE LAST DAY.**

Not fragments. All of it.

The wrong-colored sky through a great window, cracking like glaze. The floor trembling. And in the middle of a room that is beginning to come apart — a family, at war with each other, out of love.

A YOUNGER KARA — a teenager, maybe fifteen, furious and terrified — is being pulled toward a small launch pod by her parents, ALURA and ZOR-EL, and she is fighting them every step.

**YOUNG KARA**

No — *no,* I'm not leaving, I'm not getting in that, you can't make me —

**ZOR-EL**

Kara —

**YOUNG KARA**

If it's ending then it ends with *all of us,* I'm not going out there *alone,* I won't — 

*(a child's whole logic, unanswerable)* — I would rather die here with you than live out there without you, I *mean* it —

**ALURA**

*(cradling her face, both of them crying now)* Listen to me. Listen — 

**YOUNG KARA**

No —

**ZOR-EL**

*(breaking, the strength going out of him)* If you don't — Kara, if you stay, then all of it, everything we — it'll all have been for *nothing.* Do you understand? You're the part that *doesn't* have to end.

And the fight goes out of everyone at once. The three of them collapse together — not parents forcing a child, just a family holding each other at the end of a world, sobbing, tangled up, the floor shaking, the sky coming down.

A moment. Just breathing. Just holding.

Then Alura pulls back, holds Kara's face in both hands, makes her look. And says the first half of the thesis — not a philosophy, a *command,* the last order a mother gives:

**ALURA**

You must live.

Kara, wrecked, nods. Barely.

And Zor-El — hands on her shoulders, the pod hatch open behind her, the world ending — gives the other half. Quiet. The most important thing he will ever say.

**ZOR-EL**

Promise me you'll be good.

Beat. Young Kara looks at her father. At her mother. At the ending sky.

**YOUNG KARA**

*(a whisper)* I promise.

They put her in the pod. The hatch. A hand on the glass from the outside, and a hand on the glass from the inside — matched, for a second, through the barrier. Then the launch, and the silence after.

That's it. That's the whole engine of the film, and it was never a position Kara reasoned her way to. It's a *promise she made to a dying woman.* Every time she steps between a person and a blade, every time she holds her own fire, every time she chooses the hard restraint over the easy cold kill — she is keeping it. And every morning she spent under a red sun, drinking, hiding, disappearing — she was breaking it.

**END FLASHBACK.**

**EXT. BILKI — UPPER ATMOSPHERE — LOBO'S BIKE — CONTINUOUS**

The great blue-flamed bike claws up through the poisoned sky. Lobo hunched over the bars, Ruthye clinging on inside her wobbling rainbow bubble, one arm locked around Kara — and Kara slumped against the machine, the shredded leather jacket torn open and fallen around her shoulders like a shawl. A death shawl. The thing she wrapped around herself to disappear.

Nuclear winter thins as they rise. The gray gets brighter, then thinner, then — 

Kara, eyes closed, a single tear breaking loose from the corner of one eye and floating free in the thinning air, weightless, catching light that isn't there yet.

Silence. The bike. The last of the gray tearing away in rags.

And they punch through the top of the dead sky into — 

**BLINDING. YELLOW. LIGHT.**

The one solar transition of the whole film, and it's held long, and it's earned. The sun — the actual, enormous, life-giving yellow sun — floods everything. It pours over the bike, over Ruthye's bubble, over the woman slumped against the machine.

Kara's limp body drifts up off the bike's flank as Lobo cuts the climb — floating now in raw sunlight, unshielded, no glass, no barrier, no filter between her and the star. (She floats *fetal,* curled, small — never cruciform. This is a rebirth, not a martyrdom.)

For a moment, nothing.

Then her body takes a breath.

A reflexive, involuntary, whole-body breath — the gasp of something being born. Her chest fills. Her spine straightens. And under the yellow light, the wounds *close* — the sword-cut, the shrapnel, the turret-tears, all of it knitting shut, the blood stopping, the color coming back into her like a photograph developing.

The sun does what the sun does. Life insists on itself. Her body insists on living, exactly the way her parents insisted she live.

And the jacket — finally too shredded to hold together, the old hide, the disguise, the blanket she disappeared inside — slips off her shoulders entirely and drifts away behind her into the black, tumbling, gone. She doesn't take it off. It simply cannot come with her into the person she's choosing to be.

Power floods back. And with the power — everything she's been hiding from under a dead red sun for so long. It all comes back at once, in the light. The metaphor doesn't need a line of dialogue: from death to life, from avoidance to engagement, from the surface of a dead world up into the sun.

Kara's eyes open.

They are clear. Clear for the first time in the entire film. No fog. No flinch. She's awake, and she's *here,* and she's whole.

She turns, floating in the gold, and finds Ruthye — inside her silly bubble, crying, streaked with ash and Kara's own blood, thirteen years old and having just chosen a life over a death for the first time.

Kara reaches out. Pulls the bubble, and the girl in it, in against her. Holds her. Under the sun.

It's the lesson Supergirl came all this way to teach — and the one she finally, in the same motion, teaches herself. You can choose the living over the dead. You can come back. Life insists on itself, if you let it.

Off to the side, straddling his idling bike in the full glory of a life-giving star, **LOBO** watches two people hug in the sunlight, and is *mortified.*

He has faced down the worst the universe can offer and never flinched. This — two people crying and *holding each other,* feelings, sincerity, unironic love — is the one thing he cannot be in the same sky as. His whole liminal, wink-and-nudge, ultraviolent existence has no defense against it. Our earnest little movie is, it turns out, the Main Man's kryptonite. He drops in, realizes he's in the wrong film entirely, and — 

He kicks the bike into gear.

**LOBO**

*(to himself, still processing the woman who stepped into a blade for feelings)* ...Well. That was fucked up.

He guns it, already accelerating away from the sunlight and the crying and everything about it he cannot stand. Over his shoulder, as the blue flame catches and hauls him off into the black:

**LOBO**

Fuckin' *codes,* man.

Gone. A blue streak, dwindling.

The strongest being in the scene exits — not defeated, not persuaded, just *repelled* — because vulnerability is the single thing he can't do. The universe, personified, can do anything except feel. Which is exactly why he could never have been the one to help them, and exactly why he had to be the one to clear the table so they could help each other.

And then it's just the two of them, floating in the yellow light above a dead world.

Kara, reborn. Full-strength Supergirl for the first time in the film — and never to power down again.

Ruthye, in her bubble, held.

The sun on both of them.

Below, under the gray, a beaten man crawls through the ash with an antidote around his neck, and a debt still unpaid, and a dog on the wrong side of a hill.

The temptation has changed. It's no longer weakness. From here to the end, the only thing that can stop Supergirl is Supergirl.

**FADE TO WHITE.**

**EXT. BILKI — UPPER ATMOSPHERE — CONTINUOUS**

From white — into blue-gold clarity. Kara, floating, whole, the light on her. She looks down at the gray lid of the dead world beneath them and her jaw sets. Not with dread now. With purpose.

The audience has waited the whole film for this, and the film, finally, hands it over: she takes Ruthye — bubble and all — gathers her in close and *safe,* and drops.

**EXT. BILKI — DESCENT — CONTINUOUS**

Supergirl carries a thirteen-year-old down through a poisoned sky under her own power. She punches through the gray cloud-deck and a SONIC BOOM rolls out ahead of her, blowing a clean hole in the murk, a shaft of yellow light stabbing all the way down to the ash for the first time in years.

This is Supergirl. Unhidden, unhungover, unafraid. The one true spectacle beat of the movie, spent here, on a rescue, not a fight.

**EXT. BILKI — BRIGAND DEPOT — CONTINUOUS**

She sets down light as a leaf in the ruined courtyard, Ruthye beside her, the bubble dissolving with a last apologetic chime.

The courtyard is Lobo's gallery — bodies, wreckage, the crushed hauler, the fires. And through the smoke, Kara scans — actually *scans* now, X-ray-easy, taking in the whole field in a glance. A row of Brigand shuttles sits half-buried along the depot wall. One of them is warmed up: running lights on, loading ramp down, engines ticking over. The ride Krem is trying to use to run again.

And there — near it — Krem.

He's gotten some of himself back together. Not the man from the kitchen; that man is gone for good. But functional. Desperate. He has a huge duffel of Brigand plunder — gold, gems, the loot of a dead world — in one fist, and with the other he's wrenching a heavy energy weapon off the corpse of one of his own crew. He fits it to his left arm. It attaches with a grisly, wet, mechanical *thunk,* biting into him. He grimaces. It's the arm of a man who has nothing left but hardware.

Kara starts forward. Ruthye moves with her, hand going to the sword — she's picked it up again, the father's blade, off the ash where she dropped it.

**KARA**

*(without turning, quiet, absolute)* Stay back.

**RUTHYE**

I have the Writ, I —

Kara turns and *looks* at her. Not angry. Not pleading. Just a look — level, clear-eyed, the look of someone who has stepped in front of a blade for you and earned the right to it. They're past words now, the two of them. Ruthye stops.

She stays back.

Krem straightens, the gun-arm humming. He sees Supergirl — the woman who was a wreck on his floor a lifetime ago, now standing in a shaft of yellow light, whole, radiant, terrible — and he can't hold her eyes. His own are still red-rimmed from Lobo, from the ash, from the end of everything he believed. He glances once at Ruthye, past Kara. Looks away from her too.

He gestures, small and tired, at the whole gray dead world around them. And the dead world lets him make his case without a villain's speech. He barely raises his voice.

**KREM**

*(quietly)* Look around you.

*(a breath)* There was never anyone to save us.

He could be talking about Lobo. About his own dead planet. About the neighbors who turned up their lamps. It doesn't matter which. He's not twirling a mustache. He's a bitter, tired, hollowed-out man who survived the worst thing and mistook surviving it for understanding the universe. This is the place that taught him his creed, and he learned the wrong lesson in it, and he's standing in the proof and calling it wisdom.

His gun-arm stays at his side. Instead he lifts his *other* hand — and in it is the poison bow, the same ugly weapon that started all of this, an arrow already nocked, its tip beading wet and dark.

Kara recognizes it. She doesn't care about it. Her eyes are fixed on one thing: the small vial on a chain around Krem's neck. The antidote. The cure. The whole reason she came.

**KREM**

*(flat, aiming)* You should have stayed on your rock.

He looses the arrow.

**THE NON-FIGHT.**

And the film delivers, in one beat, the entire argument it's been making about power.

The arrow leaves the bow — and it is *slow.* Absurdly slow. It hangs in the air, barely crawling, a thing from another, weaker timescale.

Because Kara is already off the ground. Already moving. A shockwave blooms out from where she was standing as she crosses the courtyard faster than sound, faster than sight — Krem's eyes have only just begun to widen, his body still frozen in the follow-through of a shot that, to him, hasn't landed yet.

She plucks the poison arrow out of the air between two fingers, without slowing.

And she reaches him. And she brings her hand down on the poison bow and knocks it out of his grip — rougher, harder, than she needed to. A great deal harder than she needed to.

**SNAP BACK TO REALTIME —**

— and Krem *howls.* The shockwave of her arrival has blasted him meters backward across the ash. He lands hard. His right arm is bent in a direction arms don't bend. The bow is gone, shattered, somewhere in the murk.

Kara stands over the wreck of him, the poison arrow still pinched in her fingers, and she's — taken aback. She looks at her own hand. At the too-hard hit. At the man screaming in the ash with a broken arm she didn't strictly need to break.

She didn't expect him to be this weak. Or — worse, truer — she didn't expect *herself* to be this angry. The temptation the whole third act warned about, showing its face for the first time: now that nothing can stop her, the only danger left is what she wants to do.

She takes a beat. Presses two fingers to her own temple, steadying, the way you talk yourself down. When she speaks, she makes herself gentle. She makes herself keep the promise.

**KARA**

*(quiet, reaching for calm)* Listen. Listen to me. I don't — I'm not here for you. I just want the antidote. Around your neck. That's all I want, I just need it to save —

A huge RED GLOW floods the frame.

Kara looks up. Krem — on his back, broken-armed, done, *hollow* — has overloaded the energy weapon fused to his other arm. It's sparking, swelling, venting light. Not aimed at anything. A kamikaze. A dead man's last spite, or a dead man's mercy on himself, or both — he's not even looking at her. He's looking at the gray sky.

The screen goes red — 

— and then, instantly, WHITE.

**EXT. BILKI — WIDE — CONTINUOUS**

From a distance: a single concussive flash on the dead ground, and a great red-white arc of energy tearing across the gray sky, over the horizon, gone.

Where Supergirl was standing, there is nothing but smoke and settling ash.

Silence, after the blast. The old fires burn. The gray closes back over the hole the sonic boom punched.

And through the smoke — a figure. Small. Walking, not running. Toward the wreck of Krem.

**RUTHYE.**

She crosses the courtyard the way she disarmed a ruffian in a bar, the way she stood in a kitchen doorway. Sword drawn. Face set. Nothing in her body language telegraphs mercy. Nothing in it suggests hesitation. This girl has been walking toward this exact moment for two hours and every line of her says she is going to finish it.

The audience should be in real suspense here. Genuine. Because everything about her says *yes.*

She reaches Krem.

He's a ruin. Bleeding, burnt, one arm broken and the other a charred smoking stump where the gun was. But the little vial on its chain still hangs at his throat, untouched, impossible, gleaming.

Krem looks up at her.

And he is not the evangelist from the kitchen. Not the philosopher who killed her father with such deliberate, teaching cruelty. He's the man who begged Lobo for his life and watched his own creed die in his hands. He's tired. Hollowed all the way out. There is nothing regal left in him, nothing to rage against.

**KREM**

*(a rasp — not a taunt, not a lesson, nothing left in it)* ...Just do it.

Not *this is what the world is.* Not an offer of graduation, not a recruitment, not a lesson. Just a used-up man who no longer cares whether he lives or dies, because the version of him that believed in something — even something monstrous — died on his knees in the ash. He's not giving her anything. He's just done.

Ruthye looks at him.

And the thing she'd been marching toward isn't there. She came to kill the monster from her kitchen and there's no monster here. Just a tired, broken person in the rubble of the world that made him. Killing him wouldn't prove anything. It wouldn't disprove anything. It wouldn't bring her father back or fill the hole or answer the Writ in any way the Writ could feel. It would just be killing. And she has, once already, on a bike ride into the sun, learned the difference between choosing death and choosing life.

She raises the sword.

The audience holds its breath. This is it.

She *slashes* — 

— and the blade takes the *chain.* Not the neck.

The vial of antidote drops — and Ruthye catches it, lifted on the flat of her father's sword, the cure balanced on the blade that was meant for blood. She turns away from Krem. She does not look back at him. Not once.

Behind her — 

**KARA.**

Standing in the settling smoke, whole, unmarked, not a scratch on her — of *course* she survived the blast; this is Supergirl now. She's been there the entire time. How long? Long enough. She did not intervene. She could have — she could have crossed the courtyard between two heartbeats, plucked the sword from the air, ended the suspense.

But *would* she have?

She didn't. She let Ruthye choose.

And the look on Kara's face as Ruthye turns around holding the antidote instead of blood — it's relief, mostly. But under the relief, brighter: *pride.* She's watching a thirteen-year-old step off Krem's road. And, maybe, in the same instant, stepping off her own.

This is the final beat of their whole relationship, completed in a single act of *inaction.* Kara protected Ruthye. Kara restrained Ruthye. Ruthye saved Kara. And now — the last thing, the hardest thing — Kara *trusted* Ruthye. Trusted her to be the one who chooses. Stood behind her and did nothing and let her be the person she'd become.

Ruthye reaches her. Holds out the vial. Their hands meet around it — hands, again, the film's whole thesis in close-up: what you choose to do with your capacity. Kara closes her fingers over Ruthye's and the cure both.

And then Kara pulls her in, and they hold each other, both of them crying now, in a shaft of yellow light on a dead world.

**RUTHYE**

*(into Kara's shoulder)* Let's go home.

The line that started as a drunk's plea across a bar — *go home* — is now the truest thing either of them has ever said. And this time, they both hear it.

They walk to the warmed-up Brigand shuttle, ramp down, engines ticking. They climb aboard. The ramp closes.

Behind them, in the smoke and the ash, the wreck of a man who may be alive or may be dead. We do not find out. The camera does not go to him. It stays with Ruthye, walking away, not looking back — and so we don't either. His fate is irrelevant. The refusal to show it is the point.

The story was never about him.

The shuttle lifts off the dead world, punches up through the gray, and is gone.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. MERIDIAN — DOCK CLINIC — LATER**

The amber tube. Krypto floating in it, the decay-curve on its side sloped almost, almost into the shaded band — hours, maybe less.

The tube drains. The seal breaks. Dr. Silt and Kara ease the big white dog out onto the gurney. He's limp, cold, barely there.

No fanfare. No speech. Kara takes the vial — the vial Ruthye lifted on a sword instead of a life — and hands it to Dr. Silt, who loads it, finds the vein, and pushes it home.

The needle. And then — the breath.

Krypto's chest fills. His eyes crack open. His head lifts off the gurney, ears swiveling, finding the one smell in the universe he was looking for.

And Kara — who has not really let herself feel one single thing land, all the way, since a kitchen this movie never showed us — puts her forehead down against his. Life, chosen and saved. The dog's tail thumps the gurney, weak, then less weak.

**KARA**

*(a wet laugh into his fur)* Hey. Hey, dummy. There you are.

Across the clinic, Ruthye watches, the ghost of a real smile on her. Behind her, through the bay windows, Vesk the mechanic gives a slow thumbs-up: Kara's little ship sits repaired on the apron, a salvaged Brigand drive crystal — one that annoyingly, finally, *did* grow, or was pulled off a dead raider, it doesn't matter which — seated where the cooked seed used to be. They have a way home. All of them.

**DISSOLVE TO:**

**EXT. EVELIE — KNOLL HOMESTEAD — "EVENING" (RED, AND FOR ONCE, WARM)**

The red sun again. The same enormous cooling coal on the same horizon. But the grade is different now — golden-hour warm where the opening was embalmed. The palette forgives its own beginning. The same red light that lit Kara's avoidance is just, here, the ordinary blaze of evening over a home.

The blue-leafed crops. The irrigation wheels. The farmhouse, warm light in the windows.

**EXTREME CLOSE-UP:** a streak of white, launching — 

**KRYPTO** hits Kara full in the chest, ninety pounds of resurrected dog, knocking her flat into the soft field-dirt, licking her face, tail a blur, alive, warm, whole. The reunion the whole film has been running toward, in close-up, no irony, no distance, the camera loving it all the way.

Kara laughs — really laughs — and lets him.

**PULL BACK —**

— and it's not just Kara in the frame. Ruthye stands beside them. And behind Ruthye: PIP (6), gawking at the enormous white dog and the flying woman; and NELL, one hand on Pip's shoulder, the other pressed to her mouth, looking at her daughter — *her daughter, home, alive* — like she's looking at the sunrise.

Ruthye went to the road of death and came back to the road of life. She left this, and it's still here, because she chose to come back to it.

Kara's repaired ship waits in the background, gleaming faintly in the red-gold.

Kara gets up, brushing off dirt, Krypto orbiting her. She and Ruthye stand a little apart from the family, in that space two people who've been through something share.

Ruthye looks at Kara. At the ship. And there's a wistfulness in it — because a part of her, the part that saw a nebula through a bus window, wants to go.

**RUTHYE**

*(quiet)* I wish I could go with you. But I have — 

She doesn't finish. She just nods, back over her shoulder, at Nell and Pip in the doorway.

She doesn't need to finish.

Kara looks at the family. At the warm windows. At the whole impossible ordinary miracle of a house where the people you love are still inside it. And she says the truest thing she has, solemn, meaning every syllable:

**KARA**

I would trade the world for what you have.

*(a beat)* This is where you belong.

The line that began as a drunk's plea across a bar — *go home* — arriving, finally, as a blessing. And this time they both hear it, and this time it's true, and this time it doesn't hurt.

They stand in it a moment. The red-gold light. The dog. The family in the door.

And then — Kara can't help herself. The solemnity cracks, and underneath it is something the whole film has been waiting to let out: an impish, delighted, wildly inappropriate grin.

**KARA**

*(brightening)* ...but, you know. We never *did* get a chance to finish celebrating my birthday.

Ruthye looks at her.

And the face — the cold, serious, code-citing, ruffian-disarming, Writ-reciting face of a girl who has not been a child since a kitchen table — 

— cracks open.

She *giggles.* For the first time in the entire film, the weight comes off her shoulders, all at once, and you can feel it go — the audience feels it lift. A thirteen-year-old, giggling, in the red-gold evening light, home.

**KARA**

*(already moving toward the ship, Krypto bounding ahead)* Come on. There's a place three systems over, the pies are the size of a *table,* you have not lived —

**RUTHYE**

*(chasing, laughing)* We can't just — my mother —

**KARA**

*(calling back to the doorway, waving)* We'll have her back tonight! Tomorrow at the latest!

Nell, in the doorway, laughs and shakes her head — *these two* — and waves them off. Pip is already running after the dog.

Kara and Ruthye and Krypto pile into the little repaired ship. It lights up — healthy, orange-gold, *right* — and lifts off the field, and banks, and climbs.

**FINAL IMAGE:**

Through the canopy, or from the ground looking up: the little ship crossing a *fiery red sky* — the same red sun that opened the film, that lit Kara's avoidance, that embalmed a whole world — now just the ordinary, glorious blaze of a living evening over Ruthye's home.

And inside, or against the light: Ruthye, laughing. Free. The adventure that was always supposed to be fun, finally, *finally,* being fun — because it earned it.

*The film began with a girl in a doorway, shielding her brother, her eyes full of rage. It ends with the same girl, giggling, choosing the life.*

**FADE OUT.**

**MID-CREDITS**

**INT. METROPOLIS — HIGH APARTMENT — NIGHT**

A window. Beyond it: a skyline. Not Argo, not Bilki — the opposite of both. Gleaming, alive, a whole city of lit windows, each one a person who chose to build a life. Clean yellow-white light. Home, or the offer of one.

Kara stands at the glass, looking out. No jacket — that's gone, drifted off into space above a dead world, and it's not coming back, and she's not looking for it. Just her, at a window, in a city that says *whenever, no expiry.*

Her face in the reflection: sad. But *present.* Not numb. Not fogged. Not drinking — there's no bottle in frame, and its absence is the whole point. She's feeling all of it, at once, the way the whole film has been asking her to.

**KARA**

*(to the glass, to the city, to herself, quiet)* ...Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer this time.

A breath. The city lights. Somewhere out there, a cousin with a buzzer that has her name on it.

**KARA**

I mean. It's good to be — 

*(and the word, which cost her a whole film to be able to say, comes out simple and true)* — home.

Behind her, in the reflection, a streak of white: KRYPTO, gleefully destroying something he absolutely should not be destroying — a couch cushion, a houseplant, the leg of an expensive table. A terror. Alive. Her true home was never a planet.

Kara turns from the window, catches him mid-crime.

**KARA**

*(no heat in it at all, pure warmth)* Hey — *hey* — that's not — okay, that's the third one, buddy —

She's not sure yet. She might not be sure for a long time. She's not healed — she's *decided.* Decided to stop running, to stop hiding on a red-sun world, to stop dodging the calls, to stop reaching for the bottle before her eyes are open. To be here. To keep a promise she made a long time ago, to a dying woman, on a world that no longer exists:

*You must live. Promise me you'll be good.*

She's living. She's here. She's going to be all right.

She goes to save the houseplant. The city glows behind her.

**FADE OUT.**

**THE END.**
