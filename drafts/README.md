## Runtime estimates

Rubric: estimates are based on playable scene units, not just word count. Dense action can play faster than the page, while grief beats, silent reaction shots, travel imagery, and spectacle can stretch beyond the raw text. Mid-credits scenes are included; actual title/end credits are not.

### `draft-script-opus.md`

Two passes exist for this draft, and they disagree by a lot:

- **Initial estimate (gpt-5.5):** ~**60 minutes** (range 56-68).
- **Recount (Opus, scene-by-scene):** ~**94 minutes** — main feature ~**91:45**, plus a ~**2:30** mid-credits sting. Practical range **85-100 minutes**, depending on how much air the grief beats, the Bilki action, and the yellow-sun rebirth are given.

The recount runs **~34 minutes longer**, but it is not new material. The gap is pacing realism: the initial pass compressed the load-bearing set pieces (the kitchen murder, the Long Dusk bar + brawl, the bus confession, the Bilki gauntlet, Lobo's massacre) and folded several distinct beats into single rows. Timing each scene at true performance/cut speed — honoring the pauses the script repeatedly calls for — pushes it to feature length.

**Act structure** (the story's dramatic beats — same spine and boundaries as the `draft-script-gpt5.5.md` read below):

- **Act I — Setup** ends when the yellow-sun sprint fails and the dead ship limps to Meridian; the two wounded protagonists and their fused goal (save Krypto → the cure is on Krem) are locked in.
- **Act II — The Pursuit** runs through Meridian, the bus, and Bilki, and **climaxes on the sword fall** (scene 32) — Kara stepping into Ruthye's blade. This is the decisive low point, everyone farthest from their goals. The last Act II beat is scene 33 (Ruthye forcing Lobo to fly them up); the Argo flashback opens Act III.
- **Act III** opens on **Kara's full Argo flashback**, turns on the yellow-sun rebirth and her restraint with Krem, resolves on Ruthye cutting the chain instead of the neck, and settles into denouement (Krypto cured, homecoming).

At this cut the proportions land ~30% / 41% / 26% — a proper long Act II, tighter than the gpt-5.5 draft's more evenly split front half.

## Executive summary

Both drafts follow the same macro story spine from the story bible. They are not competing scenarios so much as competing executions of the same scenario:

1. Evelie / red sun / Kara in avoidance.
2. Ruthye's living family and murdered father.
3. Krem as survivor-turned-predator, not a cartoon monster.
4. Krypto poisoned by Brigand weaponry.
5. Failed yellow-sun sprint, ship breakdown, rest stop, bus.
6. Bilki as Brigand dead-world / worldview made landscape.
7. Lobo as Act II agency concentrator, not plot crutch.
8. Yellow-sun rebirth.
9. Full-power climax as moral choice, not fight.
10. Ruthye cuts the antidote chain, not Krem's throat.
11. Krypto lives; Ruthye goes home; Kara chooses Earth/home.

The best next draft should probably use **`draft-script-gpt5.5.md` as the structural/playability base** and import the **Fable draft's specificity, visual grammar, Krem texture, Lobo sequence, yellow-sun rebirth, and ending warmth**.

Short version:

- **GPT draft = cleaner screenplay machine.** More immediately playable, stronger dialogue utility, more causal clarity.
- **Fable draft = stronger movie.** More cinematic, stranger, more emotionally and visually aligned with the bible, but sometimes too literary/meta to remain untouched.

## High-priority tweaks before merging

These apply to the next combined pass, regardless of which draft is used as base.

### 1. Mid-credits: Clark should be physically present, not a call/video call

Both drafts currently make the mid-credits beat a phone/device call to Clark/Kal. Since Kara is already on Earth in Metropolis, the stronger version is probably **Clark in person**.

Recommended adjustment:

- Keep the Metropolis apartment/window/tea/no-bottle setup.
- Kara looks out at the living city and says some version of, "Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer this time."
- Clark is there in the room, or arrives at the door / balcony / kitchen threshold — gentle, not grand.
- Krypto mauls him or betrays Kara by greeting Clark first.
- Kara says, in person, "It's good to be home."

This pays off the unanswered voicemails while making Earth feel like an actual place she has returned to, not another remote signal.

### 2. Argo/Krypton canon: Argo launches from Krypton, then dies later

Both drafts currently treat the Argo memory as a simple end-of-world launch chamber. The next pass should clarify the two-stage catastrophe:

1. **Krypton dies / explodes.** Argo City survives by launching or breaking away.
2. **Argo then dies slowly later**, with basically everyone killed by Kryptonite poisoning / radiation sickness.
3. Kara's trauma is that she did not merely inherit a dead world. She lived through the first death, survived on Argo, then watched the second death happen slowly.

Suggested use:

- In the bus confession, Kara can distinguish Kal's story from hers: Kal left before remembering; Kara remembers Krypton, Argo's survival, and then Argo's poisoning.
- In the Lobo near-death flashback, the launch pod is from dying Argo after the poisoning, not from an undifferentiated Krypton/Argo apocalypse.
- Make the green/Kryptonite poisoning visually and verbally distinct from **Brigand poison** so the present plot does not turn into kryptonite gadget chess. The story bible's "no kryptonite escalation" rule should still apply to Krem and the Act II/III mechanics.

### 3. Bus flask beat: Kara should finish the first swig

Both drafts currently have Ruthye tell Kara to stop and Kara more or less obey immediately. Better:

- Kara is already mid-swig when Ruthye says, "You should stop that."
- Kara hears her — and still finishes the swallow. Not triumphantly; habit just wins that first round.
- That small failure opens the confession. She tells Ruthye part of the Argo story because Ruthye naming the drinking makes the load visible.
- After telling the story, Kara reaches for the flask again and this time chooses not to drink / puts it away.

This makes the later decision feel earned: Ruthye does not magically fix Kara by scolding her; telling the truth lifts enough weight for Kara to choose differently the second time.

### 4. First meeting staging: Kara should be in a back table/booth, not at the bar

Final staging should put Kara in a **back table or booth**, with Krypto under/near the table, not posted at the bar.

- `draft-script-gpt5.5.md` already does this and should preserve it.
- `draft-script-fable.md` currently has Kara established at the bar; move her to a booth/table while keeping the excellent bartender texture if desired.

The booth better supports Kara's avoidance: she is not performing at the social center of the room; she is hiding in public.

## README alignment rubric

The story bible's key rules for evaluating divergences:

| Bible principle | What it asks of the draft |
|---|---|
| Goodness is an active choice | The climax must resolve through Ruthye/Kara choosing life, not through defeating Krem. |
| Grief is not reasoned away | Kara and Ruthye change through mirroring, not speeches that magically work. |
| Red sun is avoidance made physical | Kara's early weakness should feel like consequence, not arbitrary power math. |
| Krem is not a power peer | He matters through leverage, logistics, terrain, poison, ships, and worldview. |
| The real line is combat vs. execution | The story may contain violent combat, but the forbidden act is the cold killing of a beaten enemy. |
| Mercy must cost something concrete | Restraint must risk Krypto, Kara, Ruthye's vengeance, or all three. |
| Lobo is the universe personified | His violence should be fun, seductive, funny, then queasy; he clears the table for choice. |
| Every scene load-bearing | No detachable brawls or repeated mechanics; each scene must change someone. |
| The ending is home/life | Ruthye returns to mother/brother; Kara returns to Earth/home without pretending she is healed. |

## Base-and-graft plan

`draft-script-gpt5.5.md` is the **base**; `draft-script-fable.md` and `draft-script-opus.md` are **donors**. The target is the base's clean spine and economical, castable dialogue, *carrying* fable's structure and opus's atmosphere.

> For an objective, side-by-side description of how each draft solves each character/scene/action, see **`three-way-comparison.md`**.

**Key finding (from re-reading all three in full):** fable already *reaches for* much of the "Structural additions" addendum — the **fragmented flashback** (sanitized-told-now / fully-seen-later), **Bilki-as-Krem's-Yellow-Hills**, and the **hands-through-a-barrier** motif are on the page. But two of its versions are the **wrong shape** and must be fixed, not grafted as-is: its **revenge is a spoken confession** ("it doesn't matter who… it was justified") — too on-the-nose (know-and-show, don't tell); and its **flask beat stops *before* the swig** — we want beat 1 to *finish* the swig. Also note **register**: fable narrates its own intent in the margins ("this is the thesis," "the audience holds its breath"), which is treatment, not shootable craft — so what reads as gravitas is largely prose to be **converted and heavily cut**. Net: **graft fable's structure + opus's atmosphere onto gpt5.5's cleaner surface**, repairing fable's revenge/flask shapes along the way. Genuinely new beats (in *no* draft): **puppy Krypto, sequenced mother-death, Ruthye tending sick Kara.**

### Locked decisions for the second draft

These are the current production decisions. They supersede older recommendation rows elsewhere in this workbench where those rows conflict.

- **Voice and family characterization:** `gpt5.5` remains the dialogue, tone, and spine. Mine selected characterization and visual texture from Fable. Sol Ultra's opening-family dialogue is specifically **not** a donor: its faux-rustic, over-cute cadence feels forced, and Ultra dialogue in general should be presumed rewrite-only.
- **No tracked family weapon:** reject both Fable's family-crossbow system and Ultra's white-gripped family pistol. Krem already owns a Brigand poison crossbow/bolt-caster; use his own weapon for Father's murder and for the later poison attack. Ruthye has no reason to recover a family heirloom that was used to murder her father.
- **The jump-seed is the causal and tracked object:** after the murder, Ruthye steals the removable jump-seed from Krem's berthed ship while the Brigands are away unloading, selling loot, or drinking. The theft happens **offscreen**; there is no service-yard heist and no theft during the family scene. In the booth she produces the seed and explains only that the Brigands cannot leave the system without it.
- **Bar collision and pursuit:** waking/opening the seed triggers its recovery handshake. Kara recognizes the chirp too late; Krem arrives to recover it. He retakes the seed during the brawl and escapes with it. Ruthye's reader is keyed to the seed's jump signature, which supplies an intermittent **bearing, not coordinates or distance**. This is the story's one FTL-tracking allowance because the tracked object is itself FTL hardware.
- **Jump-seed donor repair:** Kara and Ruthye return from Bilki aboard Krem's tracked Brigand ship, bringing the same jump-seed back to the station. The mechanic grafts it into Kara's burned drive in place of the original core/coil that would have taken two weeks to regrow. This does not require a repair scene or explanatory joke. When Kara's own ship later departs, its open drive cowling/background machinery reveals the payoff: a differently colored seed/crystal still nested in recognizable Brigand casing, visibly bodged into the cleaner Kryptonian assembly with improvised brackets, leads, or hoses. The foreign graft should be legible but incidental. This closes the seed's full chain — theft → recovery handshake → bar collision → Krem's escape → directional trace → transport home → Kara's repair — without adding another object.
- **Starmap and Bilki:** Ruthye aligns that bearing with the public starmap. Krem names **Bilki** once while describing the Yellow Hills in the cold open; the later map query identifies it only as a dead/quarantined world, not a publicly known active Brigand headquarters. Ruthye recognizes the name, and the trace confirms that Krem has gone home. The Yellow Hills quietly plant the later yellow-sun geography without explaining it.
- **Ruthye's Code:** formalize the Code enough to support both halves of her arc, but do not import Ultra's wording, family catechism, or legalistic dialogue cadence. Ruthye initially cites only the blood-debt clause. Kara does **not** need to ask whether that is the entire Code; she can instead observe that it sounds unusually bloodthirsty for Evelie, leaving Ruthye's selective reading implicit. After Kara takes the sword meant for Krem, Ruthye invokes the omitted priority: Kara gave/risked her life for Ruthye, so that **living debt comes before the blood debt**. The Code binds Ruthye, not Lobo; her demand means that she must save Kara and will make Lobo kill her before she abandons that duty. The climax and homecoming embody the completed reading without reciting it again.
- **Kara's ages:** Kara is turning **twenty-one** during the story and was **sixteen** when Argo finally died and she left. For American audiences, her long-established drinking carries an unstated irony; no character needs to explain the U.S. drinking age in space. The five-year interval keeps the wound recent while allowing her avoidance to become an entrenched way of life; it also makes Krypto roughly five after entering the pod as a puppy.
- **Mother's death on Argo:** Mother dies slowly from Kryptonite/radiation poisoning before Kara's launch. Kara is angry and refuses the premise of survival — she will not leave and would rather die with her family. Mother tells her she must not say that and **must live**. Kara cannot give her the needed promise; the scene ends in collapse and embrace as Mother dies, leaving love, anger, and unsaid things together. A funeral-procession image may bridge the later fragments, but should not become additional trauma inventory.
- **Puppy Krypto:** after Mother's death, Father presents puppy Krypto as Mother's final gift to Kara and the surviving piece of life she wanted Kara to carry. Let the puppy embody hope and life's value; do not make Mother or Father state an "as long as there is life, there is hope" slogan.
- **Father's sendoff and Kara's promise:** the final departure is no longer an argument. Father returns to Mother's unfinished demand that Kara live; Kara answers, still somewhat sullen, that she knows. He holds her, makes her look at him, and asks her to promise that she will protect life / those who need her and **be good**. Kara gives him the answer she could not give Mother: **"I promise."** This promise is what Kara later means by **"That's my code."** Do not explain yellow-sun powers in the farewell; the promise is moral and personal, not a superhero mission briefing.
- **Three-stage Argo reveal:** preserve the fragmented architecture. (1) Kara's morning nightmare supplies disjointed images only — Argo's destruction, Mother's sickness/death, the escape pod, and Argo disappearing — without chronology or the promise. (2) On the bus, Kara gives Ruthye an incomplete but more coherent history: Krypton died, Argo survived, then its people sickened slowly from Kryptonite/radiation poisoning. She can acknowledge the losses but withholds her angry refusal, what she could not say to Mother, puppy Krypto's provenance, and Father's final promise. (3) After Kara takes Ruthye's sword and before the yellow-sun rebirth, the full sequence finally plays: the argument and embrace at Mother's death, selected aftermath/funeral fragments, Father's gift of Krypto, the final sendoff, and Kara's "I promise." The audience learns the source of "That's my code" only after watching Kara keep it.
- **Ship/medbay and failed yellow-sun sprint:** use `gpt5.5` as the scene base. The medbay identifies an unusually complex/proprietary Brigand venom and is not equipped to synthesize its counteragent; do **not** add empty or expired supplies. It can stabilize Krypto only briefly. A medical/navigation model predicts that genuine yellow-sun exposure could strengthen him enough to resist the poison, not cure it; there is no artificial-sun lamp, which would create a power loophole and diminish the later rebirth. Avoid a precise countdown: compare a broad patient-viability curve with the route to the nearest yellow star. After an initial forced jump, the model shows they will not arrive in time. Kara knowingly overrides the warnings and forces the poorly maintained ship again. Keep Ruthye's valve/strap competence and the "Ships can be fixed" / "Can dogs?" pressure. Deferred maintenance removed the drive's safety margin, but Kara's desperate overburn actively destroys the **jump coil** rather than the ship randomly conking out. Automatic safe-harbor protocol drops them toward the rest stop. Keep Kara's failed jump coil terminologically distinct from Krem's tracked jump-seed.
- **Rest-stop scene order and clock contrast:** the clinic comes first. The doctor can buy Krypto **hours, perhaps a day**, and confirms that yellow sunlight would have bought strength rather than removed the poison. **Smash cut** to the mechanic: Kara's jump coil will take **two weeks** to regrow or replace. Kara's "Are you kidding me?" lands against the brutal mismatch. This immediately locks Krypto in the clinic, Kara's ship in the repair bay, and Kara/Ruthye onto public transit.
- **Rest-stop character lanes:** keep the three service figures rhythmically distinct rather than giving the whole station one deadpan voice. The doctor is an old public-service **robodoc** that has seen everything: kind, quietly sad, clinically exact, and physically gentle. Its sadness comes from accumulated triage, not Marvin-style self-pity; the casting lane is dry warmth rather than broad robot comedy. Preserve one small diagnostic-care button: **"Strong heart." / "Best dog in the universe." / "Not a medical category. But noted in patient file."** The mechanic is gruff, overworked, practical, and secretly generous — already beginning the repair while complaining that Kara broke it. The combined information/ticket agent is the underpaid salaryman of the universe: literally stuck at the desk, not malicious or truly unhelpful, simply trying to finish a shift without inheriting anyone else's catastrophe.
- **No WAR CAPTAIN/ticket-system routine:** reject Ultra's sponsor-menu rejection, WAR CAPTAIN classification, waiver scroll, and repeated gate-scanner payoff. If the booking flow naturally requires the agent to ask whether Kara is Ruthye's guardian, Kara may pause, say **"Yes,"** and receive a look from Ruthye. Preserve that as an optional one-beat relationship accent only; do not build surrounding machinery to force it, and cut it if the rest-stop flow is cleaner without it.
- **No secret prior revenge killing:** do not invent a separate person Kara hunted or executed after Argo. There is no target, confession, wordless kill-flash, or writer-side revenge credential. Kara's authority comes from the loss and self-destruction already on screen: drinking, isolation, and choosing red-sun powerlessness. On the bus, prefer the ambiguous warning **"It won't help, you know. When your world ends, nothing fills the hole. Most things just make it bigger."** Ruthye hears revenge; Kara is also speaking about what she has done to herself. Avoid "I've been down your path," which would reassert a literal revenge history. Ruthye is not persuaded, and Kara's later reach toward the flask carries the second meaning.
- **Why Kara chose the red sun:** during the medbay/ship sequence, Ruthye should be incredulous that Kara voluntarily lives beneath a sun that makes her weak. Kara's answer is simply **"That's the point."** Do not follow it with "no powers, no responsibilities" or an explanation that she came only to get drunk. The line should hold all of it at once: alcohol can affect her, she cannot hear or answer every need, nobody can demand Supergirl, and she has made herself physically incapable of keeping her promise. Red-sun weakness is chosen avoidance and self-punishment, not arbitrary power math.
- **No Evelie complicity in Bilki's death:** reject Ultra's Bilki-made stove and Evelie-component trail. Neither the Knolls nor Evelie bear inherited or material responsibility for Bilki's destruction, and Father does not need to defend his world from Krem's accusation. Krem's grievance is broader and more ordinary: nearby worlds minded their own business, protected their own people and problems, closed their doors, and let life continue around a world that was ending. The universe was indifferent rather than secretly conspiratorial. That abandonment explains Krem's wound but does not mitigate the predatory choices he made afterward. On Bilki, the ruins confirm that his loss was real; they do not retroactively put Evelie on trial.
- **Homecoming is relief, not a reckoning:** Mother and Ruthye's younger brother are simply happy she came back. They never wanted her to leave and could not stop her; do not import Ultra's angry "You left" confrontation or a verbal accountability catechism. Plant one spare promise before Ruthye leaves — Mother asks her to come back and Ruthye says she will — so that, inside Mother's homecoming embrace, Ruthye can say **"I promised I would."** The brother may then ask the literal question: **"Did you kill him?"** Ruthye looks meaningfully toward Kara and answers **"The debt has been repaid."** The child does not understand the distinction; Mother follows Ruthye's look and does. The blood-debt language has been transformed by the other half of the Code: Ruthye repaid the living debt and returned to the life she was in danger of abandoning. Mother need not explain her understanding aloud. Move from that recognition into food, birthday warmth, and Ruthye's first real laughter.
- **The Knoll family and cold-open register:** use Fable's names: **Andy Knoll**, **Marye Knoll**, **Ruthye Marye Knoll**, and five-year-old **Tomme Knoll**. Their dialogue must be newly written in `gpt5.5`'s natural register rather than lifted from Fable's more literary passages or Ultra's faux-rustic/cute family banter. They are a real, ordinary, loving family and fundamentally good people. Give the domestic exchange more work than disposable jokes: show Ruthye as loved but unusually serious, exact about promises and obligations, while Andy and Marye gently keep that seriousness connected to ordinary care. Express Evelie's culture through behavior, rhythms, and a small commitment Ruthye insists on keeping — not a child reciting the Code's thesis at dinner. Tomme can be recognizably five without becoming a punchline machine. The warmth and normality are what Krem violates.
- **Bilki station and reciprocal care:** Kara and Ruthye arrive through the remains of a civilian transit station, not a generic military ruin. Preserve two principal images rather than stacking a tour: a mural of Bilki alive — its people, children, transit, and Yellow Hills — and an arrivals board still cycling through towns that no longer exist. The ordinary civic machinery continuing without citizens makes Krem's real loss visible without dialogue or assigning blame to Evelie. The place physically and mentally destabilizes Kara: exhaustion, withdrawal, red-sun depletion, and Argo memory become inseparable. Sound and attention can fragment briefly, but do not spend the full Argo reveal early. Ruthye catches or steadies Kara, helps her sit or keep moving, and asks only if she is here. When a tear cuts through the ash on Kara's face, Ruthye returns the rest-stop washroom gesture by wiping it away or leaving a hand on Kara's shoulder. Kara does not joke or refuse; she accepts the contact, perhaps covering Ruthye's hand for one beat. The meaningful progression is **Kara tends Ruthye → Ruthye tends Kara → Kara accepts care**, not a decorative shot-for-shot callback.
- **Lobo sequence delegated to the drafting pass:** no further author fork is required before Draft 2. Use Fable's structural function and escalation, rewritten completely into `gpt5.5` cadence. Lobo may be wildly entertaining and his arrival may feel like an exhilarating collision with a different movie, but the camera must not make death itself aspirational: no slow-motion glory for a kill, no celebratory needle-drop montage, and no loving corpse tour. The fun comes from Lobo's scale, attitude, impossible physical solutions, and impatience; the accumulating ease of the deaths turns that fun queasy. He clears the ordinary combat qualifiers, leaves Krem helpless for Ruthye's choice, respects commitment to a Code without becoming a mentor, helps only because Ruthye stakes her life in terms he recognizes, and exits once the yellow-sun rescue is complete. Give the sequence a dedicated revision pass after the merged draft if the funny-to-horrifying turn does not feel right.

Don't fall in love with your own dialogue. Things that are better left unsaid... should be.
Dialogue should reflect the thesis, not monologue it. When we are on the nose, it better be saved because it's IMPORTANT.

## Structural additions for the next draft (Opus consult)

Working notes toward a stronger cut. **Base assumption:** `draft-script-gpt5.5.md` has the best dialogue and vibe of the three, but it's too **breezy** — it needs more **meat and gravitas** grafted from `draft-script-opus.md` (weight, atmosphere, the flashback) and `draft-script-fable.md` (visual grammar, Krem texture, Lobo). These are candidates, not commitments; nothing here should override the Watch-outs above (don't over-explain, don't wallow, carry it in symbol and repetition).

### The lens: this is a *promise* story, not a trauma story

The double death of Krypton/Argo is the **wound** (why she's numb, the red sun, the bottle) — but it is not the engine. The engine is the buried line both drafts already have: **"Promise me you'll be good." — "I promise."** She has been *failing that promise by hiding.* Goodness-as-active-choice (bible rule #1) is the arc; the trauma only explains the avoidance. Test for every addition below: **does it deepen the wound, or does it charge the promise?** Imply the wound in fragments; build toward the promise. This is the guardrail against trauma-wallowing.

### 1. Fragment the flashback; make the *told* version lie

The strongest structural move. Split Kara's backstory into a **told version** (sanitized, spoken) and a **seen version** (suppressed, shown), and let the gap between them be the drama.

- **What she *says* (bus, Act II, aloud) — the sanitized cut:** Krypton went first and fast, never shown ("the sky I was born under just… stopped"). Then Argo *survived* — and that slow second death is the one that broke her: the poisoning, people going "in ones and twos," her mother's hands going cold, her father putting her in the pod. **She skips the fight, and she skips the promise.** The omission should be *audible* — Ruthye, who reads people like doors, clocks the gap.
- **What we *see* (Act III flashback opener) — the suppressed material:** Kara **refusing** to go, saying cruel things, her mother already gone, the father *making* her leave, and the promise. It lands as the Act III opener because she has *just kept it* — stepping into Ruthye's blade ("that's my code") is the promise made flesh, and only then do we learn where it came from.
- **Runtime:** ~neutral. Redistributes the ~3 min Act III flashback into 20–40s shards (morning nightmare, bus glimpses) and lets the Act III version be the only *complete* telling.

### 2. Two content changes inside the flashback

- **Mother dies of poison → Krypto is poisoned now (the missing rhyme).** Sequence the mother's death as a *slow poisoning* on dying Argo (the "last hands touching as she fades"), not a launch-chamber explosion. Then the present-day A-plot — Krypto fading from Brigand poison — *re-stages the deepest wound.* In Act I she was helpless and numb watching poison take someone she loved; now she refuses. The plot suddenly resonates with the backstory. **(Keep the poisons distinct: Argo = Kryptonite/radiation, Krypto = Brigand venom. See Watch-outs.)**
- **Puppy Krypto goes into the pod with her.** He is "the last of Argo that still breathes" — make it literal. A puppy handed in beside her: *"take this piece of home, and live."* His poisoning then threatens her with losing her world a *third* time, and re-weaponizes the film's saddest beat ("the quiet after") — right now she isn't alone in that pod; if Krypto dies, the quiet finally becomes total. Raises the central stakes for free. *Caution: puppy-Krypto is not comfort — he's the thing she's kept alive instead of living herself.*

### 3. The revenge question — no secret credential

Answer to "who do you avenge a homeworld against?": **no one.** Krypton/Argo dying is grief you *cannot* avenge — nothing to punch, no debt to collect — which is exactly why it curdled into drinking and hiding instead of a hunt. A named cosmic villain (Brainiac, etc.) cheapens it and breaks the mirror with Ruthye, whose grief *has* a face.

Do **not** add a separate post-Argo revenge killing to give Kara moral credentials. If it is never shown or named, it adds hidden biography rather than drama; if it is confessed, it turns Kara into the experienced lecturer. Her warning is earned by what the audience has already watched her do to the wound: drink, isolate, ignore family, and deliberately make herself powerless.

The cleaner survivor geometry is visible on screen: **Krem turns grief outward into predation; Kara turns it inward into numbness; Ruthye is still choosing a direction.** Lobo remains the control case for violence without grief or moral cost.

On the bus, Kara can say, with no explanatory follow-up: *"It won't help, you know. When your world ends, nothing fills the hole. Most things just make it bigger."* Ruthye hears a warning about revenge; Kara is also naming the bottle and the red sun. Her later hand returning to the flask, then stopping, does more than a revenge confession would.

### 4. Expand the drunk-Kara open — but make it *specific* avoidance

A bit more runway (+1–2 min), with a job beyond "establish drunk." Show her **actively choosing numbness**: unread messages piling up, the face-down photo, the ritual of the bottle, an almost-callback to Clark that she doesn't make, Krypto as the one thing she still shows up for. It should read as *competence at hiding*, not a bender — a routine she's good at. Pays off when the same hand-to-flask reflex returns on the bus and she finally puts it away. Tuning fork, not a sequence.

### 5. Bilki as the *third* dead world — and the relationship rung we're missing

Bilki is under-used. It is her third dead world *and* Krem's homeworld — the Brigand worldview made landscape. Walk her through the **remains of a civilization** (schoolyard under ash, a mural of children with painted hands — gpt5.5 gestures at this) so she's moving through both her own grief and the origin of the man who murders.

- **Have her be physically sick on the hike** — withdrawal *and* retraumatization at once, hard to tell apart (she dropped the flask; this externalizes it) — and **let Ruthye tend her.** This is the **highest-value single addition**: care currently flows one direction until very late (Kara shields Ruthye, buys her figs, wipes her tear). A mid-movie rung where **the child takes care of the adult** makes the bond *earned*, pays off the washroom beat in reverse, and fully cooks Ruthye's later choice to save Kara ("take us up"). Fixes shape *and* character in one beat.

### 6. Hold the line on Clark / no "discovery" scene

Resist a landing-and-discovery beat (à la the 2026 film). This is a **Kara/Ruthye two-hander**; Clark is a *destination*, not a character — voicemail in Act I, warm payoff in mid-credits. A discovery scene pulls focus. Keep Clark to one feather-light touch (the almost-callback in the open). Note: **Ruthye already is the "someone who finds her"** — a kid walks into a bar and finds her. That's our discovery scene.

### 7. Don't over-extend the rest stop

It already carries worldbuilding + antidote reveal + washroom + vendors, and it's the film's exhale. One more tiny character beat at most; no new set piece (guards against the Act-I/II front-loading the gpt5.5 runtime numbers flagged).

### Priority ranking

1. **Ruthye tends sick Kara on Bilki** — relationship reciprocity + Bilki mirror + withdrawal payoff, one beat.
2. **Mother-dies-of-poison ↔ Krypto-poisoned rhyme** — A-plot resonates with the wound.
3. **Fragmented flashback with the suppressed promise** — the structural spine; told-vs-seen gap.
4. **Puppy Krypto = last of Argo** — raises central stakes for free.
5. **Kara's grief warning without a secret revenge history** — let the unfillable-hole line and the flask behavior connect Ruthye's vengeance to Kara's visible self-destruction.
6. **Expanded, *specific* drunk open** — tuning fork.

Most of this is redistribution, not padding. On the opus draft (~94) it lands ~98–100 (feature-max — be disciplined). On the **gpt-5.5 base (~73) it all fits and still lands ~82–88** — a concrete argument for gpt-5.5 as the structural base into which opus's gravitas and fable's grammar are grafted.

### Why the dialogue is not the donor

The issue is less any one bad line than a repeated **cadence and tonal system** across the whole draft.

#### 1. Too many exchanges are built as polished semantic reversals

The recurring Ultra rhythm is:

1. Character A makes a precise assertion.
2. Character B reframes one word in it.
3. Character A supplies a dry button.

Examples include "stealing with extra grammar," objecting to the *verb* in a docking instruction, "made us honest," and Ruthye "accepting" Kara's responsibility. Any one of these can work. In accumulation, dialogue begins to sound engineered rather than overheard, and scenes repeatedly land on the same little click.

#### 2. Kara and Ruthye converge on the same adversarial intelligence

Kara is dry, procedural, and verbally evasive. Ruthye is literal, legalistic, and verbally exact. Those are compatible but distinct ideas. In Ultra they too often become two versions of the same hyper-articulate speaker, trading symmetrical corrections at equal speed.

For the target draft:

- **Kara** should be spare, funny when cornered, and capable of losing the conversational beat. Her wit is defense, not a standing performance.
- **Ruthye** should sound formal and filed when armored, but still occasionally reveal that she is thirteen through bluntness, silence, misread custom, or an unfinished thought.
- **Krem** should be precise and calm, but his history should not become a lecture every time he speaks.
- **Lobo** should be blunt, obscene, and anti-literary — the character least likely to polish a thesis.

#### 3. The whole universe shares one joke-writing voice

The Healer, Mechanic, Information Clerk, Ticket Agent, Bus Steward, drop-shell voice, ring voice, and ship AI all deliver adjacent varieties of bureaucratic deadpan. The individual premises are often good; the density flattens the world because everyone appears to have the same comic timing.

Mining rule: keep **one governing comedy lane per sequence**. At the rest stop, choose the strongest clerk/waiver bit and let other functionaries be ordinary, impatient, strange, or silent.

#### 4. Emotional scenes often finish explaining their own meaning

The bus explicitly covers Kara's prior revenge killing, why it qualified as execution, how long relief lasted, and the conclusion that revenge will not help. The Bilki walk explains how Krem turned a true grievance into predation. The homecoming has Tavi ask why Ruthye went, whether it ended anything, what she learned, and what she wanted more.

These scenes contain the right underlying ideas, but the draft frequently says the lesson after the image or action has already carried it. This conflicts directly with the standing rule: **know it and show it; do not brief the audience on it.**

#### 5. Silence rarely gets the last word

### Translation rules when grafting Ultra material

1. **Extract the dramatic function before the wording.** Write down what changes, not the line that announces it.
2. **Every Ultra graft must replace something.** No additive graft enters without identifying the duplicate beat it displaces.
3. **Rewrite in the receiving scene's voice.** `gpt5.5` cadence remains the default; Fable/Opus determine image and weight where already selected.
4. **Break the ping-pong.** Allow pauses, failed answers, interruptions, physical business, and unequal conversational control.
5. **Limit semantic buttons.** One polished reversal can define a scene; five make every character sound alike.
6. **Reserve overt theme language.** The Code's life-before-blood priority earns one explicit turn because it changes Ruthye's action; do not turn Ultra's exact "life in your keeping" phrase into a refrain. Most thematic conclusions should remain image or subtext.
7. **Choose one comic system per location.** Preserve contrast among ship, station, bus, Bilki, and Lobo rather than letting all five run the same deadpan routine.
8. **End scenes earlier.** After the irreversible choice or strongest image, test cutting the answer, joke, or explanation that follows.
