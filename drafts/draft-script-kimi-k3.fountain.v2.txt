FADE IN:

EXT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - EVELIE - DUSK

A red sun sits fat and low over tilled fields, the color of an
ember that has been burning too long. Everything it touches --
the rows of grain, the stone fences, a tilted windmill turning
with arthritic patience -- looks embalmed. Preserved. Waiting.

A farmhouse. Timber and fieldstone, smoke rising from the
chimney in a straight, unhurried line. Somebody built this on
purpose. Somebody keeps it.

SUPER: "EVELIE. A FARM WORLD UNDER A RED SUN."

INT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - KITCHEN - CONTINUOUS

Warmth. Lamplight. A long table scarred by a thousand meals.

BREN (40s), weathered, forearms like rope, cuts a pie with the
ceremony of a man performing sacred work. LISSA (40s), his wife,
carries a pot past him and, without looking, moves the knife
three inches to the left so he won't cut himself on the reach.
They have done this dance for years.

RUTHYE (13) sets out plates. Braids. A serious face -- serious
in a way that unsettles adults, though no one at this table is
adult enough to notice anymore. She folds each napkin into a
perfect square. Precision as love.

Through the window behind her: Ruthye's handiwork
everywhere. The fence rail replaced with fresh timber. The
windmill's new bearing, oiled and turning true. On this
farm, when something breaks, the thirteen-year-old fixes
it, files it mentally, and moves on.

                         BREN
           Ruthye. The hinge on the cold
           shed's gone again.

                         RUTHYE
           I know. I have the part on order.

                         BREN
           Course you do.

WILL (6) is under the table, feeding crusts to a floor.

                         BREN (CONT'D)
           ...and then the tax assessor says, "But
           this is a value-added province," and I
           say, "Then add some value to my fence,
           which has been down since winter --"

                         RUTHYE
           That is not how the joke goes.

                         BREN
           It's how the joke goes now.

                         LISSA
           It wasn't how the joke went the first
           eleven times, either.

Will giggles from underneath the table. Bren serves pie. His
HANDS -- rough, careful -- slide a plate in front of each of
his children. Hold on the hands a beat longer than we need to.

                         BREN
           Eat. Tomorrow we fix the fence.

                         WILL
           You said that in winter.

                         BREN
           And it's been tomorrow ever since.
           That's how time works, Will. You'll
           learn.

                         LISSA
           He'll learn by watching you not fix
           a fence.

                         RUTHYE
           I fixed the fence.

                         BREN
           And that's why you're my favorite.

                         WILL
           HEY --

Laughter. The real kind, the kind that lives in a house
and not in a memory yet. Bren steals a bite off Lissa's
fork. She lets him. Will narrates the theft to the floor.

ON THE WALL: a shelf of small things. A painted stone. A
child's drawing of four stick figures and a sun. A ribbon
from a harvest fair. The archaeology of a family, displayed.

A KNOCK at the door. Not loud. Bren looks at Lissa -- travelers
are rare, not alarming. He rises, wiping his hands on a cloth.

                         BREN
           Company. Lissa, another plate.

He opens the door.

KREM (50s) stands in the red dusk. Long coat, dust to the
knees, a face that has been tired for twenty years. Behind him,
THREE BRIGANDS -- mismatched armor, rifles held low and casual,
the way professionals carry things they don't expect to need.

                         KREM
           Evening.

He walks in. Past Bren. Not a push -- a man entering a room he
already considers his. The Brigands follow, fanning out through
the house with the unhurried interest of appraisers.

                         BREN
           -- can I help you?

                         KREM
           You can.

Krem sits at the table. At Bren's place. He pulls the pie
toward himself, cuts a slice with Bren's knife, eats.

For a moment nobody speaks. The only sounds: Krem chewing, and
Will's small breathing under the table.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           That's good pie.

He cuts a second slice. Slides the plate toward the floor
beside the table -- toward Will, hidden underneath.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           Boy. Pie's better when you're not
           under a table.

Will doesn't move. Doesn't breathe. Under the tablecloth,
Ruthye's hand finds her brother's and closes around it.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           Suit yourself.

Krem eats the slice himself, unbothered.

Lissa moves, slow, to stand between the Brigands and the
children. Ruthye has gone very still. Her eyes track Krem the
way you'd track weather coming over a hill.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           My ship needs some things. Grain.
           Ammunition. Cell charges. Whatever
           weapons you've got -- and you've got
           some; everyone out here does.

He eats. He is not performing menace. He is a man itemizing.

                         BREN
           You can have the grain. And charges.
           We'll load the wagon tonight and you
           can be on your way by --

                         KREM
           Don't rush me, friend. I like it here.

And now Krem looks around the room -- really looks. At the
lamp. The patched curtains. The four plates. The love, sitting
in plain view like silver on a shelf.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           I had a table like this.

                         BREN
           ...I'm sorry?

                         KREM
           I was like you, once. So haughty.

He turns the fork in his fingers, studying it.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           My world had cities that made their own
           weather. Bilki. The Yellow Hills --
           you could see them from every roof,
           gold all year. We had arguments about
           art. We thought the universe was a
           place with manners.

                          (beat)
           Then the sky opened, and the ones who
           came through it wanted what we had.
           And you know what the rest of the
           universe did?

                          (off Bren's silence)
           It traded with them. Watched. Sent
           condolences, at bulk rates.

He says it all quietly. The violence is in the past tense. The
man at the table seems tired, not cruel -- which is worse.

IN THE ROOMS: the Brigands keep moving. One opens the
cupboard, appraises, takes the good tin. Another stands in
the doorway of the children's room a moment too long,
looking at the small beds, then moves on. They are not
ransacking. They are shopping.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           You know what I learned, after? The
           destroyers were honest. That's the
           strange part. They said what they
           were.

                          (he eats; unhurried)
           It was everyone else. The neighbors
           who waved at the ships going over.
           The traders who asked for a discount.
           The ones who took us in, after, and
           charged us rent on our own graves.

                          (beat)
           Politeness, friend. That's the real
           killer in this universe. Everyone
           was so polite.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           Worlds like this one heard, I expect.
           Everyone heard.

                          (he eats; unhurried)
           You minded your fields. Kept your own
           problems. Watched the ships go over,
           then went back to your dinners.

                          (beat)
           Nobody did anything. That's all
           anyone ever does. Politely.

                         BREN
           I'm sorry about your world. Truly.

He means it. It costs him nothing, and they both know it.

                         KREM
           Hm.

A small sound. Not agreement. Not argument. He lets the
sympathy sit in the room like a held breath.

Krem finishes the slice. Sets the fork down, tines aligned with
the edge of the table -- a precise little gesture from a
precise, ruined man.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           I'll take the weapons now.

                         BREN
           There's a locker in the barn. Rifles,
           my service blade. Take it all, it's
           yours. You don't need to --

Krem's gaze drifts past him to the shelf. The painted
stone. The drawing of four stick figures and a sun. He
looks at it the way a man looks through a window into a
house he used to live in.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           That your daughter's?

                         BREN
           ...My son's. He's six.

                         KREM
           Six.
              (a breath through the nose;
               almost a laugh, nowhere near
               one)
           I had a boy. Older than him. He used
           to draw the weather.

The room goes still in a new way. Krem doesn't explain.
Doesn't soften. He turns from the shelf and it's gone from
his face like it was never there.

Krem stands. Crosses to the wall -- where BREN'S SWORD hangs on
two pegs, old, well-kept, a soldier's memento. He lifts it
down. Weighs it. Nods to a Brigand: take it.

And then, almost as an afterthought --

Krem strikes LISSA. Open hand, across the face. She goes down
against the counter.

The room DETONATES. Bren lunges -- two Brigands catch him,
drive him against the wall, a forearm across his throat. Ruthye
snatches Will out from under the table and pulls him into
herself. Will is crying without sound. Lissa, dazed, bleeding
at the lip, claws at the counter to rise.

                         BREN
           Don't you touch her -- DON'T --

                         KREM
           Shh.

And it does go quiet. Not calm -- quiet the way a held rope is
quiet.

Krem stands in the middle of the kitchen, looking at what he's
done with a mild, clinical disappointment, as if the noise had
been the regrettable part.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           See how quickly it goes loud. And then
           it cools. It always cools.

He's right. It's cooling. The Brigands have Bren pinned but
alive. Lissa is upright, holding her face, eyes on her
children. The weapons are being carried out the door. The
transaction is ugly, and survivable, and over.

The off-ramp is right there. We can all see it.

Krem turns to go.

And stops.

RUTHYE is looking at him.

She has Will folded against her chest, one arm barricading him,
her hand over his eyes. And over her own small barricade she is
staring at Krem with open, level, thirteen-year-old defiance --
the unshakeable conviction that the world is fair, that this is
an aberration, that someone will answer for it.

Krem recognizes it. We watch him recognize it. Something in his
face -- the thing that chose, and kept choosing -- refuses to
let it stand.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           Look at that.

He crosses back. Takes Bren's chin in his hand, almost gently,
and turns the father's face toward his daughter.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           She thinks this is unusual.

                          (beat)
           I did too.

He draws a long knife and opens Bren's throat. Deliberate.
Unhurried. His eyes never leave Ruthye's.

Lissa SCREAMS. Will buries his face in Ruthye's shoulder. The
Brigands let Bren drop and step back, practiced, out of the way
of the blood.

Ruthye does not scream. Ruthye does not move. Krem watches
her watch her father die -- with the patience of a man
delivering a lesson he believes is a mercy.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           This is what the world is.

He says it like he's doing her a favor. Like he's the first
honest man she's ever met.

He wipes the knife on Bren's shirt. Leaves it in him. Walks
out, past Lissa crumpling over her husband, into the red dusk.

His men follow, carrying the grain, the charges, the sword.

EXT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - CONTINUOUS

The Brigands load the wagon in the dark, unhurried. Krem
stands a moment at the fence line, looking back at the
lit windows of the house. A man checking that the lesson
landed.

Behind him, through the open door, small shapes move:
Lissa on the floor with Bren. Ruthye, standing. Watching.

Krem holds her eye through the doorway, across the yard,
across everything. Then he turns, climbs onto the wagon,
and the Brigands roll out into the red dark, taking the
farm's winter with them.

INT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - KITCHEN - CONTINUOUS

The door stands open on the dark.

LISSA crawls the last two feet to Bren and does the
impossible arithmetic of a hand pressed to a throat,
knowing. Her sound is not a scream anymore. It's smaller
than a scream. It's the sound a house makes on the inside.

WILL is shaking against Ruthye's ribs, face pressed into
her, and Ruthye holds him, and holds him, and doesn't look
at her mother, or her father, or the blood finding the
grooves in the floorboards her father cut himself.

ON RUTHYE

Holding her brother. The boy is huddled into her, shaking --
he knows, but he hasn't seen. Her arms are around him and her
eyes are fixed on the door Krem walked through.

Not grief. Not shock. Rage -- the kind that has already
decided something, before the mind catches up.

HOLD on her eyes.

SMASH TO BLACK.

TITLE: "S U P E R G I R L"

Then, smaller: "a woman of tomorrow"

INT. KARA'S SHIP - BERTH - EVELIE PORT - NIGHT

Darkness. Breathing. A woman's eyes OPEN --

-- and we are inside the nightmare with her. FRAGMENTS:

A sky the color of a wound. A floating city tilting, slow,
like a dropped plate deciding whether to break.

A WOMAN's hand going still in dark hair. A bed by a window.

A MAN's face through glass, mouth shaping words that don't
arrive.

The ground shaking. A door closing. Glass.

A bird, startled out of a transit garden, four notes
cut off mid-song.

BACK TO --

KARA ZOR-EL (21 today, though nobody here knows it) bolts
upright in a cramped bunk, gasping. Sweat. The shriek of the
nightmare still in her ears --

-- and fading into the ordinary hum of a ship's berth. Her
hand is already moving. It finds the bottle beside the bunk
before her eyes are fully open. That's the tell: this isn't
a morning. It's a practice.

She drinks. Winces. Breathes. Becomes, roughly, a person.

KRYPTO -- a white dog, mid-sized, built like a comma that
loves her -- puts his chin on the bunk. Tail. Concerned eyes
that have seen this exact morning a hundred times.

                         KARA
           Don't look at me like that.

He looks at her like that. She scratches his ear despite
herself.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Yeah. Me too.

She swings her legs down. The berth is a disaster: bottles,
a boot in a bowl, a battered OVERSIZED LEATHER JACKET slung
over the back of the one chair -- too big, beat to hell, half
armor and half blanket. She puts it on like other people put
on excuses.

A console light blinks. A CHIME, warm and calm, like a
hotel lobby.

                         SHIP (V.O.)
           Good morning. Fuel reserves at eleven
           percent.

                         KARA
           Later.

                         SHIP (V.O.)
           You have said "later" six times.

                         KARA
           Then you know how it goes.

She thumbs on a handheld COMM UNIT on the fold-down desk.
One unread message. CLARK (V.O.) -- warm, midwestern,
worried, trying not to sound worried:

                         CLARK (V.O.)
           Hey, Kara. It's me. Again.
              (a breath; a smile in it)
           Happy birthday. Twenty-one. I know
           you're not doing the party thing, I
           know you need your -- whatever this is.
           But Ma made the cake anyway and it's
           in my freezer, and it's getting
           freezer burn, Kara, it's a really
           good cake --
              (the warmth folds)
           Just call me back. Kid. Please.
           Whatever you're doing out there, it's
           not worth disappearing over. Come
           home.

The message ends. Kara sits very still. Krypto's head comes
up, watching her.

She picks the comm up. Her thumb finds CALL BACK. Hovers.
The light of it plays on her face, and for a second the
whole movie hangs on one thumb.

She sets the comm down. Does not call back. Gets the bottle
again instead -- the decision made with the ease of long
rehearsal --

-- and stops. Looks at the bottle. At the dog. At the red
light leaking around the berth's shutter.

She drinks anyway.

EXT. EVELIE PORT - DAWN

The red sun climbs over a shabby spaceport: grain silos next
to landing cradles, a fuel farm, a fence mended with a
repurposed wing panel. Kara's ship sits in a dirt cradle --
a blunt, lived-in courier tub, older than she is.

Through the open berth shutter, the red light falls across
her face. She watches it like a person watching weather
from inside. Nothing moves in her expression. That's the
problem. Nothing is supposed to.

INT. KARA'S SHIP - BERTH - LATER

The routine. Kara pours yesterday's drink down the sink and
today's into a mug. She passes the small mirror by the hatch
and -- practiced, unconscious -- does not look at it.

On the console: the COMM unit's message queue. CLARK (4).
The number sits there like an unopened door. She covers it
with the mug as she drinks. Out of sight is a kind of
answer.

Krypto retrieves his own ball, drops it at his own feet,
waits, retrieves it again. Self-sufficient joy. It still
doesn't work on her today.

                         KARA
           Okay, listen. We're not doing
           anything today. We're staying.
           Nothing is happening.

Krypto wags anyway. He knows something she doesn't. Or
he's a dog.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           That's fair.

INT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - KITCHEN - DAWN

The same red light, forty miles away, through the same kind
of window. Two lives, one sun, not yet touching.

The kitchen has been cleaned. That is somehow worse. The
table is bare. Through the window, in the north field, a
fresh mound of earth with the fence tools still stuck in
it. Nobody has put the tools away. Nobody can.

LISSA sits with both hands around a cold cup, wearing the
stillness of a person who has decided not to come apart in
front of her children.

RUTHYE, in traveling clothes, moves through the room with
quiet purpose: bread into a satchel, a water skin, a
small grungy metal-and-glass device of her own assembly --
a TRACKER, its screen dark. Nothing to follow yet.

On her forearm, written in ink in her own careful hand:
words. The code. So she won't forget them.

WILL stands in the doorway in his pajamas, holding the
doorframe like it might hold him back.

                         RUTHYE
           Will. Eat your breakfast.

                         WILL
           Are you going to kill him?

Ruthye stops. Lissa closes her eyes.

                         RUTHYE
           The code demands that I find my
           father's killer. The law of our
           people gives the right to blood.

                         WILL
           That's not what I asked.

She looks at him -- and for half a second she is just his
sister. She crosses, kneels, takes his face in her hands.

                         RUTHYE
           Hold the house. You are the man of it
           now. Lissa can't do the north field
           alone.

                         WILL
           I'm six.

                         RUTHYE
           Then be six very well.

She kisses his forehead. He grabs her sleeve; she lets him,
for exactly one breath, then gently peels his fingers loose.

Lissa rises. Crosses to the hearth. Kneels, works loose a
floorboard, and brings out a long wrapped bundle. She holds
it out.

                         LISSA
           Your father's field sword. He kept the
           good one on the wall for pride.

Ruthye takes it. Heavy. Real.

                         LISSA (CONT'D)
           If the code is taking you --
              (her voice does one small break
               and then reforms)
           -- take this too.

Ruthye buckles it on. At the door she stops.

                         RUTHYE
           I will come back when it is done.

                         LISSA
           That's what your father said about the
           fence. Every winter.

A flicker -- not a smile, the ghost of the place a smile
used to live. Ruthye almost answers it. Can't. Goes.

Lissa watches her daughter walk into the red morning,
carrying a sword and a sandwich.

EXT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - FENCE LINE - CONTINUOUS

Will follows her as far as the half-mended fence. He is
not allowed past it. That has always been the rule.

                         WILL
           Ruthye.

She stops. Doesn't turn. If she turns, she stays.

                         WILL (CONT'D)
           He sang again last night. In my head.
           The field song. The bad one.

A beat. Ruthye's shoulders do something small.

                         RUTHYE
           Sing it wrong. That's how he liked
           it. He said if you sing it wrong
           they can't take it away.

She goes. Will stands at the fence line, holding the rail
his father never finished, and sings the field song,
wrong on purpose, quietly, at her back.

EXT. EVELIE - THE LONG TRACK - SERIES OF SHOTS

Six days, compressed:

-- Ruthye walking a ridgeline at dusk, the Brigand engine
smoke a smudge on the horizon. She sights it through a tin
monocular, fixes the line, walks on.

-- Ruthye sleeping in a hayloft, sword unsheathed across
her knees, one hand resting on the inked words of her
forearm. Like a rosary.

-- Ruthye trading eggs at a crossroads for directions,
exact change counted, no words wasted. Which way the
smoke went. That's all she needs.

-- Rain. Ruthye under a culvert, eating the last of the
bread, looking at the words inked on her forearm. She
reads them. She does not look convinced by them anymore.
She follows the pulse anyway.

EXT. EVELIE PORT - RIDGE ABOVE - LATER

The port spread out below: grain silos next to landing
cradles, a fuel farm, the rough end of nowhere.

And parked in a dirt cradle at that rough end: a brutalist
raider, all angles and welded-on armor. The ship from the
horizon. Brigands drift between it and the port's bars,
unhurried, spending what they took.

RUTHYE stands on the ridge a long moment, reading it the
way she reads everything. Then she looks at the inked
words on her forearm, pulls her hood up, and walks down
into the port.

Never run if you can walk. Her father taught her that.

INT. THE LOW SUN - DAY

A bar crouched between a chandlery and a grain office,
dark inside against the red day. KARA at her usual table,
Krypto under her stool, the bottle at half mast. Outside
the window, the red sun does its slow slide.

A DOCKHAND tries his luck, sliding onto the bench opposite
her, smile first.

                         DOCKHAND
           Buy you a --

                         KARA
           No.

                         DOCKHAND
           You don't know what I was --

                         KARA
           I do. I'm a great guesser. It's my
           one talent. No.

He retreats. Kara raises her glass to Krypto, who sighs
the sigh of a dog who has watched this exact play on a
hundred worlds.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Happy birthday to me.

She drinks. The red light moves another inch across the
table.

EXT. EVELIE PORT - THE LOW SUN - LATER

A hand-painted sign over the door: THE LOW SUN, and below
it, in smaller, wearier paint: NO CURRENCY TOO STRANGE.

Ruthye looks at the door. Adjusts the satchel on her
shoulder -- heavier than it was this morning. Goes in.

INT. THE LOW SUN - CONTINUOUS

A long room under a low ceiling, lit by jar-lamps and the
red bleed of the windows. Port town ecology: dockhands,
grain brokers, two things in cloaks that might be pilgrims
or might be luggage. A BARTENDER polishes a glass with the
air of a man who has polished this exact glass through four
bar fights and intends a fifth.

At the far end: KARA, alone with a bottle and a glass she
has stopped bothering with. The jacket. The posture of a
woman hiding in plain sight.

KRYPTO lies under her stool, chin on her boot. The one
living thing in the room she answers to.

                         KARA
           Another year, buddy.

Krypto's tail taps the floor.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Don't start. I can hear you not
           saying it. You're very loud when
           you don't say it.

She pours. The BARTENDER slides a small dish of something
fried toward her, unasked.

                         BARTENDER
           Eat something.

                         KARA
           That's not on the house, is it.

                         BARTENDER
           It's on your tab. Which is now a
           historical document.

He moves on. Kara eats one, mostly for the dog, who gets
half. Tradition.

Ruthye steps in. Small, dusty, sword on her hip, tracker in
her palm. The room does its arithmetic and dismisses her --
all except one man.

GRASH -- a dock ruffian built like a loading pallet --
slides off his stool into her path.

                         GRASH
           House rule. No unaccompanied --

Ruthye walks into him like he isn't there. He grabs her
shoulder --

-- and then he is on one knee, his own knife in her hand,
his wrist bent to the edge of its patience. One motion.
Clean. No hesitation, no anger. A chore completed.

                         RUTHYE
           I am accompanied by the law of my
           people.

She hands the knife back, handle first, and walks on. Grash
stays kneeling, re-evaluating several life choices.

The room recalculates. Kara, through the bottle's haze,
watches the kid cross the room -- intrigued despite herself.
Or maybe she recognizes something.

Ruthye stops at Kara's table. Takes in the bottle, the
jacket, the dog. Sits down opposite, uninvited.

                         KARA
           ...No.

                         RUTHYE
           You are the strongest person in this
           room.

                         KARA
           You just folded a man like a map.
           Buy yourself a drink.

                         RUTHYE
           I am thirteen.

                         KARA
           It's that kind of bar.

Krypto's head comes up at the word "thirteen." He slides
out from under the stool and puts his chin on Ruthye's
knee, tail going. Ruthye looks down at him. Something
crosses her face -- the last normal feeling available --
and she scratches his ear. Once. Like a stamp on a form.

                         RUTHYE
           Your dog is a good dog.

                         KARA
           He's the worst. He eats my shoes and
           he judges me.
              (off Krypto's look)
           You do.

Ruthye places the tracker on the table, screen up. Dark.
Nothing to follow yet.

                         RUTHYE
           Six days ago, raiders came to my
           family's homestead. They took grain,
           charges, and weapons. They killed my
           father.

She says it the way she'd read a weather report, and the
flatness is louder than a wail.

                         RUTHYE (CONT'D)
           I followed them overland for six
           days. Smoke. Wagon ruts. Questions
           at crossroads. Their ship is parked
           in the fuel cradles right now. They
           are not leaving this world yet.

                         KARA
           ...Okay. Wow. You're, what -- a
           bounty hunter? You're a bounty hunter
           with homework.

                         RUTHYE
           The code of my people demands I find
           my father's killer. The law gives the
           right of blood to the child. I am the
           elder child.

                          (beat)
           I am not asking you to find him. I
           have found him. I am not asking you
           to kill him. That is mine.
              (a beat; the smallest
               recalibration)
           There are nine of them. I require
           muscle.

Kara almost smiles. Then doesn't. She pours, drinks,
studies the kid over the glass.

                         KARA
           And after?

                         RUTHYE
           ...What?

                         KARA
           The code. Say it all works. You find
           him, you do the thing the code says.
           What does the code say happens to
           you, after?

Ruthye's jaw sets. The answer exists. She's read it. She
just doesn't like how it sounds out loud.

                         RUTHYE
           The debt is paid. The family is
           restored to honor.

                         KARA
           And you sleep better?

                         RUTHYE
           The code does not concern itself
           with sleep.

                         KARA
           Yeah. They never do.

Kara drinks. Ruthye watches her do it, filing that too.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           What's your name?

                         RUTHYE
           Ruthye.

           Kara.
              (she gestures at the room)

           the floor. There was no spill.

                         KARA
           Kid. Ruthye. Look at me.
              (Ruthye does; it costs
               Kara something)

The bottle stops halfway to the glass.

Kara goes quiet. Not a speech -- a crack, the kind alcohol

                         KARA
           Go home.

                         RUTHYE
           --What?

                         KARA
           You heard me. Go home.
              (quieter; worse)
           You have a mother. You have a little

           waiting, and you're here. Renting
           strangers.
              (off Ruthye's stillness)

For the first time, Ruthye's composure flickers -- not

                         RUTHYE
           The code demands it. My father
           demands it.

                         KARA
           Your father demands you be thirteen.

                         RUTHYE
           My father is dead.

                         KARA
           And your mother isn't. Neither is
           your brother. And every day you
           spend on this --
              (she stops herself; too much.
               Drinks.)

           a good pitch. I've had all night to
           work on it.

Ruthye studies her. Whatever she sees -- the bottle, the

The DRIVE CRYSTAL in its grungy case, baseball-sized,
glowing like a captive coal.

                         RUTHYE
           Their ship's drive core. I removed
           it from the engine cowling an hour

           family's winter in the port's bars.

                          (beat)
           They aren't leaving without this.

Kara stares at it. Then at Ruthye. Somewhere under the

                         KARA
           You robbed nine raiders.

                         RUTHYE
           I walked. Running is suspicious. My
           father taught me that.

                         KARA
           Your father sounds like he knew some

                         RUTHYE
           He did.
              (a single beat; the flatness

           He does not anymore.

She slides the tracker beside the case and thumbs it on.

                         RUTHYE (CONT'D)

           Not coordinates. A direction.

           He cannot go home without my knowing

The case CHIRPS. Bright. Cheerful. Answering something.

Kara goes very still. She knows that sound. Every spacer
does.

                         KARA
           ...Tell me that wasn't a recovery
           ping.

The door SLAMS open.

KREM fills the frame, dust and long coat, with SIX BRIGANDS

                         KREM
           Afternoon, all.

He scans the room the way a man checks a coat pocket. Finds

Tuts -- one dry sound.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           The farm girl.

He crosses the room. Unhurried. Men step out of his way

of man walks into a bar and doesn't look at the exits.

He stops a polite distance from the table. Looks at Ruthye
with something that is not quite recognition at first,
and then is.

                         KREM (CONT'D)
           I know you. The eyes.

                          (a small, tired smile)
           You haven't lost them. Most do, after.
           The fight goes out of them, they
           learn how the world is. But not you.

                          (beat)
           How's the world?

Ruthye is on her feet, sword half out. Kara's hand closes
around her forearm.

                         KARA
           Sit down, kid.

                         RUTHYE
           Release me.

                         KARA
           Seven to two, and I am NOT the two
           you think I am right now --

                         KREM
           Girl has something of mine. Engine
           part. Walked off my ship not two
           hours ago -- and woke up, polite as

           I'll take it, and the little thief,

           your glasses.

He does not hurry.

                         KARA
           Okay. Counter-offer.
              (she stands; the chair
               scrapes; the room
               recalculates again)
           You walk out. You leave the kid, you
           leave the rock, you leave the sword

Krem looks at Kara. Really looks -- the way Ruthye did,

He half-smiles.

                         KREM
           Or?

                         KARA
           Or I stop being charming.

                         KREM
           ...I doubt that.

Ruthye draws her sword all the way out -- and sweeps the
crystal off the table into her satchel in the same motion.

The room DETONATES.

It is not a superhero fight. It is a bar fight that happens
to contain Supergirl -- and a version of her with residue,
not command.

Kara moves once, FAST -- a blur-step that takes two Brigands'
rifles out of their hands before the first one fires --

-- then a punch that hits too hard and launches a man
through the pie case in a glittering explosion of meringue --

-- then the room TILTS under her like a boat deck and she
catches herself on a post, blinking. Red sun in her blood.
Whiskey on top of it. Her body remembers being a weapon
and keeps forgetting mid-swing.

                         KARA
           ...That used to be easier.

Ruthye is a small storm at the edge of it -- she doesn't
brawl, she EDITS. A wrist here, a knee there. Where men are
big she is precise, and she is always, always moving toward
Krem.

A BRIGAND twice her size plants himself in her path, knife
low. Ruthye doesn't slow down. She goes UNDER the knife,
takes his knee out from the side with her whole body
weight, and steps over him without looking down. Eyes on
Krem. Always on Krem.

Krypto launches off the floor and hits a rifleman at chest
height, all teeth and joy, riding him into the grain sacks.
The best dog. The worst dog.

Behind the bar: GRASH, the folded ruffian, watches Kara
catch a swung stool one-handed and fold it around a
Brigand's skull. He picks up his own stool, thinks better
of everything, and starts dragging the wounded out of the
way instead. His bar fight too, he figures. Different job.

Kara moves again -- too fast, then too slow, a rhythm she
can't hold. She catches a Brigand's rifle barrel barehanded
and it bends around her fingers like warm taffy --

-- and then she blinks at it, swaying, and the moment
almost costs her: a second Brigand crashes a bottle across
her shoulders. She drops to one knee. Shakes it off. Gets
up angry, which at least is a fuel.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Okay. New rule. No more bottles.

A BRIGAND by the door swings a bulky hand-CASTING BOW up --
a squat crossbow of ugly design, its bolt tipped with
something that glistens violet-black. He draws a bead on
Kara's back.

RUTHYE sees it.

                         RUTHYE
           KARA --

Kara turns, too slow --

-- and KRYPTO comes out of the air and takes the bolt in
the shoulder. He drops with a sound no dog should make.

Time stops being a fight and becomes a single white point.

Kara crosses the room without deciding to. The great oak

field -- goes up in her hands and THROUGH THE WALL, taking
the door frame and two Brigands with it into the street.

The whole building shudders. Dust falls. The room goes
silent except for Krypto, whimpering.

Krem, at the threshold, looks at the hole where his exit
used to be a wall. Recalculates one more time.

                         KREM
           ...That's not normal for here.

Kara is already kneeling, both hands around Krypto, the
bolt still in him, violet-black glisten at the barb.

                         KARA
           Hey. Hey, hey. Look at me. Look at
           me, buddy.

Krypto's tail thumps once. Traitor tail.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Don't. Don't be brave, it's a scam,
           it's a total scam --

Ruthye stands over them, sword still out, breathing hard --
and then her head comes up. THE CRYSTAL.

A Brigand has it -- snatched from her cut satchel strap in
the chaos. He tosses it to Krem, who catches it one-handed,
gives Ruthye a small, almost apologetic nod --

-- and then the table, the hole, and Kara's eyes all say
the same thing, and Krem is a man who understands leverage.

                         KREM
           Pleasure doing business.

The Brigands withdraw through the hole, quick and
professional, dragging their wounded. Gone.

Ruthye takes two steps after them --

-- and stops, because Kara is still on the floor with the
dog, and because the dog's whimper has changed pitch.

                         KARA
           It's barbed. It's -- there's something
           on it, I can smell it --

Ruthye kneels. Looks at the bolt with her inventory eyes.

                         RUTHYE
           Brigand tox. Raiders tip their bolts
           for big game. There is a bonesetter
           two streets --

                         KARA
           My ship has a medbay. It's --

                          (honesty arrives)
           -- it's the closest thing I've got
           to a hospital. Help me carry him.

Ruthye hesitates one half-beat -- the door, the trail, six
days of walking -- then shoulders Krypto's hindquarters.

                         RUTHYE
           Then we go to your ship.

They carry him out into the red afternoon. Over her
shoulder, Ruthye looks once at the hole in the wall, the
direction the Brigands went.

Not grief. Not shock. Rage -- with a leash on it now.

The BARTENDER emerges from behind the bar, regards the
hole, the pie case, the wreckage. He flips the sign back
to OPEN. Business is business.

He produces Kara's tab -- the historical document -- and
adds one line at the bottom, unhurried, in pencil: HOLE.

Then he pulls the tarp off the top shelf and gets back to
his glass.

EXT. EVELIE PORT - STREETS - CONTINUOUS

The port watches them pass and does not help. That is how

what.

KRYPTO's breathing comes shallow. His tail taps once

                         KARA
           I know, buddy. I know. Almost there.

                         RUTHYE

                         RUTHYE

EXT. EVELIE PORT - DUSK

INT. KARA'S SHIP - COCKPIT - CONTINUOUS

EXT. SPACE - THE SHIP

INT. KARA'S SHIP - MEDBAY - LATER

INT. KARA'S SHIP - COCKPIT - CONTINUOUS

Silence. The kind with ringing in it. Kara's hands rest on

INT. MERIDIAN WAYSTATION - CONCOURSE - CONTINUOUS

anyway. He has won. He knows it. Everyone knows it.

A MONK in lost-luggage tags dozes on a bench under a sign:
INFORMATION IS ELSEWHERE. A tour group of identical twins
photographs a trash receptacle. A small robot sells maps
to a bigger robot, who already owns them.

actually answers questions.

           You have been here before.

                         KARA
           I've been everywhere before. It's
           kind of my thing.
              (off Ruthye's look)

at an angle arms do not fold.

Kara stands there, the bolt still in her hand, and is
visibly taken aback -- not at him. At herself. She either
didn't expect him to be that weak or didn't expect herself
to be that angry.

She closes her fist. Crushes the bolt. Wipes the violet
off her palm in the ash.

And then, barely audible -- not to Krem. To herself. The
sound of a woman talking herself down off a ledge:

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Okay. Okay. Not cold. Not like
           this.

                          (a breath)
           Be good.

RUTHYE, close enough to hear, hears it. Files it.

Kara takes a beat, fingers pressed to her forehead, and
walks toward Krem again -- slower now. Careful, with
herself.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Listen to me. I just want the
           antidote, to save --

A RED GLOW floods the frame from the side.

Kara looks up. Krem, on his back in the ash, has the grafted
arm-cannon leveled at her point-blank -- every cell in it
screaming past capacity, sparking, whining, slagged metal
dripping off it. A kamikaze kill shot. His last card,
overloaded to take them both out.

                         KREM
           Take THIS --

The screen goes RED. Then WHITE.

A CONCUSSIVE LANCE of energy tears out across the yard,
over the horizon, lighting the dead sky like a second,
uglier dawn. The blast flattens the wreckage for a hundred
meters. The duffel bursts -- gold and jewels scatter across
the ash in a glittering drift.

When the smoke starts to sag: no Supergirl. Anywhere.

Silence. Ash falls like snow that's given up.

RUTHYE walks out of the wreck line.

Done being protected. She crosses the yard -- slow,
unhurried, sword in hand -- through the smoke and the
scattered treasure, toward the shape in the middle of it.

KREM lies in a drift of his own loot. Burnt. What's left
of the arm-cannon smolders on the charred wreck of his
left arm. His right arm is wrong. He is ruined, smoking,
finished --

-- and around his neck, intact, on its chain: the vial
case.

Ruthye stops over him. Looks down. The same look from the
cold open -- the bar fight look -- level, cold, decided.
Nothing in her body says mercy. Nothing says hesitation.

Krem's eyes find her. And here is the terrible thing: the
evangelist is gone. The philosopher who killed her father
with deliberate spite is gone. What's left is a tired man
who begged for his life and watched his own creed collapse.

                         KREM
           ...Just do it.

Not a lesson. Not a taunt. Not "this is what the world is."
A man who doesn't care anymore whether he lives or dies,
because the version of himself that believed in something
-- even something monstrous -- died on his knees in front
of Lobo.

Ruthye looks at him. Really looks.

                         RUTHYE
           Why did you come back here? To this.
           There is nothing here.

Krem's eyes move over the dead horizon. The folded towers.
The ghost mural.

                         KREM
           ...It's where I keep my things.

The saddest sentence in the world. A whole philosophy,
collapsed into a storage problem.

                         RUTHYE
           My father sang. In the fields.
           Badly. The whole valley complained.
           In petitions.

She says it to no one. Not even to him, really. To the
world. A fact she is putting back where it belongs.

Krem closes his eyes. Waits.

And the thing she sees isn't the monster from her kitchen.
It's just a used-up person in the rubble. No creed to
defeat. No philosophy to reject. Nothing left to rage
against. Killing him wouldn't prove anything. It wouldn't
disprove anything.

It would just be killing.

She raises the sword.

HOLD. HOLD. Everything about her says she's going to
finish it --

She slashes.

Not his neck. THE CHAIN.

The links part. The vial case lifts off his chest on the
flat of her blade. The cure, not the kill.

She turns around.

KARA is standing behind her. Ten feet back. Whole, clean,
none the worse for anything -- Supergirl. How long has she
been there?

She saw the sword come up. Read the kid's body the way the
kid reads doors. Took one step -- and stopped, and opened
her fists.

She didn't intervene. She certainly could have.

The look on her face: relief, mostly. And pride, the size
of the sky. She is watching a thirteen-year-old step off
the path.

Ruthye walks to her. Holds out the vial case on the blade.
Kara takes it -- their hands meet around the case, and stay
there a breath. The hands motif, completed.

Then Kara pulls her in and hugs her. Both of them crying
now, without ceremony.

                         RUTHYE
           Let's go home.

EXT. BILKI - BRIGAND SHUTTLE - LATER

They walk to the shuttle together, across the ash and the
scattered gold. Ruthye first. Kara beside her.

THE CAMERA STAYS WITH RUTHYE.

On her face. On the ramp under her boots. On her hand on
the hatch frame. Behind her, somewhere in the smoke, there
is a ruined man in a drift of stolen treasure -- and the
frame never looks. Ruthye never looks back. So we never
look back.

We never see if Krem lives or dies.

The story was never about him.

INT. BRIGAND SHUTTLE - COCKPIT - LATER

The shuttle rises off the dead world, climbs for the gray
ceiling. Kara at the controls -- quiet, competent, a woman
who can do anything now, doing the simplest thing.

Ruthye in the co-seat. The vial case in her lap. Her hand
closed around it, knuckles pale, like it might try to get
away. Her father's sword across her knees.

Neither of them speaks for a long time. The gray thins.
Stars come out.

                         RUTHYE
           He was not worth it.

                         KARA
           No.

                         RUTHYE
           You were.

Kara looks over. The kid is crying again, quietly, without
theatrics, the way people cry when they're mostly done.

                         KARA
           ...Yeah. I keep trying to tell
           people that. Nobody listens.

The ghost of the bar-Kara in that. Ruthye almost laughs.
Almost. The shuttle climbs into the black.

EXT. SPACE - LATER

The little shuttle, a spark among sparks, making for the
bright truck-stop glow of Meridian Waystation.

INT. MERIDIAN WAYSTATION - DOCK CLINIC - LATER

The tube. The blue liquid. Krypto, floating, unchanged.

The Dock-Healer takes the vial case from Kara's hand. Reads
the batch marks with four eyes. Nods once.

                         DOCK-HEALER
           Brigand counter-agent. Matched.

No fanfare. No speech. A needle, an injection port, a
hiss of pale liquid leaving the vial.

HOLD on the dog.

One breath. Then another. Then a deeper one -- the first
deep breath in days, a ribcage remembering how. The
assist lines go green and quiet, one by one, like lights
coming home.

Krypto's eyes open. His head lifts. His ears, floating,
settle against his skull as the liquid drains.

Kara puts her forehead to the glass, and he puts his to
hers through it, and her hand is flat between them, and
nobody says anything for a long moment.

                         DOCK-HEALER (CONT'D)
           Stubborn dog.

                         KARA
           Told you.

The healer opens the tube. KRYPTO steps out on four wobbly
legs, sniffs himself, offended by the smell of medicine --
and then wags, and the wag takes over his whole back half,
and he climbs Kara like she's a hill he owns.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Hey -- okay -- okay, easy, patient
           zero, you're supposed to REST --

He licks her face. She lets him. Ruthye puts a hand out,
and he transfers half his joy to her without being asked.

                         DOCK-HEALER
           He'll sleep for two days and then
           he'll be insufferable again.

                         KARA
           He was insufferable before. It's
           his whole thing. It's why I keep
           him.

The dog sneezes. It's the best sound in the world.

In the doorway, Ruthye watches. Her hand rests on the
glass too, a foot from Kara's. The dog she poisoned,
getting better. The choice she didn't make, paying out.

                         RUTHYE
           I was going to kill him with this
           hand.

                         KARA
           Yeah. And now you're petting a dog
           with it. Same hand. Your move,
           every day.

The healer busies himself with invoices. Some things
aren't for functionaries.

INT. MERIDIAN WAYSTATION - MECHANIC'S BAY - LATER

The Mechanic bolts a salvaged Brigand drive seed -- pulled
from the shuttle's engine -- into Kara's starboard housing.
It glows. It holds.

                         MECHANIC
           Brigand horticulture. Ugly, hardy.
           Grows anywhere.

                          (she wipes her hands)
           Should void the warranty.

                         KARA
           There was a warranty?

                         MECHANIC
           No.

EXT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - EVELIE - GOLDEN HOUR

The red sun, low and warm over the mended fence. Smoke from
the chimney. Somebody rebuilt. Somebody kept going.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP: KRYPTO, leaping, mid-air, tongue out --

-- into KARA, who catches him laughing, alive, warm, the
reunion the whole film has been running toward --

-- and we PULL OUT: Ruthye beside her. And behind Ruthye,
LISSA and WILL, in the doorway of the house. And behind
them all, Kara's repaired ship, squat and homely in the
north field, a salvaged heart ticking in its chest.

Home. The place Ruthye almost threw away.

WILL sprints past everyone and tackles the dog. Krypto
takes him down into the grass and licks his face with the
enthusiasm of a creature who has no idea what he survived.

And LISSA gets to Ruthye and folds her up -- the embrace
she has been holding since the doorway. Ruthye goes limp
in it. Thirteen, finally. Home.

                         LISSA
           You came back.

                         RUTHYE
                          (into her shoulder)
           I promised I would.

LISSA approaches Kara. Two women who both know what the
road costs. Lissa looks at her a long moment -- at the
stranger who brought her daughter home.

                         LISSA
           She wrote the code on her arm before
           she left. So she wouldn't forget the
           words.

                          (beat)
           You made her forget the words.

                         KARA
           ...She forgot them herself. I just
           made sure somebody was there when
           she did.

Lissa nods. Takes Kara's hand in both of hers, once. That
is all, and it is enough.

Ruthye comes to stand with Kara at the fence line, watching
Will and the dog wrestle in the grass.

                         RUTHYE
           I wish I could go with you.
              (off Kara's look)
           But I have --

She nods back at the house. At Lissa. At Will. She doesn't
finish the sentence. She doesn't need to.

                         KARA
           I would trade the world for what you
           have.

                          (quiet; certain)
           This is where you belong.

The line that started as a drunk's plea in a bar. Go home.
Now it's the truest thing either of them has ever said to
each other -- and they both hear it this time.

A beat. The red light moves. And then Kara's face does
something new -- solemnity cracking into an impish,
lopsided grin.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           ...But you know. We never did get a
           chance to finish celebrating my
           birthday.

Ruthye looks at her.

And the cold, serious, code-citing girl who won a barfight
at thirteen CRACKS OPEN -- a giggle escapes her, helpless,
bright, the first one of her entire life on screen. The
load comes off her shoulders and we feel it go.

                         RUTHYE
           It is NOT your birthday.

                         KARA
           It was! Last week! I spent it
           poisoned and on a bus. Doesn't count.
           Everyone knows that.

                         RUTHYE
           That is not how birthdays --

                         KARA
           LISSA. Is there cake?

                         LISSA
           There can be cake.

                         WILL
           CAKE!

DISSOLVE TO:

INT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - KITCHEN - EVENING

Before the cake. The table half-set. Through the window:
Kara in the yard with the dog; laughter.

WILL boosts himself onto a chair next to Ruthye and asks
the question only six-year-olds get to ask:

                         WILL
           Did you kill him?

Lissa goes still at the counter.

Ruthye looks through the window. At Kara -- at the woman
in the grass with her brother and her dog.

                         RUTHYE
           The debt has been repaid.

Will waits for more. There isn't more. He accepts it the
way children accept weather, and goes back to folding
napkins. Badly. She lets him.

Lissa follows Ruthye's look -- to Kara, in the yard.
Understands without being told, the way mothers do.

                         LISSA
           Good.

She crosses. Takes her daughter's hand -- the sword hand,
the plate-setting hand, the smudged ghost of the code
still inked on the forearm. Holds it. Once.

                         LISSA (CONT'D)
           I sent you out there to come back.

She starts collecting plates, because grief lives in
houses and houses run on dishes.

                         LISSA (CONT'D)
           Now. Your friend claims it's her
           birthday.

                         RUTHYE
           It is not her birthday.

                         LISSA
           Then we'd better make it a good one.

INT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - KITCHEN - LATER

Golden-hour domesticity. The long table, loaded. A cake
with too many candles, because nobody here does anything
small. Will conducts the lighting with ceremony. Krypto
has frosting on his nose and dignity nowhere in evidence.

Lissa cuts the cake with Bren's knife -- cleaned, kept,
still in use, the way love outlasts. Ruthye sits between
her mother and a superhero, with a dog under the table
committing small crimes, laughing at something stupid Will
said, and for one whole scene nothing is dying anywhere.

                         RUTHYE
           Wait. How old are you?

                         KARA
           Twenty-one.

                         RUTHYE
           That is so many.

                         KARA
           It's ONE. It's one more than twenty.

                         RUTHYE
           That is still so many.

                         KARA
           Eat your cake.

                         WILL
           Can you lift the whole house?

                         KARA
           Easy.

                         WILL
           Prove it.

                         LISSA
           Will.

                         KARA
           No, no. Fair question.

Kara reaches over and lifts Will's CHAIR -- with Will in
it -- one-handed, level, not a wobble, and holds it there
while she takes a bite of cake with the other hand. Will
grips the seat, thrilled out of his mind.

                         WILL
           HIGHER.

                         KARA
           Don't push your luck, tiny man.

She sets him down, gentle as snowfall. He looks at her
like she hung the moon, which she probably could. Ruthye
watches her brother laugh at a magic trick, in their
kitchen, alive -- and has to look at her plate a moment.

Lissa's hand finds her shoulder. Stays there.

Later: Lissa raises her cup. The table quiets.

                         LISSA
           To Bren. Who would have liked you,
           Kara. And who would have pretended
           not to, for at least a week.

                         KARA
           To Bren.

They drink. Will drinks his juice with enormous ceremony.

                         WILL
           To Dad!

A beat where it could all fall apart. It doesn't. Ruthye
looks around the table -- her mother, her brother, a
superhero with cake on her sleeve, a dog asleep on her
feet -- and the thing on her face is not the code, and
it's not the mask. It's just a kid, home, at dinner.

EXT. RUTHYE HOMESTEAD - DUSK

Kara's ship, warm-lit, engines waking in the north field.
Ruthye at the ramp, satchel on her shoulder -- packed
light, coming back.

Kara leans out of the hatch and yells across the field to
Lissa, on the porch with Will on her hip:

                         KARA
           We'll be back tonight!

                          (beat)
           Tomorrow at the latest!

Lissa waves. Will waves with both arms. Krypto barks once,
the official sound of departure.

The ship lifts, banks over the tilled fields, and climbs.

INT./EXT. KARA'S SHIP - DUSK

Ruthye in the co-seat -- where she sat when everything was
falling apart -- except now her feet don't touch the floor
and she doesn't care, face pressed to the glass, laughing,
free, as the ship roars across the fiery red sky.

The same red sun that lit Kara's avoidance. Now just the
ordinary blaze of a living evening over Ruthye's home.

The palette forgives its own opening.

Krypto sits between the seats, ears lifted by the rush of
air through the vents, the exact dog he was on day one --
except now there are two of them to love, and he intends
to collect.

Kara reaches over and puts Ruthye's hand on the nav stick.

                         KARA
           Feel that? Little wobble? That's the
           port thruster being dramatic. You
           can correct for it. Go on.

Ruthye corrects. The ship answers. Her eyes go round.

                         RUTHYE
           I did that.

                         KARA
           You did that. Tomorrow I'll teach
           you the landing. The landing's the
           part that matters.

                         RUTHYE
           Where are we going?

                         KARA
           No idea.

                          (a grin)
           That's the good kind.

The ship banks into the light and is gone.

FADE OUT.

THE END

=== MID-CREDITS ===

INT. METROPOLIS APARTMENT - NIGHT

A window, forty stories up. METROPOLIS sprawls below --
gleaming, vertical, alive, the opposite of Argo in every
way except the one that matters: it's full of people.

KARA stands at the glass. No jacket. A sweater. A mug of
something that is not whiskey -- tea, maybe, going cold
while she forgets it.

The apartment is small and half-unpacked. Boxes labeled
in two alphabets. On the windowsill, in a chipped mug of
water: a single pressed flower, the kind they leave on
memorial walls. She's keeping it alive.

Her face in the reflection: sad, but present. Not numb.
Feeling it all at once, the way the whole film has been
asking her to.

                         KARA
           Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer
           this time.

A breath. The city hums.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           I mean, it's good to be...

She trails off. In the reflection, behind her: KRYPTO,
mid-crime, dismantling a sofa cushion with surgical joy,
foam everywhere. Her true home, being a terror.

A tired smile.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           ...home.

Krypto looks up, cushion in mouth, delighted with himself.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           Okay, that's -- we're getting you a
           yard.

A knock at the door. Two raps. Polite. Midwestern.

Kara looks at the door like it's speaking a language she's
rusty in. Krypto abandons the cushion mid-crime and beats
her to it -- sits, vibrating, foam still on his face, the
worst secret-keeper in the world.

She opens the door.

CLARK. Glasses, flannel, holding a TUPPERWARE CAKE CARRIER
like an offering. He takes in the boxes, the tea going
cold, the foam, her.

                         CLARK
           You were going to call me back.
           Eventually. Statistically.

                         KARA
           I was composing. It's a process.

                         CLARK
           Four messages.

                         KARA
           It's a careful process.

Krypto mauls him -- or tries to; Clark kneels into it,
laughing, taking the face-licking like a man who has
missed it. Kara watches the two of them. Something in
her chest unknots.

                         CLARK
                          (up again; hefts
           the carrier)
           Ma thawed the cake. She says the
           freezer burn is "character."

He crosses to the window. The city, awake below. And on
the sill, in its chipped mug: the pressed flower. Alive.

                         CLARK (CONT'D)
           You kept something.

                         KARA
           ...Somebody should get to keep
           something.

Clark doesn't push. Doesn't ask about the messages, the
red sun, any of it. He sets the cake down like a truce.

                         CLARK
           I'm glad you kept listening.

                         KARA
           I'm glad you kept calling.

She looks at him. At the dog. At the mess. At the life.

                         KARA (CONT'D)
           It's good to be home.

Krypto barks once, mouth full of cushion. Agreement,
probably.

HOLD on the three of them a beat longer than we need to.
On the city, awake below.

SMASH TO BLACK.
