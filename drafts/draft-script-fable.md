# SUPERGIRL

### *Woman of Tomorrow*

**Draft screenplay — adapted from the story bible (README.md)**

*A coming-of-age story about grief, in which Supergirl must win an argument about what it means to be good — for herself as much as for the child she's trying to save.*

FADE IN:

## COLD OPEN — "THIS IS WHAT THE WORLD IS"

### 1. EXT. EVELIE — THE KNOLL FARMSTEAD — RED DUSK

A farm world under a red sun. Everything the color of embers and old brick: the fields, the dust, the light itself — flat, sodium-lamp, embalming. Terraced crop rows climb a low hill toward a stone-and-timber farmhouse with a crooked chimney. Laundry on a line, dyed rust by the sky.

It is beautiful the way a photograph of a beautiful place is beautiful. The light will not let anything glow.

From inside the house: the clink of dishes. A man's voice, humming something old and off-key.

### 2. INT. KNOLL FARMHOUSE — KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS

A kitchen that has been loved for three generations. A long table, mid-meal. Bread torn, not sliced. A pie cooling on the sill.

THE FAMILY:

ANDY KNOLL (40s) — broad hands, a farmer's sunburn even under a sun this weak. He is the one humming.

MARYE KNOLL (40s) — quick-eyed, dry-witted, ladling stew.

TOMME (5) — small, gap-toothed, secretly lowering scraps to a six-legged pillbug-hound under the table.

And RUTHYE MARYE KNOLL (13). Braids pulled back too tight for a child. She eats the way she does everything: precisely, seriously, like it's a task she has decided to do well. She catches Tomme mid-scrap-drop without looking at him.

**RUTHYE**
The beast is fat, Tomme. You are making it fatter.

**TOMME**
He's not fat. He's *armored.*

**MARYE**
*(to Ruthye, sly)*
The Havversen boy asked after you at market. The tall one. He wanted to know if you'd be at the harvest dance.

**RUTHYE**
*(not looking up)*
Then he knows how to ask questions. That is a beginning. He may next learn arithmetic.

Andy laughs — a big, easy laugh — and points his spoon at his daughter.

**ANDY**
Where does she get it? Not from me. I was charming.

**MARYE**
You were *loud.*

**ANDY**
Loud is how charm sounds from a distance.

Marye swats him with the ladle-hand. Tomme giggles. Even Ruthye — almost, nearly — smiles. Warmth. The kind of life that exists because someone chose to build it, and kept choosing, every ordinary day.

Then: a low sound under the floorboards. A vibration in the water glasses.

The humming stops. Andy looks up.

Through the window: lights descending in the far field. A ship — ugly, welded, wrong for a farm — settling into the crop rows like a boot into a face.

**ANDY**
*(quiet)*
Marye.

Marye is already moving the children back from the window. Nobody screams. This is a house that knows the difference between weather and trouble, and knows you meet both with your face calm.

A long silence. Boots on the porch. More than one pair.

The door doesn't burst open. It simply *opens*, as if the man opening it had every right.

KREM steps in.

KREM OF THE YELLOW HILLS (50s) — lean, weathered, dressed in a raider's patchwork that was once good cloth. He does not look like a monster. He looks *tired* — like a man at the end of a long day of work he no longer questions. Behind him, four BRIGANDS file in: armed, unhurried, professional. They fan out through the house without being told. One heads for the cellar. One for the back rooms.

Krem looks at the family. At the table. At the pie on the sill.

He crosses the kitchen, takes the pie, sets it on the table, and sits down in Andy's chair at the head of the table. He cuts himself a wedge with the family's knife.

**KREM**
Sit. Please. Finish your meal.

Nobody sits. Krem eats a bite of pie. Nods slowly, honestly.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
That's good. You grew the fruit yourselves?

**ANDY**
*(even, careful)*
The east terrace. Sir.

**KREM**
"Sir."
*(a small, humorless smile)*
Weapons. Whatever you dry-store. Fuel cells, if you keep them, and you keep them — every farm keeps them. My men will find it all anyway; you'd spare us the mess by pointing.

**ANDY**
Take it. All of it. There's no need for —

**KREM**
There's no need for anything. And yet.

He gestures with the knife: *sit.* This time, slowly, Andy sits. Marye stands behind the children, hands on both their shoulders.

A BRIGAND comes back through with an armload — tools, a shotgun-looking implement, cured meat. Another lifts, from its pegs above the hearth, a HUNTING CROSSBOW. Old, beautiful, the stock worn smooth by one man's hands over decades.

Krem holds up a finger. The Brigand brings it to him.

Krem turns the crossbow over. Tests the draw. Approves.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
Yours?

**ANDY**
My father's.

**KREM**
Good bones.

He sets it across his knees like it's his now — because it is — and goes back to the pie. Eats. Takes his time. The room is forced to stand inside his patience.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
I had a farm. You wouldn't think it, to look at me. Terraces, like yours — steeper. The Yellow Hills. You'd have liked them.

*(beat)*

You've never heard of Bilki.

**ANDY**
No, sir.

**KREM**
No. Nobody has. That's the trick of it. A whole world can end, and the neighbors just... reroute the shipping.

He says it without heat. He's told this story so many times it's worn smooth as the crossbow stock.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
When it started for us — the sky going bad, the crops going gray — we sent for help. We had treaties. Trade partners. Worlds we'd fed in their bad years. And do you know what our neighbors did? They *honored their contracts.* With the people killing us. Freight on schedule. Very correct. Very lawful.

*(he looks at Ruthye for the first time — briefly, clinically)*

I was like you once. So haughty. Before my world was destroyed. Before I saw how things really are.

**ANDY**
*(gently, the de-escalation of a man who has calmed animals and children and drunk neighbors all his life)*
I'm sorry. For your world. Truly. But we're not those people. My folk trade grain to the valley, no further. We've done no part of any —

**KREM**
*(mild)*
Everyone's done no part. It's remarkable. A universe of the uninvolved.

The lie — and it is at least partly a lie, and everyone in the room can feel that it might be — sits in the room like a held breath. Krem registers it. Lets it pass. Keeps eating.

Marye moves — just to gather Tomme closer, just a mother's half-step —

Krem backhands her. Casual. Sudden. Not rage: *procedure.* She goes down against the dresser, lip split.

**TOMME**
*(screaming)*
Mama!

Andy is up and lunging before thought — and two Brigands have him, arms wrenched back, slammed chest-down onto his own table. Dishes jump. The room goes loud — Tomme wailing, Marye pulling herself up, Andy roaring, the Brigands leaning their weight —

Krem doesn't move. He watches it the way you'd watch rain.

And then — it cools.

Andy stops fighting. Breathing hard, pinned, but alive. Marye, on her knees, gathers Tomme into her, blood on her chin, eyes down: *survive it, survive it, we survive it.* The Brigands have the crates by the door. The ship is loaded. It is over. Every person in this room can feel that it is over — the transaction ugly but *complete.*

Krem stands. Wipes the knife. Picks up the crossbow. Walks toward the door. The off-ramp is right there. He is *on* it.

He passes Ruthye.

And Ruthye looks up at him.

Not crying. Not down. Thirteen years old, jaw set, and in her eyes — clear as posted signage — defiance. Pride. The unshaken conviction that the world is fair, that this is an aberration, that men like him *lose* in the end.

Krem stops.

He looks at her for a long moment. And something moves behind his tiredness — recognition. He has seen those eyes before. In a mirror, a long time ago, in the Yellow Hills.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
*(almost kind)*
Oh, child.

He turns around.

He draws a bolt from his belt quiver — black-tipped, wet-looking — and seats it in the crossbow. Andy's father's crossbow. He does it slowly, so she can watch every part of it.

**MARYE**
*(barely a whisper)*
No. No — please —

Krem levels the crossbow at Andy, pinned on the table. But his eyes never leave Ruthye. He holds her gaze the whole time — this is not for Andy. This was never for Andy.

**KREM**
*(to Ruthye, gently, like a favor)*
This is what the world is.

He fires.

WE STAY ON RUTHYE'S FACE. The sound of it. Marye's scream, somewhere far away. Tomme burying into his mother.

Ruthye doesn't look away from Krem. She never once looks away.

Krem studies her a moment longer — checking his work. Whatever he finds in her face, he takes it with him.

**KREM** *(cont'd)*
*(to his men)*
We're done.

They file out. He does not touch the children. He doesn't need to — he has already done the worst thing he came to do, and it wasn't the killing.

The door stands open. Engine-thrum. Light sweeping the window. Gone.

### 3. INT./EXT. KNOLL FARMHOUSE — MOMENTS LATER

Silence, except for Marye, keening over Andy, and the small awful sounds of a five-year-old who knows but hasn't seen.

FINAL IMAGE OF THE SEQUENCE:

Ruthye, on the floor by the wall, shielding Tomme against her body. His face is pressed into her shoulder, shaking. Her arms are around him — the life she should choose, held tight.

Her eyes are fixed on the open door. On the road.

Not grief. Not shock. *Rage* — the kind that has already decided something, before the mind catches up.

HOLD on her face. Longer than is comfortable. Longer than that.

## KARA'S MORNING — THE NIGHTMARE AND THE VOICEMAIL

### 4. INT. NIGHTMARE — ARGO — FRAGMENTS

Hard cuts, half a second each, sound smeared and underwater:

— A sky the wrong color, curdling.

— A hairline crack racing across a ceiling fresco of a golden sun.

— A woman's face — beautiful, exhausted — mouthing words we cannot hear.

— A girl's hand pressed flat against the inside of a pod's glass. A larger hand pressed against the outside. The glass between them.

— White. Everything white —

### 5. INT. KARA'S SHIP — SLEEPING BERTH — RED MORNING

KARA ZOR-EL (mid-20s) wakes gasping — upright, heart slamming, hand already moving.

Moving to the floor beside the bunk, where a BOTTLE stands among three empty ones. Her fingers close around it before her eyes are fully open. That's the tell. That's the whole biography: *before her eyes are open.*

She drinks. Breathes. Sits in it.

The berth around her: a good ship gone to seed. Laundry on instrument housings. A cape — red, folded, unworn — shoved on a high shelf like off-season bedding. Through the porthole: the red sun of Evelie, low and huge and dead-feeling, laying its embalming light across everything.

A jingle of tags. KRYPTO — a big white dog of no known breed and boundless, idiot-saint sincerity — pads over and puts his chin on the bunk. Looks at her with uncomplicated love.

**KARA**
*(rough)*
Don't start.

He starts anyway: tail. She scratches his ears despite herself. The one thing she hasn't numbed.

A soft chime from a DEVICE on the shelf. Its screen: a glyph, pulsing. UNHEARD MESSAGES: 11. The newest: KAL.

She looks at it a long moment. Plays it.

**CLARK (V.O.)**
*(filtered, warm, careful — a man trying not to spook a deer)*
Hey. It's me. Again. So — happy birthday. Officially, as of this morning — Ma did the conversion math to Earth years and then baked to her findings. There was cake. I ate your slice. I'm not sorry.

*(beat)*

Lois says hi. Lois says more than hi, actually, but I'm paraphrasing for length.

*(beat; the care shows through)*

Kara. You don't have to call back. Just — pick up once. So I know that wherever you are isn't somewhere I should be worried about.

*(beat)*

...Okay. Bye.

Silence. Kara's thumb hovers over the device. CALL BACK, it offers, in tidy Kryptonian glyphs.

She sets it face-down on the shelf.

She gets another drink.

The SHIP'S VOICE speaks up — pleasant, chipper, the customer-service register of a machine that has been ignored for months.

**SHIP (V.O.)**
Good morning! Reserve fuel: eleven percent. Drive coil integrity: poor. Recommend service before any burn exceeding —

**KARA**
*(muting it)*
Nobody's burning anything.

She stands, sways just slightly, steadies on the bulkhead. Finds yesterday's clothes. And then — from a hook by the hatch — THE JACKET.

A battered, oversized leather jacket. Too big, beat to hell, half armor and half blanket. She puts it on the way other people pull blankets over their heads.

### 6. EXT. EVELIE — PORT TOWN — MORNING

Kara and Krypto come down the ramp into a red-lit frontier port: dust streets, docking cradles, vendors selling engine parts and skewered somethings. Everything rendered in that flat dead red.

She walks it like a regular. A VENDOR nods; she nods back. She steps around a puddle she clearly knows is always there. Krypto trots at her heel, delighted by garbage.

She's not lost. That's the terrible part. She has a way home, she has people who love her, and she is *choosing* this — the bottle, and the dead light of a star that makes her feel nothing.

Avoidance isn't something that happened to her. It's a practice she maintains every morning.

She pushes through a door under a buzzing sign.

CUT TO:
## FIRST MEETING — "GO HOME"

### 7. INT. PORT BAR — EVELIE — DAY

A port bar at midday: half-lit, half-full, the clientele split between haulers, farmhands, and people between bad decisions. Behind the bar, the BARTENDER — a tall, patient fungoid whose head-frills droop like a wet newspaper — polishes a glass with a cloth that appears to be slightly alive.

Kara is at the bar. Established. Sedimentary. Krypto lies across her boots like a rug that chose her.

She finishes a glass. Taps it. The Bartender refills it, and — with the fluid sleight of long habit — cuts it with water from a hidden jug.

**KARA**
*(not looking up)*
You're watering these.

**BARTENDER**
Since midmorning.

**KARA**
*(beat)*
...Keep doing it.

The door opens. Red daylight. A small silhouette.

Ruthye walks in.

Weeks harder than the girl from the kitchen. Road dust. A shortsword at her hip, sized to her, plainly not a toy. A satchel worn crosswise and gripped like it matters. She stops inside the door and scans the room — not like a lost child. Like an auditor.

A RUFFIAN — big, scarred, entertained — steps into her path.

**RUFFIAN**
Lost, cub? This isn't a —

Her hand moves. It isn't flashy: she steps *through* his reach, turns his wrist against itself, and his knife is suddenly in her hand, and then suddenly standing upright in the bar-top wood, quivering, and she has already walked past him.

Not a trained fighter. Just cold, serious, and utterly without hesitation. The Ruffian looks at his own empty hand. The room recalculates.

Kara watches over the rim of her glass. Despite herself: intrigued. Or maybe — recognizing something.

Ruthye addresses the Bartender, formal as a summons.

**RUTHYE**
I seek the strongest arm in this port that may be hired. I can pay a little. I will owe the rest as a debt of honor, which among my people is collectible across generations.

**BARTENDER**
*(polishing)*
Strongest arm.

The Bartender's eyes — all five — slide, involuntarily, to Kara.

Kara raises her glass in a small toast: *don't.*

Ruthye crosses to her anyway. Stops. Looks her over: the jacket, the glasses lined up, the dog on her boots. Unimpressed does not begin to cover it.

**RUTHYE**
You?

**KARA**
Him, mostly.

Krypto's tail thumps.

**RUTHYE**
I was told a woman in this port put a drunkard through a wall in the autumn.

**KARA**
The wall was load-bearing. Nobody died. Sit down or don't.

Ruthye sits. Back straight, sword angled, satchel in her lap. She does not order anything.

**RUTHYE**
I am Ruthye Marye Knoll. My father was Andy Knoll, a farmer of the eastern terraces, three days' walk from this port. Eighteen days ago a man came to our farm with soldiers and took our stores and our weapons.

*(no tremor; recitation as armor)*

When he had everything and my father restrained and no cause remaining, he murdered him. In our kitchen. Before my mother and my brother and me.

Kara has gone still. The glass stays on the bar.

**RUTHYE** *(cont'd)*
By the law of my people, when blood is spilled unlawfully, the nearest kin of age must pursue the killer and lay payment upon him. My mother has my brother. I am eldest. The duty is mine. I have tracked the man and his brigands overland to this port. They are still on this world. Near.

**KARA**
*(flat)*
You're thirteen.

**RUTHYE**
The law does not ask my age. It asks my blood.

**KARA**
The law sounds like it's never met a thirteen-year-old.
*(beat)*
These men. You know who they are?

**RUTHYE**
Brigands. Raiders of many worlds. Their leader is called Krem, of the Yellow Hills. I know because he told us — he talks of himself generously.

*(leaning in slightly; the pitch)*

I do not seek a guardian. I seek *muscle.* You will be paid. All you need do is what I cannot: hold them. The rest of it is mine by right, and I will do it myself.

A long beat. Kara turns her glass slowly on the bar. Then, quietly, not looking at her:

**KARA**
Family?

**RUTHYE**
*(thrown, for the first time)*
...What?

**KARA**
You said mother. Brother. Alive?

**RUTHYE**
Alive. At the farm. Waiting on my duty to be done.

Something moves through Kara — through the drink, through the armor of the jacket. Not performed. A crack the alcohol lets through. When she speaks, her voice has changed register entirely.

**KARA**
Waiting.

*(beat)*

You have a mother who is alive and waiting for you, and a brother who is alive and waiting for you, and a house they're both alive *in* — and you walked three days *away* from it, toward a man who kills farmers over pie.

**RUTHYE**
The law —

**KARA**
*(low; the crack widening)*
You know what I'd give —

She stops herself. Looks down at Krypto. Long beat. When she looks up, her eyes are wet, and she hates it.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
Go home. While it's still there to go to.

Silence. Ruthye studies her — this drunk, leaking stranger — the way she'd study weather that might turn.

**RUTHYE**
No.

*(and then, because Kara has to understand this is not a child's fantasy)*

You believe I chase a ship already gone. I do not chase. I *hold.*

From her satchel, with two hands, she brings out a grungy, baseball-sized case of scuffed metal and glass. Inside, cradled in dirty padding: a DRIVE CRYSTAL — faceted, faintly pulsing with a slow inner light. Old tape on the case reads, in raider stencil, a serial code.

**RUTHYE** *(cont'd)*
The heart of their ship's drive. I crept their camp on the third night and cut it free of its housing. They have hunted a thief across half this world for two weeks and have not thought once to look down at a girl.

*(flat, factual)*

They aren't leaving without this.

Kara stares at the crystal. Then at the girl. Genuine, unwilling admiration fights with a rising, sobering horror.

**KARA**
You *robbed* them.

**RUTHYE**
I collected. Partially.

And she sets a second object on the bar: a TRACKER — hand-sized, farm-built, ugly, honest. A needle-gauge and a dull lens.

**RUTHYE** *(cont'd)*
Keyed to what they took from us. My father's bow among it. Faint, and it points, and that has been enough: it has walked me here. Now I will find their camp, and you — or someone less soaked — will hold them for me.

**KARA**
*(the horror gaining)*
Kid. Listen to me. Take the rock, drop it in a well, and go *home* —

Ruthye turns the tracker on.

The lens doesn't glow dull. It BLOOMS — bright, then brighter, the needle slamming its pin. Ruthye frowns, adjusts —

It is not saying *somewhere out there.*

It is saying *here.*

Kara and Ruthye look at the tracker. Then at each other. Krypto's head comes up off the floor. A low growl.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
*(dead sober)*
How close does it think —

The door SLAMS open.

Krem and five BRIGANDS fill the doorway, and every one of them is armed, and the thing in Krem's hands is a scanner of his own, its lens blooming just as bright.

Ruthye isn't the only one with tracking capabilities.

**KREM**
*(to the room, almost polite)*
The girl took something of mine.

### 8. THE BRAWL

It goes off like a dropped glass — immediate and ugly.

A hauler at the nearest table stands up on principle and takes a rifle-stock to the head. Chairs go over. The Bartender drops behind the bar with the practiced grace of a professional.

Kara moves — and here is the truth of her, told in three beats:

— A BURST: she's suddenly *across the room*, faster than a human, catching a thrown chair mid-air before it hits a bystander. The Brigand who threw it blinks —

— A TOO-HARD HIT: she palms a Brigand's chest, just to move him, and he goes through a table and into the wall and does not get up. She stares at her own hand, half a second of surprise —

— A STUMBLE: the room tilts. The drink and the months of red light call in their debt, and Supergirl — *Supergirl* — grabs a post like a sailor in weather.

She has residue, not command. She is not invincible. Just stubborn, trained, and still Kara.

Ruthye fights like she disarmed the ruffian: small, cold, effective. She puts a stool into a Brigand's knee and rolls under the bar-flap.

Krypto is everywhere — a white blur of loyal idiot-saint, latched onto a Brigand's forearm, shaking like it's laundry.

And Krem — Krem doesn't brawl. Krem stands in the doorway and assesses, the way he assessed the kitchen. He raises the crossbow. Andy Knoll's crossbow. A black-tipped bolt seated.

He levels it at the biggest threat in the room: the swaying woman by the post.

**RUTHYE**
*(seeing it)*
DOWN —

Kara turns — too slow, tilted, red-sun slow —

A white shape leaps.

The bolt takes KRYPTO mid-air, in the flank. He lands wrong, tumbles, tries to stand, and his legs argue about it.

Everything in Kara's world narrows to that.

**KARA**
*(a sound more than a word)*
No —

She FLIPS the nearest table one-handed — it goes THROUGH the wall in a burst of board and brick, daylight punching in through the hole, the whole bar frozen by it — and she is on her knees beside Krypto, hands hovering, afraid to touch.

Black threads spider out from the wound under his white fur.

Behind the bar, a Brigand hauls Ruthye up by the satchel strap and simply *takes* it — she fights, bites, is peeled off like a burr and dropped. He tosses the satchel to Krem.

Krem draws out the drive crystal case. Checks it against his scanner. Satisfied — and for one moment, he looks down at Ruthye on the floor.

Recognition. The kitchen. Those eyes.

**KREM**
*(quietly; almost gently; the worst thing he could say)*
You should have stayed home.

**BRIGAND SERGEANT**
Krem — the woman —

**KREM**
*(already turning; boredly)*
Is a drunk with a dog. We have what's ours.

They beat a hasty, orderly retreat out the door and are gone. They have what they came for.

The bar: wreckage, groans, a hole in the wall shaped like a table. Ruthye picks herself up, tracker still in her fist, still glowing.

Kara, on the floor, has Krypto's head in her lap. His tail moves — once — because she's there, and being there is his whole religion.

**KARA**
*(to the room; to no one; wrecked)*
Somebody — is there a doctor in this —

The Bartender surfaces and looks at the black threads. His frills droop lower.

**BARTENDER**
Not for that.

### 9. EXT. PORT TOWN — RUNNING — MOMENTS LATER

Kara carries Krypto through the red streets at a dead run — a big dog, a full sprint, drunk, sobbing, refusing to slow. Vendors scatter.

Ruthye runs behind her, sword slapping her hip, satchel gone, tracker in hand.

**RUTHYE**
*(running)*
Where do you run to?

**KARA**
My ship.

**RUTHYE**
Can your ship heal him?

**KARA**
*(gasping)*
No. But it can find out what's killing him, and it can *move.*

### 10. INT. KARA'S SHIP — MEDBAY ALCOVE — MINUTES LATER

A fold-out medbay meant for one humanoid, occupied by one dog. Kara's hands fly over controls sticky with disuse. Ruthye stands in the hatchway — her first time inside a starship, and she gives the wonder exactly one second before filing it away.

The scanner passes over Krypto. Unhappy sounds from the machine.

**SHIP (V.O.)**
Foreign compound detected. Class: toxin. Origin: unregistered. Profile matches raider-grade weapon coatings.

**KARA**
Cure it.

**SHIP (V.O.)**
No synthesis template available. The compound is engineered against therapy. Counteragent required.

**KARA**
Then *find* a counteragent —

**SHIP (V.O.)**
Counteragents to proprietary toxins are held by their makers.

On the medbay display: a stylized image of Krypto's body, black threads mapped through it. A bar beside it, draining. No numbers. Just the bar, and the soft, regular *tick* the medbay begins to make — a metronome the movie will never quite let us stop hearing.

**RUTHYE**
How long does the machine give him?

**KARA**
*(reading; hollow)*
It gives him "soon."

She stands there one second. Two. And we watch her choose to become someone who moves.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
*(to the ship, strapping Krypto's cradle down)*
Plot the nearest yellow star. Maximum burn.

**SHIP (V.O.)**
Good morning! Reserve fuel: eleven percent. Drive coil integrity: poor. Recommend —

**KARA**
I know what you said. Do it anyway.

**RUTHYE**
*(from the hatchway)*
Why a yellow star? He needs a cure, not a lamp —

**KARA**
*(working; not turning around)*
Because he and I aren't from here. Under a red sun we're — this.
*(gestures at herself: the sway, the jacket, all of it)*
Under a yellow one, we're something else. It heals us. It might hold the poison down. It's the only thing I can reach that might.

Ruthye absorbs this: the too-fast blur in the bar, the table through the wall. The pieces assemble behind her eyes. She doesn't gasp. She *re-categorizes.*

**RUTHYE**
Then why in all the worlds do you live *here?*

Kara stops working. Just for a breath. She doesn't turn around, and she doesn't answer.

**KARA**
Strap in or get off. I'm not landing again to bury him.

Ruthye straps in.

The ship rises off Evelie — rattling, protesting, a good ship gone to seed asked for everything at once — reaches the black, and BURNS.

CUT TO:
## THE ROAD, PART ONE — THE SHIP, THE REST STOP, AND THE BUS

### 11. INT. KARA'S SHIP — IN FLIGHT — CONTINUOUS PRESSURE

The drive HOWLS. Every surface vibrates. On the nav display: a yellow dot, achingly far, and a little ship-glyph crawling toward it. Beside it, the medbay bar, draining. *Tick. Tick.*

Kara is all motion — rerouting power barefoot-in-the-wiring style, slamming access panels, swearing in Kryptonian at a fuel readout.

**KARA**
Come on. Come on, you can do this, we've done stupider —

**SHIP (V.O.)**
Drive coil temperature exceeds —

**KARA**
Exceed it MORE.

Ruthye sits strapped in beside the medbay, one hand resting on the cradle's rim — not petting Krypto, just... present. She is stillness. She watches Kara fail forward through sheer stubbornness, and we watch her *read* Kara: this woman is not refusing revenge because she feels less.

She may feel too much.

**RUTHYE**
*(over the howl)*
The dog. What is he called?

**KARA**
Krypto.

**RUTHYE**
He took the bolt meant for you.

**KARA**
*(beat; not looking at her)*
Yeah. He does that. He'd do it twice.

*(quiet, to herself, the confession slipping out sideways)*

He's the last thing left that knew me before.

Strong heart. Four, in fact. No — one. One large heart. He is mostly heart.

It is Brigand work, the poison. I have seen it on haulers who crossed them. It is tipped on everything they fire.

Then there's a cure. Tell me there's a cure.

You would have to take it from a Brigand of rank.

And until then?

Kara puts her palm flat on the tank glass.

Inside, slow as continental drift, Krypto's paw floats over and rests against the glass opposite her hand. Glass between them.

(The audience has seen this shape before: a pod window, a hand on either side. Kara has too. It goes through her like current.)

*(quiet; a vow with no audience but the dog)*
I'm coming back with it.

Or I'm not coming back.

### 17. INT. OASIS STATION — INFORMATION DESK — LATER

The Attendant follows their eyeline. All its eyes close briefly, in what can only be described as a sigh of the soul.

**ATTENDANT**
You don't want to go there.

**KARA**
What is it?

**ATTENDANT**
Bilki. *Was.* Brigand homeworld, depending who you ask. No one lives there anymore.
*(tapping the advisory glyphs, one by one)*
Nuclear winter. Collapsed biosphere. Automated defense relics. And no atmosphere processing, which matters less than you'd think, given the atmosphere.

Ruthye points at the route line.

**RUTHYE**
Is it still on the drop-off route?

Every eye the Attendant has opens and looks at her. It hates — *hates* — that the answer is:

**ATTENDANT**
Technically.

**ATTENDANT** *(cont'd)*
*(stamping something with finality)*
Gate six. Departs on the quarter-chime. You want the *last* row.

**KARA**
Why the last row?

**ATTENDANT**
You'll know.

### 18. INT. OASIS STATION — CONCOURSE / BAZAAR — CONTINUOUS

The comedy engine, against the tick: errands, run at a hair below panic speed.

— A FOOD STALL sells skewers of something iridescent. Ruthye's eyes linger on them a half-second too long as they pass; she says nothing. Twenty steps later Kara hands her one without comment, already eating her own. Ruthye examines it with suspicion, eats it with dignity, and is visibly amazed by it, which she conceals from everyone but the camera.

— A VENDING ALCOVE of safety equipment, staffed by a creature that is mostly a smile. Kara buys a small ring-shaped device and tosses it to Ruthye.

**KARA**
Self-deploying bubble suit. Wear it on your belt.

**RUTHYE**
*(turning it over)*
What does it do?

**KARA**
Now you won't die in space.

**RUTHYE**
I had not planned to.

**KARA**
Nobody plans to.

### 19. INT. OASIS STATION — WASHROOM — CONTINUOUS

Quiet in here; the concourse noise muffled to a hum. A long basin, a long mirror. Kara and Ruthye side by side, cleaning up — bar-brawl grime, road dust, engine soot.

Beside her, Ruthye washes her hands — and in the mirror, we see it happen: the shoulders come down. The auditor's face goes away. For the first time in the film she looks smaller than she has all movie — less like a soldier, more like a thirteen-year-old girl who is very far from her mother.

She puts a hand on Ruthye's shoulder. Doesn't say anything. Doesn't make it a lesson.

A breath.

### 20. INT./EXT. OASIS STATION — GATE SIX — BOARDING

THE BUS: an intergalactic coach, a chrome slug the length of a city block, its rows of mismatched windows sized for six different passenger anatomies. It is instantly recognizable — it is a *bus*, down to its soul, down to the destination scroll misspelling BILKI as BILKY — and also totally alien.

A GATE AGENT stamps their tickets with three stamps, the third with visible reluctance.

Kara and Ruthye board. Ruthye pauses on the top step — her first time leaving her world already behind her, and now this: the concourse, the ships, the map of everywhere. She takes it in for one long second.

Then she files it away and finds their seats. Last row.

The bus pulls out of frame with a sound like a whale clearing its throat —

### 21. INT. OASIS STATION — CONCOURSE — BULLETIN BOARD — CONTINUOUS

A shadow falls over the board.

**ATTENDANT (O.S.)**

Bounty confirmations are at window nine. Also, if you're following the Brigand sheet — you missed the last bus. Next one's tomorrow.

**A VOICE (O.S.)**

No need.

**ATTENDANT**
*(stamping something, to no one)*
Window nine.

## THE ROAD, PART TWO — "I'VE DONE WHAT YOU'RE PLANNING"

### 22. INT. INTERGALACTIC BUS — TRANSIT — "NIGHT"

The cabin lights dimmed to a courteous violet. The PASSENGERS, in no particular order: a commuter who is visibly three smaller commuters in a coat and fooling no one; a snoring cloud belted into a child seat; a robed pilgrim eating gravel from a paper bag, reverently, one pebble at a time.

Last row: Ruthye, awake, upright, sword across her knees like a sleeping cat. Beside her, Kara, asleep against the window —

— and IN HER SLEEP: half-second flashes. The curdled sky. The fresco cracking. The hand on the pod glass —

Kara startles awake. Breathes. The bus hums. The pilgrim crunches.

Her hand goes into the jacket, automatic as blinking, and comes out with a FLASK. Unscrews it. The quiet, practiced reach — the same gesture from the morning, the one the audience already knows by heart.

**RUTHYE**
*(not looking over; matter-of-fact; a controlled demand)*
You should stop that.

Kara stops. Not quite mid-swig — just before. A beat of being thirteen years old caught by the actual thirteen-year-old. Embarrassed, she caps the flask.

Silence. The bus hums through the dark. And in that silence — maybe because it's dark, maybe because the kid didn't make it a speech — Kara talks.

**KARA**
You know how my world ended?

**RUTHYE**
I did not know it had.

**KARA**
Everyone knows the story of the *other* one. The baby in the rocket. One big bang and done — tragic, clean.
*(beat)*
Mine wasn't clean. Mine was slow.

*(the words come out in pieces; she's not performing this; she's excavating it)*

The sky went wrong first. Colors it had no business being. Everyone had a theory. Then the ground started shaking, and it didn't stop for days, and there stopped being theories.

People you knew died in ones and twos. Then all at once. There's a smell a city makes at the end. Nobody tells you that. And a sound.

*(long beat)*

I was old enough to remember all of it. That's the difference between him and me. He inherited a dead world. I *attended* mine.

Ruthye listens. She doesn't interrupt. She doesn't compare griefs. She listens the way Kara listened to the code speech in the bar — recognizing something, not trying to fix it.

**RUTHYE**
Your mother and father?

**KARA**
Made me leave. I didn't want to. They made me.

She looks at the flask in her hands.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
*(quiet; the confession)*
I've been down your road. After. There was a — it doesn't matter who. It was justified. Anyone would tell you it was justified.

*(beat; she turns and looks at Ruthye for this part)*

I've done what you're planning. Revenge won't make you feel better.

She doesn't explain further. Doesn't say who, or when. The confession just sits between them, and the road continues.

Unconsciously — and the camera catches it even if she doesn't — Kara's hand has taken the flask out *again.* She notices it there. Looks at it, long and hard.

Puts it away.

Ruthye doesn't respond. Nothing resolves. But something has shifted: she can't file Kara under *drunk with opinions* anymore. She's been there. She came back.

She's still coming back.

Ruthye turns to the window — and we realize, with her, what we haven't been looking at: it's *beautiful* out there. Nebulas stacked like weather. A myriad of colors, star-fields going down forever.

Beautiful, and cold, and empty, and utterly indifferent — all of it at once, depending on where you put your eyes.

Ruthye's reflection watches it pass among the stars.

## THE DROP — BILKI FROM ORBIT

### 23. INT./EXT. INTERGALACTIC BUS — LAST STOP — ORBITAL DROP POINT

The bus decelerates hard out of transit and everything changes.

Below: a world that reads dead from orbit. A total gray shroud of atmosphere. Lightning crawling under the cloud deck. Here and there through rifts: patches of old fire, still burning after all these years, embers in ash.

Above it floats the LAST STOP: not a landing pad. A skeletal platform bolted to nothing, over which rotates a huge, angry, red HOLOGRAPHIC WARNING in nine scripts. Three of the scripts we can't read. One is a skull. One is simply the word NO.

The DRIVER — a wall-eyed being chewing a toothpick analog — throws a lever. From the underside of the bus, with a clunk, drops THE DROP-SHELL: a clear bubble, half escape pod, half luggage chute. Its stenciled certification reads: RATED FOR TOXIC ATMOSPHERE AND PLAUSIBLE DENIABILITY.

The remaining passengers watch Kara and Ruthye walk the aisle with the frank pity of commuters watching someone exit at the wrong neighborhood. The pilgrim offers Ruthye a pebble. She accepts it with grave courtesy.

They climb down into the shell. It seals with a domestic little *click*, profoundly out of scale with the planet below.

**KARA**
*(through the shell, to the Driver; the thought arriving exactly too late)*
Wait. How do we get picked up?

The Driver shrugs — a full-body, multi-jointed, eloquent shrug — and presses the eject control.

### 24. EXT. ORBIT OVER BILKI — THE FALL — CONTINUOUS

The shell drops away from the bus.

And for one clean second, the system's YELLOW SUN — distant, small, real — catches on the shell's curved surface. A tiny, impossible glint of life above a dead planet.

Kara presses her hand flat against the inside of the clear shell. Palm to the light. Not because it will work — the glass filters the gift, and some part of her knows the rule even if we've never said it aloud — but because she needs it to. A reflexive, almost childish attempt to drink even a thread of the sun through a barrier.

Ruthye watches the hand. Then Kara's face. A meaningful look: *she notices.* Files it away with everything else.

The glint slides off the shell's curve —

— and something else crosses the black: a BLUE FLAME ARC, streaking planetward ahead of them, toward the same horizon they're falling for. Fast. Wrong. Gone into the cloud deck.

Kara and Ruthye look at each other: another mystery, moving faster than either of them can afford to think about.

Ruthye's tracker, in her lap, pulses. Points DOWN.

The gray swallows them.

### 25. EXT. BILKI — SURFACE — LATER

The shell sits crumpled in a drift of ash like a dropped ornament. The hatch stands open.

They walk.

BILKI: nuclear winter as landscape architecture. Gray on gray on gray — the palette DRAINED, every drop of color the rest stop gave us confiscated. A sky like wet felt, lightning inside it. Wind that carries ash and a thin chemical keening. The skeletons of terrace farms on distant hills — *terraces, like yours — steeper* — and the audience recognizes Krem's Yellow Hills by shape alone, in gray.

This is what Krem's worldview looks like when it wins. He didn't invent it. He *survived* it, and mistook the surviving for understanding.

Kara is in bad shape and getting worse: no sun to speak of, alcohol leaving her system the hard way, flashbacks stacked behind her eyes, the medbay tick in her head. Grim, sweating, jaw set.

At a rest halt, her hand pats the jacket pocket. Finds the flask. Holds it — and catches Ruthye watching. Not judging. *Naming.*

**RUTHYE**
You reach for it when you are afraid.

**KARA**
*(looks at her own hand; quiet)*
It's a bad hand. It does what it knows.

She puts the flask back unopened. Small. Not nothing.

Ahead: the tracker's pulse strengthens. And the land changes — the ash floor gridded with old foundations, then half-buried structures: a DEPOT. Bunkers, a transit spine, gantry cranes rusted mid-gesture. The infrastructure of a civilization that learned to move supplies after it stopped knowing how to make a home.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
*(low)*
Stay close. This is theirs.

**RUTHYE**
It is *close.* The tracker —

**KARA**
Close means we do it careful —

A sound. Small. Mechanical. Almost polite:

*click.*

### 26. EXT. BILKI — THE DEPOT APPROACH — THE GAUNTLET

An automated TURRET unfolds from a gantry like a waking insect — no ideology, no mercy, just a very old instruction — and OPENS FIRE.

Kara moves on instinct: she wraps Ruthye entirely in her arms and turns her back to the burst. Rounds stitch across her shoulders — she grunts, staggers, keeps her feet, hauls them both behind a fallen slab.

**RUTHYE**
*(furious, muffled against her)*
Release me —

**KARA**
No.

More systems waking across the depot: a second turret. Ground sensors. Somewhere, a PRESSURE MINE thumps a test of its own readiness. Krem's crew hasn't built a trap; they've simply come home, and home is a machine that kills visitors. That is what this place was always built to do.

What follows is not superheroics. It is a ruined, depleted *approximation* of them:

— Kara breaking cover a half-beat early to draw the turret's line off Ruthye, shrapnel raking her back, the JACKET tearing —

— A sprint-and-drag through collapsing cover, Kara taking a hit meant for no one, because at this power level even machines' mistakes hurt —

— The ground starting to DETONATE in walking thumps behind them, and Kara hauling Ruthye onto her back — a piggyback run, graceless, stumbling, one part super-speed residue to three parts pure mule stubbornness — through the geysers of ash —

— Until they clear the sensor grid and collapse behind a bunker wall, and the guns, satisfied, fold back to sleep.

Silence. Ash falling like snow.

The jacket hangs off Kara in ribbons now — less cool costume, more ragged hide. Something she is still, visibly, clinging to.

Ruthye gets off her back and rounds on her, shaking — not with fear.

**RUTHYE**
*(low, fierce)*
Stop. Protecting. Me.

**KARA**
*(bleeding, spent, flat)*
No.

**RUTHYE**
I did not cross the worlds to be *carried* —

**KARA**
You crossed them to kill a man. I know. Everyone you've told has heard the same girl saying the same sentence.
*(beat; too tired for anything but the truth)*
I'm not carrying you because you're weak. I'm carrying you because I know exactly how strong you are. That's the thing that scares me.

Ruthye has no answer for that. Or has one, and —

A sound rolls over the depot and takes the argument away:

A GIANT EXPLOSION, from beyond the transit spine. Then another. Then — worse than either — GUNFIRE and SCREAMS. Men screaming. Brigands screaming.

Something alive is tearing through the Brigands.

And under the screams, distorted by distance and wreckage, something that is unmistakably... *laughter.*

Kara and Ruthye look at each other.

Kara adjusts Ruthye on her back without a word and RUNS the last stretch — barely standing, heaving against the dead world, flames beginning to lick the skyline ahead, running on fumes toward the sound of the universe having fun.

## THE LOBO SCENE — "THAT'S MY CODE"

### 27. EXT. BILKI — BRIGAND DEPOT YARD — CONTINUOUS

Kara and Ruthye come over the rubble line into the depot yard, and stop.

It is already happening. It has been happening for a while.

LOBO.

The Main Man. Seven feet of corpse-white muscle, red eyes, chains, and a leather vest that has never once been washed. He is holding a Brigand by the ankles. He is using him on another Brigand. He is LAUGHING.

**LOBO**
*(delighted, working)*
Fellas — fellas, c'mon — somebody fight BACK, I'm payin' by the hour here!

The yard is a bounty hunter's workday: Brigand vehicles burning, Brigand bodies where physics left them. A dozen survivors shooting at Lobo with everything the depot holds. It bothers him the way weather bothers a mountain.

This is what the movie has been agonizing over, performed as *slapstick.* And for the first thirty seconds — it WORKS. It's funny. He's the most charismatic thing in the film and he knows it. The audience wants this. The audience has been promised this since the poster.

— A Brigand empties a cannon into Lobo's chest. Lobo looks down at the smoking holes, looks up, genuinely touched:

**LOBO** *(cont'd)*
See, THAT'S the spirit.

*(punches him over the horizon)*

— Two Brigands flee in a WHEELED HAULER TRUCK. Lobo steps in front of it, catches it, and LIFTS —

AND THERE IT IS: the Action Comics shot. The Main Man, truck overhead, backlit by a slow-mo explosion, silhouetted in glory against the flame-choked sky. It is glorious. It is iconic. It is sixty years of comic books in one frame.

Then he throws it down.

A grisly, slick CRUNCH. The truck flattens nose-first. Blood runs out of the front cabin, spreads, spatters the inside of the windshield.

There were, of course, *people* in there.

The laugh in the theater dies in the throat. The camera doesn't cut away fast, doesn't needle-drop, doesn't celebrate. It just... stays. The frame declining to love the death.

Lobo dusts his hands and moves on through the remnants. The carnage isn't slowing; it's just stopped being fun to watch, and he hasn't noticed, because it was never fun for him. It was never *anything* for him.

— A dismembered Brigand drags himself away by one arm, sobbing.

**DYING BRIGAND**
Please —

**LOBO**
*(genially, in passing, and stamps down like a man crushing an ant)*
You're welcome.

— The last few, cornered against the depot wall, throw down their guns. It does not help them. We stay wide. It's quick. We hear it more than see it, and we hear Lobo, conversational:

**LOBO** *(cont'd)*
Nothin' personal. Well — little personal. Bounty sheet had your faces on it and your faces annoyed me.

Kara and Ruthye, at the rubble line, watching. Kara barely upright against a broken pillar, gray-faced. Ruthye — Ruthye is watching with something horribly like *hunger*, and then, as the bodies accumulate, with something under the hunger starting to curdle.

Effortless. That's the temptation. That's also the horror. Both, the whole time.

And then the yard is quiet, and only one Brigand is left.

### 28. THE HANDOVER

KREM — scrambling backward on his hands through the ash, away from the advancing Lobo. All the tiredness in him replaced by animal terror.

Lobo picks him up by the collar, one-handed, holds him at eye level like something found under a sink. Consults the wanted sheet with his other hand. Squints between the poster and the face.

**LOBO**
Yeah. There's my payday.

**KREM**
*(and here is the philosopher of cruelty, the evangelist, the man who taught a child what the world is — sniveling)*
Please. Please — wait — I was — the world made — it wasn't — I have money, routes, I can — *please* —

Not a creed anymore. Not even sentences. The instant his own gospel is applied to *him*, it turns out he never believed it — he just needed everyone else to.

This is the turning point of Krem's whole life, and he doesn't even know it's being observed.

Lobo cocks his fist, bored. Another corpse on a planet made of them —

**RUTHYE (O.S.)**
YOU CAN'T KILL HIM!

Everything stops.

Lobo turns his head, fist still cocked. Slowly. The way continents turn.

**LOBO**
...'Scuse me?

Ruthye stalks into the yard — thirteen years old, sword out, marching at the Main Man across the killing field like a summons being served.

**RUTHYE** *(cont'd)*
That man's death belongs to me. My father was Andy Knoll of Evelie, whom he murdered before my eyes. By the law of my people, the payment of his blood falls to his nearest kin, and I am nearest, and I have crossed the worlds to collect. You may not take what is mine.

*(beat; iron)*

That is the code.

Lobo looks at her. Looks at Krem dangling. Looks back at her.

He sets Krem down — keeps a boot on him, absently, like a man saving his place in a book — and crouches to Ruthye's height. And the grin goes away. Not mockery. Genuinely, unnervingly serious. Something in him recognizes what she's carrying.

**LOBO**
Kid. If anyone looks at me funny, I kill 'em. That's my rule. Not a good rule. But I never broke it once.

*(beat)*

A code, though — having a code — that's worth more than all the corpses on this planet.

Beat. We see the landscape: the burning yard, the gray hills, the corpses. All the things worth less.

Then the grin comes back. Full wattage. Main Man energy.

**LOBO** *(cont'd)*
You got moxie. I like that. And I can tell — this guy's been eatin' you up. Look at ya.
*(stands; expansive; a game-show host)*
Today's your lucky day. I'm basically done here anyway. So I'm gonna give this to ya. All yours.

He hauls Krem up and THROWS him down in front of Ruthye — on his knees, swaying, blood in his teeth.

And Lobo steps back to a comfortable viewing distance, fishing his trademark CIGAR from his vest — lighting it off a burning piece of somebody's ship, settling in. Front row. Ready for a show.

**LOBO** *(cont'd)*
*(around the cigar)*
You'll feel great. Trust me.

And here is the scene the whole movie exists for:

Lobo — the most powerful being on the planet — has made killing EFFORTLESS. No fight. No danger. No practical hedge, no self-defense clause, no chaos to hide the choice inside. He has cleared the table completely. Krem is disarmed, beaten, kneeling, gift-wrapped, *blessed.*

Whatever happens next is one hundred percent a choice.

Krem looks up at Ruthye. Pleading, pathetic, tear-streaked, nothing left of the man from the kitchen.

**KREM**
*(barely audible)*
Please.

Ruthye looks down at him. And her face — her face does the thing we watched it do at the farmhouse, in reverse. It closes. It steels. It becomes the door.

**RUTHYE**
*(quiet; his own gospel handed back)*
This is what the world is.

She draws the sword back — full extension, both hands, a swing she has rehearsed ten thousand times in her mind on the road, snarling —

— and swings —

**AND KARA IS THERE.**

Depleted, bleeding, barely able to stand — the weakest version of Supergirl that will ever appear on a screen — stepping directly between the sword and the kneeling man.

The blade catches her across the body.

It CUTS. Deep. Under a yellow sun the sword would have shattered like a breadstick. Here, now, after everything she chose — it cuts.

Kara folds to her knees, then down into the ash, blood coming fast through the ruined jacket.

Silence.

Total, ringing silence.

Even the fires seem to hush.

**LOBO**
*(the connoisseur of violence, genuinely dumbfounded, almost reverent)*
Ooh. Hoo. That's — that's gotta hurt.

*(beat; and then, quieter, to himself, because he has killed gods and fought Superman and crossed the universe twice and he has NEVER seen this)*

...Didn't see that comin'.

He is actually speechless. Even the Main Man has no frame of reference for a person stepping *into* a blade — voluntarily — to save somebody from *themselves.*

Ruthye stands frozen, sword loose in her hands, staring at what she just did. The red on the blade. She didn't mean it. She wanted Krem. And the thing she wanted just cut the one person in the universe who gives a damn about her.

The sword falls in the ash.

**RUTHYE**
*(dropping to her knees, hands hovering over Kara the way Kara's hovered over Krypto)*
No — no no no —

And in the chaos, in the margin of everyone's attention — KREM CRAWLS. Pathetic, scrabbling, unnoticed. Nobody *lets* him go. They have bigger things to deal with — Kara bleeding into the ash, Lobo transfixed by the disgusting spectacle of feelings. Krem escapes through the gap that Ruthye's own swing tore open.

Her vengeance is why the quest continues. The camera makes sure we see it: the empty patch of ash where he was, and nobody watching it.

Ruthye pulls Kara's head and shoulders into her lap. Heart to heart.

**RUTHYE** *(cont'd)*
*(a whisper that hides a wail)*
Why?

**KARA**
*(fading; and she smiles, actually smiles, because the answer is so simple)*
I had to save you.

*(beat)*

That's my code.

Two codes. One sword. Ruthye's demanded a death; Kara's demanded she prevent it; and Kara's cost her blood, which is what codes cost when they're real.

Kara's eyes flutter. She's looking past Ruthye now, up — at the sky. At the faint, filtered smear of light where a sun should be, on the far side of the whole gray weight of nuclear winter.

She's fading. She knows it.

Ruthye follows her gaze up to that drowned light. Then — one glance, exactly one, at the direction Krem crawled. The road of death, still open. The woman dying in her lap.

It's not that hard.

It was never, once you're actually holding it, that hard.

She looks up at Lobo.

**RUTHYE**
Take us up to see the sun.

**LOBO**
*(snorting back to himself; favors are for suckers, feelings are worse)*
Listen, kid. Nobody tells the Main Man —

Ruthye STANDS.

Blood on her hands, tears cutting through the ash on her face, and none of it in her voice. The same iron that recited the law in the bar. The same girl who told the Main Man to wait — and he waited.

**RUTHYE**
Take us, or kill me now.

*(beat)*

The code demands it.

And she means it. Every atom of her means it. She will die on this spot before she watches Kara die, and she has just staked that claim in the only currency this creature has ever honored.

Lobo looks at her a long, long moment. A thirteen-year-old girl who one minute ago accidentally cut the person she loves, now issuing a death-or-life ultimatum to the most dangerous man alive. Not begging.

*Demanding.*

His face is unreadable. Somewhere behind it, something is being violated and something else is being satisfied, and they might be the same thing.

**LOBO**
*(finally; disgusted; deciding)*
...Fine. Get on.

Ruthye does not waste the half-second wondering if he means it. She slaps the RING on her belt.

*CHIME.* An offensively cheerful synthesized voice: **"DEPLOYING!"** — and the bubble suit snaps open around her, seamless and ridiculous. Practical. Lifesaving. A throwaway gift from a rest-stop vending alcove — *now you won't die in space* — become the thing that lets her save the giver.

Lobo rolls his eyes at the entire concept of the moment, lifts Kara, and slings her over his shoulder in one go, gentle in the way of someone who refuses to be caught being gentle.

THE BIKE: a chrome nightmare hog, all engine and spikes, hovering where he left it. He kicks it alive. BLUE FLAME.

Kara, slung, her cheek against his shoulder-plate, has a strange, calm look. She has made her peace with her decision — if this costs her everything, it was worth it, and there is a kind of rest in that she hasn't felt in years.

Her eyes close.

### 29. THE FLASHBACK — ARGO — THE PROMISE

Not fragments this time. The whole memory, at last — the one she has been drinking against for years.

INT. THE ZOR-EL HOME — ARGO CITY — THE LAST DAY

A beautiful room at the end of a world: the sky through the crystal wall is *wrong*, curdled, the color of a bruise. The floor trembles without stopping. Somewhere below, the city sounds like a tide going out and not coming back.

A LAUNCH POD stands open. Packed. Waiting.

And KARA — sixteen, incandescent with terror and fury — is FIGHTING her parents. Physically fighting: ZOR-EL has her wrists; she's wrenching away; ALURA is trying to hold her face and Kara won't be held.

**YOUNG KARA**
No — NO — you can't make me — I'll tear it apart, I'll tear the pod apart —

**ZOR-EL**
Kara —

**YOUNG KARA**
I'll stay! I don't care — do you hear me, I DON'T CARE — I'll stay and I'll die with you and that's MINE, that's mine to choose —

It's ugly. It's desperate. It is nothing like the noble farewells the legends will tell. It's a family clawing at each other at the end of everything.

**ALURA**
*(breaking; the argument collapsing out of her all at once)*
If not — then it'll all be for nothing.

And that's what does it. Not authority. Surrender.

They collapse into each other — all three, crying, the fight gone out of everyone, just a family holding on while the floor shakes. Zor-El's big hand on the back of Kara's head. Alura rocking her.

Then Alura takes Kara's face in both hands. Steadies her. Mother and daughter, inches apart. The whole universe narrowed to this.

**ALURA** *(cont'd)*
You must live.

A beat. The floor shakes. Kara's eyes spill over.

**ZOR-EL**
*(hoarse; his hand joining, cradling his daughter's face beside his wife's)*
Promise me you'll be good.

**YOUNG KARA**
*(barely a voice at all)*
I promise.

HOLD on the three of them, foreheads together —

That's the thesis. Not a philosophy Kara arrived at. A promise she made to dying people who loved her. Every time she steps between someone and a blade — she's keeping it. And every morning she reached for the bottle before her eyes were open — she was breaking it.

The white light of Argo's end rises to take the memory —

### 30. EXT. BILKI — RISING THROUGH THE DEAD SKY — CONTINUOUS

— and becomes the gray of nuclear winter, streaming past.

The bike climbs. Lobo drives like the concept of consequences is a rumor. Ruthye, in her bubble, holds on — watching Kara.

Kara is slumped against the bike's rear rack, the battered jacket torn open and wrapped around her like a death shawl. Her blood beads away behind them in weightless dark drops.

A tear leaves her closed eye and floats, whole and perfect, in the thinning air.

This world did a number on her: everywhere she goes, worlds are ending. But the sun — the sun is RIGHT THERE, just above the shroud, closer with every second.

The gray thins. Thins. Tatters —

### 31. EXT. ABOVE THE ATMOSPHERE — THE REBIRTH

— and the screen goes WHITE.

Not Argo's white. The other one: the brilliant, warm light of the SUN, blasting the lens — a mega flare that swallows the whole frame, movement four of the palette. Held. And held. And held.

Then, slowly, the flare relents — fades down out of the white — and resolves:

Lobo, banking the bike to a drifting stop in open space. Ruthye's bubble, glittering. And Kara —

Kara's limp form floats free of the rack, adrift in the light. Fetal, curled, small — nothing iconic about it, nothing posed. A hurt person in the sun — and the light is visibly soaking INTO her, the way water leaves a shoreline: absorbed.

For one terrible moment: nothing more.

Then — she WAKES from the flashback with a GASP. Reflexive, enormous, whole-body — the gasp of a swimmer surfacing from too deep. Her body insisting on living, the way her parents insisted she live.

The sun does what the sun does. Life insists on itself.

The wound across her body knits, closes, is gone. The gray leaves her skin. Her hair drifts gold in the gold.

And the JACKET — shredded, bloodied, finally too damaged to hold together — slips off her shoulders and drifts away behind her, turning slowly, a shed skin falling toward the dead planet below.

She doesn't dramatically cast it off. It simply cannot come with her into the person she is choosing to be.

Kara opens her eyes.

Full. Restored. *Reborn* — and feeling all of it: the power flooding back, and with the power, everything the numbness was holding off. Grief and love and the promise, all arriving at once, under full sun, with nowhere left to hide from any of it.

She turns in the light — and finds Ruthye.

Ruthye, in her ridiculous bubble, is crying. Completely, finally, thirteen-years-old crying, the mask nowhere in sight.

Kara gathers her in — bubble and all — wraps her arms around the whole absurd package. Ruthye presses her palm flat against the inside of the membrane. Kara matches it from the outside. Hands, aligned, either side of the barrier.

(Every barrier in this movie has filtered the light. This one doesn't matter anymore. That's the point of it.)

The lesson Supergirl needed to show Ruthye. And herself. Held, in the sun, for as long as the movie can stand it.

Nearby, straddling his idling bike: LOBO. Watching two people hugboxing in the sunlight.

He is *mortified.*

**LOBO**
*(finally; to the universe; from the bottom of his being)*
Well.

*(beat)*

That was fucked up.

He kicks the bike into gear. Blue flame. And over his shoulder, already accelerating away from the crying and the hugging and everything he has no organ to process:

**LOBO** *(cont'd)*
Fuckin' *codes*, man.

GONE — a blue line dwindling among the stars. The strongest man in the scene, fleeing the only thing that ever beat him.

The universe, personified, can do anything except feel. Which is exactly why it couldn't help them.

Kara and Ruthye, alone in the yellow light, holding on.

Below them, the gray planet. On it, somewhere, a crawling man with an antidote on a chain around his neck.

Kara looks down at Bilki. Then at Ruthye.

No more buses. No more shells. No more being carried.

**KARA**
*(quiet; and there is something new in her voice, or something very old, back at last)*
Hold on to me.

CUT TO:
## CLIMAX — "LET'S GO HOME"

### 32. EXT. BILKI — DESCENT — CONTINUOUS

And now — two hours in — the movie finally gives it to you:

SUPERGIRL.

Kara descends through the dead atmosphere under her own power, Ruthye held easily in her arms, a SONIC BOOM rolling off her heels and CLEARING THE CLOUDS in an expanding ring — a hole punched through nuclear winter, sunlight lancing down through it onto the gray world for the first time in a generation.

No strain. No stumble. No math. This is what she is when she isn't hiding from it.

She lands in the depot yard light as a thought, sets Ruthye down. The bubble suit retracts with its idiot chime ("STOWING!").

Around them: Lobo's work, gone quiet and cold. The camera doesn't tour it. We've seen enough.

Kara stands still one beat — and her eyes track across the wreckage in fast, impossible sweeps. Full-spectrum, penetrating. Searching. It's easy now. Everything is easy now. That's the problem.

**KARA**
There.

Across the yard: a row of BRIGAND SHUTTLES half-buried in depot wreckage. One of them is live — engines hot, loading bay open, running lights on. Someone's ride out.

And crossing toward it, dragging himself through the ash and debris field —

### 33. EXT. BILKI — THE SHUTTLE PAD — KREM

KREM. He's gotten it back together, a little — the animal terror banked down into something grayer. A huge DUFFEL of gold and jewels hangs off one shoulder: a life's predation, portable. He stops at the corpse of one of his men, works free a massive ENERGY WEAPON — an arm-mounted cannon — and grimaces as it attaches onto his left shoulder and arm with a grisly, biomechanical THUNK.

He turns. And sees them.

Sees HER — floating a hand's width off the ground, cape-less, sun-lit somehow even here, entirely unhurt. The drunk from the bar. The bleeding woman from the ash.

**KARA**
*(to Ruthye, not looking away from him)*
Stay back.

Ruthye opens her mouth to argue — and Kara gives her a look. Not words. Just a look, with everything the road has built inside it.

They're past words now. Ruthye stays.

Kara walks toward the pad. Krem watches her come. He can't hold her eyes — his slide to Ruthye, flinch, come back. His eyes are still red from his time on his knees.

And the dead world lets him speak without a villain speech. Quietly. Almost bored with his own pain.

**KREM**
Look around you.

*(beat)*

There was never anyone to save us.

Maybe he means his world. Maybe he means Lobo, an hour ago. Maybe there's no difference anymore. Not a monster twirling anything — just a bitter, tired man standing in the place that taught him his creed, having mistaken surviving it for understanding the universe.

Kara doesn't answer. Her eyes are on the small thing at his throat: a VIAL on a chain. The antidote. The whole movie, hanging on a man's neck.

Krem raises his right arm — the crossbow. Andy Knoll's crossbow, black bolt seated.

**THE NON-FIGHT.**

He fires —

— and TIME FALLS AWAY into Kara's speed:

The world goes silent and syrup-slow. The bolt hangs mid-air, crawling, its fletching turning lazily — it might as well be nailed to the sky. Dust motes stand still. We see Kara move through the frozen world at a walking pace that is, outside her time, a thunderclap — a SHOCKWAVE blooming off her in a sculpted, silent ring.

She plucks the poison bolt out of the air between two fingers, the way you'd take a flower.

Krem's eyes, in this stretched forever, have just barely begun to widen. He hasn't even had time to be afraid yet.

She closes the last distance and slaps the crossbow out of his hand — and even here, even inside her own stillness, we see it: rougher than she needed. The anger arriving through her wrist before her conscience can meet it.

TIME SNAPS BACK —

— CRACK — the shockwave hits like a wall of air, the crossbow is simply GONE, spinning somewhere over the depot, and Krem is HURLED meters back along the ground, tumbling, and we hear him HOWL. He comes to rest against the wreckage clutching his right arm, which is definitely not pointing the right way.

Kara stands where he was. Looking at her own hand.

Taken aback. Either she didn't expect him to be so weak — or she didn't expect herself to be so angry. The audience gets to feel both. THIS is the temptation the movie promised: not a fight she might lose, but a power that makes her anger *load-bearing.*

She exhales. Presses two fingers to her forehead. Talks herself down — and lets us watch her do it, and lets Ruthye, far behind her, watch her do it.

**KARA**
*(low; careful; each word placed)*
Listen. I just want the antidote, to save —

A RED GLOW floods the screen.

Kara looks up. Krem — one-armed, on his back, teeth bared — has torqued the arm-cannon around at her, and its core is SHRIEKING, overloaded far past every gauge, sun-hot: a kamikaze kill shot, his last sermon, delivered at point-blank.

**KREM**
*(a whisper; to her, to the sky, to nobody)*
This is what —

The screen goes RED.

Then instantly WHITE —

### 34. EXT. BILKI — WIDE — CONTINUOUS

— and from a great distance we see it: a COLOSSAL RED ARC of energy tearing across the whole sky, the concussive blast shooting past the horizon, clouds peeling back from its wake.

And Supergirl NOWHERE TO BE SEEN.

Silence. Smoke. Ash drifting down like snow.

HOLD on the empty, burning frame — one second, two, three — long enough for the audience's stomach to drop —

### 35. EXT. BILKI — THE SHUTTLE PAD — AFTERMATH

Panning slowly back down, through the smoke:

The pad is a crater-field. The duffel has burst somewhere in the blast — GOLD AND JEWELS scattered and half-buried across the wreckage, glittering in the gray, a life's predation returned to the dirt of the world that taught it.

And KREM: on his back in the debris. Alive. Bleeding, burnt; what's left of his left arm and the arm-cannon is charred, smoldering slag. The overload consumed itself and most of him with it.

But at his throat — unharmed, obscene in its smallness — the VIAL still hangs.

Footsteps in the ash.

RUTHYE walks into frame, toward him, through the smoke and the strewn gold. Menacing. Unhurried. Sword drawn and low at her side.

The same determination from the cold open, from the barfight — that closed, cold, decided face. Nothing in her body language telegraphs mercy. Nothing suggests hesitation. This girl has been building to this for two hours and every frame of her says she is going to finish it.

(And where is Kara? Not in the frame. The audience is given no rescue to wait for. Just the girl, the sword, and the man.)

She stops over him.

Krem looks up at her. And here at the end there is nothing left of the evangelist — not the regal cruelty from her kitchen, not the philosopher of the off-ramp. The man who begged on his knees and watched his own creed collapse the moment it was applied to him. Tired. Hollowed out. A husk in the rubble of the world that made him.

**KREM**
*(flat; not a taunt; not a lesson; nothing)*
Just do it.

He isn't offering her graduation. He isn't recruiting. He no longer cares whether he lives or dies — the version of him that believed in something, even something monstrous, died on its knees in front of Lobo.

Ruthye looks at him.

And the thing she sees isn't the monster from her kitchen. It's just a tired man. There is nothing left to rage against — no creed to defeat, no philosophy to reject. Just a used-up person in the ruins.

Killing him wouldn't prove anything. It wouldn't disprove anything.

It would just be killing.

Her eyes go to the vial at his throat. To his face. Back.

*(Somewhere behind this silence: a kitchen. A code. A woman who stepped into her sword. A bubble in the sunlight. A mother, a brother, a farmhouse with a hole in it shaped like her.)*

She raises the sword. Two hands. Full extension. The same swing.

The audience holds its breath —

SHE SLASHES —

— THE CHAIN.

Steel through links, a whipcrack of parting metal — and the VIAL leaps free, and she catches it up on the flat of her blade, lifts it — the antidote, raised on the sword that was forged in her mind for one purpose and has just, forever, been given another.

She looks at Krem one last moment. Whatever is in her face, it isn't forgiveness and it isn't hatred. It's something older than both:

Done.

She turns around.

**KARA IS STANDING BEHIND HER.**

Close. Quiet. Looking none the worse for wear — this is Supergirl. How long has she been there? Long enough. She didn't intervene.

Certainly she *could* have.

...But *would* she have?

The film keeps its own counsel. Kara's face, as Ruthye turns around with the antidote instead of blood on the blade: relief, mostly, breaking through like weather. But also — pride. She is watching a thirteen-year-old step off Krem's road.

And maybe off her own.

Protected. Restrained. Saved. And now — *trusted.* The last and hardest thing.

Ruthye holds out the vial. Kara takes it — their hands connect around it, one beat, the film's whole grammar of hands resolving into this one — and then Kara pulls her in and HUGS her, fierce, both of them tearful and neither performing otherwise.

**RUTHYE**
*(into her shoulder)*
Let's go home.

### 36. EXT. BILKI — THE SHUTTLE PAD — MINUTES LATER

The warmed-up BRIGAND SHUTTLE lifts off the pad, wheels over the gray, and BURNS up through the hole Kara's descent tore in the sky — out through the shaft of sunlight, gone.

We never see Krem again.

Not a body. Not a rescue. Not a fate. The camera never once cuts back to him — it stays with Ruthye at the shuttle window, watching the light, and does not look down.

His fate is irrelevant. The story was never about him.

## ENDING — THE BIRTHDAY

### 37. INT. OASIS STATION — DOCK CLINIC — LATER

The amber tube, drained and open. Krypto on the table, shaved patch at his flank, terrifyingly still.

The Dock-Healer loads the vial. No fanfare. No speech. Just the NEEDLE —

— and the breath that follows.

A big, ragged, whole-body dog breath. Then another. The black threads under the white fur, receding.

Krypto lifts his head.

Kara puts her forehead down against his. Stays there. Her shoulders shake once, and that's all the scene needs and all it takes.

Life, chosen and saved.

By the dock window behind them: KARA'S SHIP, hull patched, umbilicals retracting — and through its open drive cowling, nestled where the burnt fern used to be, a familiar faceted glow: a salvaged BRIGAND DRIVE CRYSTAL, installed and pulsing contentedly.

**MECHANIC**
*(wiping four hands on the apron; grudging)*
Took the graft, no thanks to the donor. Where'd you say you got it?

**KARA**
Estate sale.

### 38. EXT. EVELIE — THE KNOLL FARMSTEAD — GOLDEN EVENING

Movement five of the palette: WARM HOMESTEAD. The same farm from the cold open — the terraces, the crooked chimney, the laundry line — but the red light lies on it differently now: not embalming. Golden-hour domestic. A fire lit inside. A door repaired.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP: KRYPTO — alive, warm, ridiculous — LEAPS into Kara, a full-speed white cannonball of joy, tags ringing, tail a blur. The reunion the whole film has been running toward, shot so close it's practically inside the hug.

And as we PULL OUT of the close-up, the frame widens to reveal —

— it isn't just Kara. RUTHYE stands beside her. And behind Ruthye: TOMME, half-hiding, half-staring at the dog with naked want. And MARYE — thinner, scar at her lip, alive — a hand on her son's head.

Home. The place Ruthye almost threw away. Still here. Kara's repaired ship waits in the field behind them where a raider's ship once stood.

Krypto abandons Kara for Tomme, obviously. Screams of five-year-old laughter.

Ruthye and Kara, a little apart, in the gold.

**RUTHYE**
*(a smile the film has never seen from her; wistful, not triumphant)*
I wish I could go with you. But I have...

She doesn't finish. She nods back at them — the boy, the mother, the fire in the windows. She doesn't need to finish. It is the first sentence in the film she has ever left incomplete, and it's the truest thing she's said.

**KARA**
*(solemn; the whole film inside it)*
I would trade the world for what you have.

*(beat)*

This is where you belong.

The line that started as a drunk's plea in a bar — *go home* — has finished becoming true. They both hear it land. Neither one flinches from it.

A beat. The fire crackles inside. Krypto is being ridden like livestock and permitting it.

Then Kara — an impish smile breaking up through all that solemnity, like the person she was before, surfacing:

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
...But you know — we never did get a chance to finish celebrating my birthday.

Ruthye looks at her.

And RUTHYE'S FACE — the cold, serious, code-citing face that won a barfight at thirteen, that stared down the Main Man, that did not crack in a kitchen while the worst thing in the world happened in front of it —

— cracks open.

She GIGGLES.

For the first time in the entire film. And the weight comes off her shoulders so visibly, so physically, that the audience feels it leave their own.

### 39. EXT. EVELIE — SKY — MOMENTS LATER

KARA'S SHIP tears up off the field into the evening — and from the open hatch as it banks over the farmhouse, Kara yells back down:

**KARA**
We'll be back tonight! Tomorrow at the LATEST!

**MARYE**
*(calling up; already resigned; already fond)*
Which?!

**KARA (O.S.)**
YES!

FINAL IMAGE:

The ship crossing Evelie's enormous, fiery RED SKY — the same red sun that once lit a woman's hiding, now just the ordinary blaze of a living evening over somebody's home — and in the cockpit window, RUTHYE, laughing, actually laughing, free, Krypto's ears streaming in the blower vents, the adventure that was always supposed to be fun finally getting to be.

*The film opened on this girl shielding her brother, eyes full of rage, staring at a road.*

*It closes on the same girl, giggling, flying — having chosen the life.*

## MID-CREDITS — HOME

### 40. INT. APARTMENT — METROPOLIS — NIGHT

A window, high up. Beyond it: METROPOLIS — the opposite of Argo, the opposite of Bilki. Gleaming, layered, loud with light. Alive.

KARA stands at the glass, looking out. New jacket — hers, fitted, chosen. A mug of something steaming in her hand that is definitely, visibly, tea.

Her face in the window's reflection: sad. But *present.* Not numb. Not drinking. Feeling all of it at once — the city, the year, the promise — the way the whole film has been asking her to.

Behind her in the reflection: KRYPTO, being an absolute terror — dragging a couch cushion to a crime scene of his own devising, supremely unrepentant. Her true home, shedding on the rug.

**KARA**
*(to the glass; to the city; to herself)*
Maybe I'll stick around a bit longer this time.

A breath. She watches the city not end.

**KARA** *(cont'd)*
I mean — it's good to be...

*(the word costs exactly as much as it should)*

...home.

She's not sure yet. She might not be sure for a long time. But she's here, and she's choosing to stay.

She looks down at the device in her other hand. On the screen, a name: KAL. Her thumb hovers — the same hover from the morning on Evelie, the same distance.

She presses CALL.

One ring —

**CLARK (V.O.)**
*(picking up on the first ring; like he's been holding the phone for a year)*
Kara?

*The wonder and hope of All-Star Superman; the seasonal melancholy of For All Seasons; the cosmic loneliness of Woman of Tomorrow. In a world that can be cruel and unfair, goodness is a choice you must actively make — and this is the story of two people, at different mile-markers on the same road, making it.*
